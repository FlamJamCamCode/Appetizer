## Appendix: Comprehensive Altruism Scenarios and State/Private Expenditure Study

### A. Purpose and Scope

This appendix is a standalone technical artifact that:

- **Formalizes multiple altruism scenarios** (individual and state-level).
- **Quantifies them** with explicit formulas and clearly flagged numerical assumptions.
- **Defines data needs and structures** for future empirical refinement.
- **Supports visualizations** (PNGs/SVGs) generated by scripts in this same folder.

All starred quantities (`*`) are **thought-experiment parameters or ranges**, not precise empirical estimates. They should be replaced or tightened using official datasets (World Bank, OECD, OECD-DAC, CAF World Giving Index, etc.) for more precise work.

The goal is to act as a “maximal scaffolding” for a very large study. Many sections are structured so you can later plug in more detailed, country-by-country values.

---

### B. Core Concepts, Notation, and Symbols

Let:

- **Countries and Groups**
  - \( c \in C \): country.
  - \( W \subset C \): set of “Western” countries (OECD high-income democracies + close analogues).
  - \( N(c) \): total population of country \( c \).
  - \( N^\text{adult}(c) \): adult population (e.g., 16+).
  - \( N^\text{male}(c) \): male population.
  - \( N^\text{male,16\text{–}60}(c) \): working-age male population (16–60).

- **Income and Wealth**
  - \( y_i \): annual income of individual \( i \).
  - \( w_i \): net wealth of individual \( i \).
  - \( \bar{y}(G) \): average annual income of group \( G \).
  - \( \bar{w}(G) \): average net wealth of group \( G \).

- **Altruism / Giving Behavior**
  - \( g_i \): fraction of income given by individual \( i \), \( g_i \in [0,1] \).
  - \( d_i = g_i y_i \): annual donations/charity from individual \( i \).
  - Percentile of altruism: percentile rank of \( g_i \) in the distribution of \( g \) (not income).
  - Top \( p \%\) altruists: individuals with \( g_i \) above the \( 1-p \) quantile.

- **State-Level Expenditure**
  - \( \text{GDP}(c) \): gross domestic product of country \( c \).
  - \( \text{GNI}(c) \): gross national income of country \( c \).
  - \( S^\text{dom}(c) \): domestic social expenditure (welfare, health, pensions, etc.) in \( c \).
  - \( S^\text{dom}_\%\!(c) = S^\text{dom}(c) / \text{GDP}(c) \): social expenditure as % of GDP.
  - \( A^\text{ODA}(c) \): official development assistance / foreign aid from \( c \).
  - \( A^\text{ODA}_\%\!(c) = A^\text{ODA}(c) / \text{GNI}(c) \): ODA as % of GNI.

Starred numbers (e.g. \(\bar{y}^\*(W) = 55{,}000\)) are **assumed inputs** used for scenario calculations and should be treated as adjustable parameters.

### C. Scenario 1 – “Angels of Man as Mass Man”

#### C.1 Concept

We imagine that the **distribution of altruistic giving** (in % of income) is compressed upward:

- Today’s **top 0.1%** altruists (by \( g_i \)) become the **new top 1%**.
- Today’s **top 1%** become the **new top 10%**.
- Today’s **top 10%** become the **new floor** (even the “least altruistic” in the new world).

Let:

- Current top 0.1%: average giving rate \( g^\text{0.1\%}_\* \in [0.2, 0.4] \). We use **0.30\*** as a working value.
- Current top 1%: average giving rate \( g^\text{1\%}_\* \in [0.10, 0.20] \). We use **0.15\***.
- Current top 10%: average giving rate \( g^\text{10\%}_\* \in [0.03, 0.10] \). We use **0.05\***.

Then in the scenario:

- New top 1%: \( g^\text{new 1\%} = g^\text{0.1\%}_\* \approx 0.30^\* \).
- New top 10%: \( g^\text{new 10\%} = g^\text{1\%}_\* \approx 0.15^\* \).
- Bottom 90%: \( g^\text{new 90\%} \ge g^\text{10\%}_\* \approx 0.05^\* \).

#### C.2 Aggregate Formula

Take a region \( R \) (e.g. Western countries):

- Adult population: \( N^\text{adult}(R) \).
- Average income per adult: \( \bar{y}^\*(R) \).

Then:

- Adults in top 1%: \( 0.01 N^\text{adult}(R) \).
- Adults in top 10% (including top 1%): \( 0.10 N^\text{adult}(R) \).
- Adults in bottom 90%: \( 0.90 N^\text{adult}(R) \).

Total donations in the Angels-of-Man scenario:

\[
D^\text{Angels}(R) \approx 0.01 N^\text{adult}(R)\, g^\text{0.1\%}_\* \bar{y}^\*(R)
 + 0.09 N^\text{adult}(R)\, g^\text{1\%}_\* \bar{y}^\*(R)
 + 0.90 N^\text{adult}(R)\, g^\text{10\%}_\* \bar{y}^\*(R).
\]

#### C.3 Example Calculation for Western Countries (Illustrative)

Assume (all starred):

- \( N^\text{adult}(W) = 400\,\text{million}^\* \).
- \( \bar{y}^\*(W) = 55{,}000\,\text{USD}^\* \).
- \( g^\text{0.1\%}_\* = 0.30^\* \), \( g^\text{1\%}_\* = 0.15^\* \), \( g^\text{10\%}_\* = 0.05^\* \).

Then:

- Top 1% (4 million adults): per person donation \(\approx 0.30 \times 55{,}000 = 16{,}500^\*\) USD.
- Next 9% (36 million): per person donation \(\approx 0.15 \times 55{,}000 = 8{,}250^\*\) USD.
- Bottom 90% (360 million): per person donation \(\approx 0.05 \times 55{,}000 = 2{,}750^\*\) USD.

Aggregate:

- Top 1%: \(4\,\text{M} \times 16{,}500 \approx 66\,\text{bn}^\*\) USD.
- Next 9%: \(36\,\text{M} \times 8{,}250 \approx 297\,\text{bn}^\*\) USD.
- Bottom 90%: \(360\,\text{M} \times 2{,}750 \approx 990\,\text{bn}^\*\) USD.

So:

\[
D^\text{Angels}(W) \approx 1.35\,\text{trillion USD per year}^\*.
\]

This is of the same order of magnitude as multiple times current global official development assistance (ODA) levels.

### D. Scenario 2 – “Top Altruist State/Tax as All”

#### D.1 Concept

We consider **state-level altruism**, via:

- Domestic social expenditure \( S^\text{dom}(c) \).
- External official development assistance \( A^\text{ODA}(c) \).

The scenario:

> All countries spend on social protection and foreign aid as if they were among today’s most altruistic states (relative to GDP/GNI).

Empirically, many top social spenders (e.g. France, Scandinavia) spend **25–30% of GDP** on social expenditure, and top ODA donors meet or exceed **0.7% of GNI** in foreign aid.

#### D.2 Formalization

Let:

- \( s^\text{top}_\* \): benchmark social spending ratio (e.g. 0.28\* = 28% of GDP).
- \( a^\text{top}_\* \): benchmark ODA ratio (e.g. 0.009\* = 0.9% of GNI).

Scenario allocations for each country \( c \):

\[
S^\text{dom}_\text{scenario}(c) = s^\text{top}_\* \cdot \text{GDP}(c),
\]
\[
A^\text{ODA}_\text{scenario}(c) = a^\text{top}_\* \cdot \text{GNI}(c).
\]

Globally:

\[
S^\text{dom}_\text{scenario}(C) = \sum_{c \in C} S^\text{dom}_\text{scenario}(c),
\quad
A^\text{ODA}_\text{scenario}(C) = \sum_{c \in C} A^\text{ODA}_\text{scenario}(c).
\]

#### D.3 Example Global Calculation (Illustrative)

Assume:

- World GDP \(\approx 110\,\text{trillion USD}^\*\).
- World GNI \(\approx\) world GDP for rough purposes.
- \( s^\text{top}_\* = 0.28^\* \).
- \( a^\text{top}_\* = 0.009^\* \).

Then:

- Global social spending in scenario:
  - \( S^\text{dom}_\text{scenario}(C) \approx 0.28 \times 110 \approx 30.8\,\text{trillion USD/year}^\* \).
- Global ODA in scenario:
  - \( A^\text{ODA}_\text{scenario}(C) \approx 0.009 \times 110 \approx 0.99\,\text{trillion USD/year}^\* \).

Compare with:

- Actual global ODA: ~0.2–0.22 trillion USD/year (OECD-DAC; empirical range, un-starred as a range).

### E. Scenario 3 – 10% of Working Men Giving 20–80% of Income or Wealth

#### E.1 Concept

We focus on **working-age men (16–60)** in Western countries and consider:

- A fraction \( p^\text{male} \) of them (e.g. 10%) decides to give a large share \( g^\text{income}_\* \in \{0.20, 0.50, 0.80\} \) of annual income, or a share \( \lambda^\text{wealth}_\* \in \{0.20, 0.50, 0.80\} \) of net wealth over time.

This is explicitly a **highly idealized thought experiment**.

#### E.2 Formalization

Let:

- \( N^\text{male,16\text{–}60}(W) \): working-age male population in West.
- Labor force participation: \( \pi^\text{LF}_\* \) (e.g. 0.75\*).
- Earners: \( N^\text{earn,male}(W) = \pi^\text{LF}_\* N^\text{male,16\text{–}60}(W) \).
- Participant share: \( p^\text{male}_\* \) (e.g. 0.10\*).
- Participants: \( N^\text{part} = p^\text{male}_\* N^\text{earn,male}(W) \).
- Average income: \( \bar{y}^\text{male}_\*(W) \).
- Average wealth: \( \bar{w}^\text{male}_\*(W) \).

Income-based giving (per year):

\[
D^\text{male-income}_\text{scenario}(W) =
N^\text{part} \cdot g^\text{income}_\* \cdot \bar{y}^\text{male}_\*(W).
\]

Wealth-based giving (one-off, or spread over horizon \( T \)):

\[
D^\text{male-wealth}_\text{scenario}(W) =
N^\text{part} \cdot \lambda^\text{wealth}_\* \cdot \bar{w}^\text{male}_\*(W).
\]

If spread evenly over \( T \) years, annualized:

\[
\text{Annualized} = \frac{D^\text{male-wealth}_\text{scenario}(W)}{T}.
\]

#### E.3 Example Calculation for Western Working Men (Illustrative)

Assume:

- \( N^\text{male,16\text{–}60}(W) = 200\,\text{million}^\* \).
- \( \pi^\text{LF}_\* = 0.75^\* \) → earners ~150 million.
- \( p^\text{male}_\* = 0.10^\* \) → \( N^\text{part} = 15\,\text{million}^\* \).
- \( \bar{y}^\text{male}_\*(W) = 65{,}000\,\text{USD}^\* \).

**Income-based giving**:

- For \( g^\text{income}_\* = 0.20 \):
  - Per person: 13,000 USD/year.
  - Aggregate: \( \approx 195\,\text{bn USD/year}^\* \).
- For \( g^\text{income}_\* = 0.50 \):
  - Per person: 32,500 USD/year.
  - Aggregate: \( \approx 487.5\,\text{bn USD/year}^\* \).
- For \( g^\text{income}_\* = 0.80 \):
  - Per person: 52,000 USD/year.
  - Aggregate: \( \approx 780\,\text{bn USD/year}^\* \).

**Wealth-based giving** (once-off, illustrative):

Assume \( \bar{w}^\text{male}_\*(W) = 700{,}000\,\text{USD}^\* \).

- 20% of wealth: 140,000 USD per person → ~2.1 trillion USD total.
- 50% of wealth: 350,000 USD per person → ~5.25 trillion USD total.
- 80% of wealth: 560,000 USD per person → ~8.4 trillion USD total.

Annualizing over \( T = 10 \) years:

- 20% wealth scenario: ~210 bn USD/year.
- 50% wealth scenario: ~525 bn USD/year.
- 80% wealth scenario: ~840 bn USD/year.

### F. Scenario 4 – 90th–100th Percentile Giving Behavior Extrapolated to 100%

#### F.1 Concept

We consider the **average giving rate of the current 90th–100th percentile altruists** (top 10% in \( g_i \)) and imagine:

> Everyone with income in Western countries gives at this rate.

Let:

- Average giving rate in top 10%: \( \bar{g}^\text{top10}_\* \approx 0.10^\* \) (10% of income).
- Number of earners in West: \( N^\text{earn}(W) \).
- Average income among earners: \( \bar{y}^\*(W) \).

Then scenario total:

\[
D^\text{top10-extrap}(W) = N^\text{earn}(W) \cdot \bar{g}^\text{top10}_\* \cdot \bar{y}^\*(W).
\]

#### F.2 Example Calculation (Illustrative)

Assume:

- \( N^\text{earn}(W) = 500\,\text{million}^\* \) (all genders).
- \( \bar{y}^\*(W) = 60{,}000\,\text{USD}^\* \).
- \( \bar{g}^\text{top10}_\* = 0.10^\* \).

Then:

- Per person: 6,000 USD/year.
- Total: \( 500\,\text{M} \times 6{,}000 = 3.0\,\text{trillion USD/year}^\* \).

This can be compared to global ODA (~0.2–0.22 trillion USD/year) or to major components of global health and social spending.

### G. Comparative Overview and Economic Potential

#### G.1 Scenario Magnitudes (Illustrative Order-of-Magnitude)

- **Current world ODA**: ~200–220 bn USD/year.
- **Angels of Man as Mass Man (Western private giving)**: ~1.35 trillion USD/year*.
- **10% of Western men giving 20% of income**: ~195 bn USD/year*.
- **10% of Western men giving 50–80% of income**: ~488–780 bn USD/year*.
- **Top 10% altruist behavior extrapolated to all earners**: ~3.0 trillion USD/year*.
- **Top-state social spending scenario (global)**: ~30+ trillion USD/year* in domestic social spending.
- **Top-state ODA scenario (global)**: ~1.0 trillion USD/year*.

These are rough, stylized numbers whose purpose is to frame the *scale* of different altruistic futures rather than to provide point estimates.

#### G.2 Adjacent Analyses

Possible extensions include:

- Modeling **behavioral response** (labor supply, savings) to high giving rates.
- Estimating **poverty-gap closure** or **health impact** from each scenario given assumed cost-effectiveness.
- Comparing **private vs. public** channels of altruism (tax vs. voluntary giving).
- Exploring **distributional effects** inside countries (e.g., do transfers mainly benefit domestic poor vs. global poor).

  - country_code, year
  - private_philanthropy_total_usd
  - private_philanthropy_pct_gdp
  - participation_rate_in_giving (from CAF or national surveys)
  - volunteering_rate
  - helping_stranger_rate

- **Table: altruism_scenarios**
  - scenario_id
  - scenario_name
  - description
  - key_parameters (JSON or structured child table)

  - scenario_id, country_code, year (or “synthetic year”)
  - private_giving_scenario_usd
  - social_spend_scenario_usd
  - oda_scenario_usd
  - comparison_to_actual (ratios, differences)

This structure enables:

- Global aggregation (sum across countries).
- Regional aggregation.
- Scenario comparison by country and globally.

### M. Measurement, Uncertainty, and Methodological Caveats

No study aiming to be “the most comprehensive” on altruism can avoid grappling with measurement and uncertainty. This section sketches methodological issues and how to handle them.

#### M.1 Conceptual Boundaries of “Altruism”

- **State vs private**:  
  - State spending on education, health, and welfare is partly altruistic and partly insurance/entitlement; classifying it as “altruism” involves normative choices.
- **Domestic vs foreign**:  
  - Domestic welfare spending primarily benefits co-nationals; foreign aid benefits non-citizens. How to weight these morally is non-trivial.
- **Voluntary vs compulsory transfers**:  
  - Taxes are compulsory; charitable donations are voluntary. Both can be altruistically motivated; disentangling motives requires surveys and cannot be perfectly inferred from budgets.

#### M.2 Data Quality Issues

- **Inconsistent definitions**:
  - Social spending definitions differ across countries (e.g., what counts as “welfare” vs “education” vs “health”).
  - ODA definitions differ between DAC and non-DAC donors (e.g., Chinese development finance).
- **Under-counted informal giving**:
  - In many lower-income countries, informal transfers, religious giving, and family support are large but poorly measured.
- **Survey biases**:
  - Self-reported giving is subject to recall error and social desirability bias.
  - High-net-worth giving can be large but opaque, especially if routed through private vehicles.

#### M.3 Dealing with Uncertainty

Methods to incorporate uncertainty:

- **Ranges instead of points**:
  - Report plausible intervals for key quantities (e.g., private giving 0.5–2% of GDP).
- **Scenario analysis**:
  - Vary key assumptions (e.g., top 10% give 8–12% of income) and show how outcomes shift.
- **Sensitivity analysis**:
  - For each scenario, identify parameters that drive most of the variance in results.
- **Qualitative footnotes**:
  - Where quantitative data are weak, explicitly annotate with methodological notes rather than silently assuming precision.

#### M.4 Ethical and Normative Considerations

- **Whose welfare counts?**  
  Different moral theories (e.g., cosmopolitan egalitarianism vs. nationalist partiality) will weight domestic vs foreign altruism differently.

- **Altruism vs justice**  
  Some theorists argue that taxes funding welfare are matters of justice, not altruism; the classification itself is contested.

These considerations do not invalidate the numeric scenarios but should accompany them as interpretive context.

### N. Future Directions for Recursion, Refinement, and Scale-Up

To move iteratively toward an ever more comprehensive study, the following loops can be repeated:

1. **Data Expansion Loop**
   - Identify a set of countries or regions with weak coverage.
   - Use the data pipeline script (see below) to ingest fresh data from:
     - World Bank WDI (GDP/GNI/population).
     - OECD SOCX (social spending).
     - OECD DAC (aid).
     - CAF WGI (giving/volunteering/helping stranger).
   - Update country tables and regenerate summary visuals.

2. **Scenario Refinement Loop**
   - Replace starred parameters with empirically grounded values where microdata or high-quality surveys exist (e.g., observed giving rates by decile).
   - Introduce new scenarios (e.g., “Effective Altruism Adoption Scenario”, “Corporate Pledge Scenario”, “Wealth Tax + Targeted Aid Scenario”).

3. **Impact Modeling Loop**
   - For each scenario, model downstream impacts:
     - Poverty-gap reduction.
     - DALYs averted (health outcomes).
     - Educational attainment improvements.
   - Use cost-effectiveness estimates (with uncertainty ranges) from global health and development literature.

4. **Behavioral & Political Feasibility Loop**
   - Integrate insights from behavioral economics and political science:
     - Likely participation rates under different policies.
     - Political constraints on high ODA or social spending.
   - Update scenarios to reflect feasible upper bounds vs pure utopian limits.

With each iteration, the study can add:

- More countries.
- More years.
- Richer decomposition of transfer channels.
- Better uncertainty and sensitivity treatment.

The present appendix provides the framework for this iterative, recursive refinement process.

### O. Additional Scenario Family – Effective Altruism and “Targeted Impact” Worlds

To move beyond volumes of giving to **quality-adjusted altruism**, we can define scenarios based on the share of resources that are directed to relatively cost-effective interventions (as judged by some metric, e.g., DALYs averted per dollar, poverty gap reduction per dollar).

#### O.1 Definitions

- Let \( D \) denote total donation or transfer volume under a given scenario.
- Let \( \alpha \in [0,1] \) be the share of \( D \) allocated to “high-impact” uses (e.g., top-decile interventions by cost-effectiveness).
- Let \( \beta \in [0,1] \) be the share of \( D \) allocated to “moderate-impact” uses.
- Let \( 1-\alpha-\beta \) be the share allocated to “low-impact” or poorly targeted uses.

Let cost-effectiveness levels (illustrative):

- High-impact: \( \kappa_H^\* \) DALYs (or equivalent welfare units) per 1,000 USD.
- Moderate-impact: \( \kappa_M^\* \) DALYs per 1,000 USD.
- Low-impact: \( \kappa_L^\* \) DALYs per 1,000 USD.

Then the expected total health-equivalent gain:

\[
\text{Impact}(D, \alpha, \beta) \approx
D \left( \alpha \frac{\kappa_H^\*}{1000} +
          \beta \frac{\kappa_M^\*}{1000} +
          (1 - \alpha - \beta) \frac{\kappa_L^\*}{1000} \right).
\]

#### O.2 Effective Altruism Adoption Scenario

Consider a scenario where a fraction of donors follow an “effective altruism” (EA) style approach:

- Fraction \( p^\text{EA} \) of total giving is allocated entirely to high-impact uses (\(\alpha = 1\) for those donors).
- Remaining \( 1 - p^\text{EA} \) behave as in status quo (e.g., mostly local, less cost-effective giving).

Total impact:

\[
\text{Impact}^\text{EA}(D, p^\text{EA}) =
p^\text{EA} D \frac{\kappa_H^\*}{1000} +
(1 - p^\text{EA}) D \left( \alpha_0 \frac{\kappa_M^\*}{1000} +
                            (1 - \alpha_0) \frac{\kappa_L^\*}{1000} \right),
\]

where \( \alpha_0 \) is the baseline share of moderate-impact giving in the status quo.

We can then apply this on top of any earlier volume scenarios (e.g., Angels of Man, top-10% extrapolation), effectively stacking **“how much”** with **“how well”**.

#### O.3 Corporate and Billionaire Pledge Scenarios

Another layer:

- A fraction of global billionaires pledge to give a large share of wealth (e.g., 50–99%) during their lifetimes.
- Corporate profits are partially pledged to high-impact causes (e.g., a global norm of 2–5% of profits to effective charities).

Let:

- \( W^\text{top1\%}_\* \): estimated total wealth of the global top 1% (or specific billionaire subset).
- \( \lambda^\text{pledge}_\* \): average share of wealth pledged (e.g., 0.5–0.9*).
- \( \phi^\text{corporate}_\* \): share of global corporate profits allocated to EA-aligned causes.

Then incremental EA-style resources:

\[
D^\text{billionaire}_\text{EA} \approx \lambda^\text{pledge}_\* W^\text{top1\%}_\* / T,
\]

where \( T \) is the time horizon (years) over which pledges are realized, and

\[
D^\text{corporate}_\text{EA} \approx \phi^\text{corporate}_\* \cdot \Pi^\text{global}_\*,
\]

with \( \Pi^\text{global}_\* \) the assumed global corporate profit pool.

These can be aggregated with the individual scenarios to yield total EA-targeted flows and corresponding high-impact outcomes via the earlier impact formula.

### P. Mechanisms to Draw In Money: Funding Channels and Design Space

Thus far we have largely treated altruistic resources \( D \) as given by scenario parameters. In practice, a “most comprehensive” study must also map the **mechanisms** that convert preferences, norms, and institutions into actual flows.

#### P.1 Tax-Based Channels

- **Progressive income and wealth taxes**
  - Mechanism: increase marginal tax rates on high incomes/wealth and earmark specific slices for altruistic uses (domestic or foreign).
  - Modeling knob: tax schedule parameters \( \tau(y), \tau(w) \), and earmark shares \( \epsilon_\text{dom}, \epsilon_\text{ODA} \).
  - Risks: disincentive effects, capital flight, political feasibility.

- **Hypothecated “global solidarity” surcharges**
  - Examples: air-ticket levies, financial transaction micro-taxes.
  - Each can be modeled as a small rate \( \sigma \) on a large base (e.g., global financial turnover) yielding large absolute volumes with limited behavioral change.

#### P.2 Voluntary Private Giving Channels

- **Individual donations**
  - Classic levers: tax deductibility, matching grants, social norms and pledges, “giving what we can” commitments.
  - Parameters: participation rate \( \rho \), average giving rate \( g \), and targeting share \( \alpha \) (Section O).

- **Corporate philanthropy and CSR**
  - Mechanism: internal policies setting a share of profits or revenue for altruistic uses.
  - Parameter: \( \phi^\text{corporate}_\* \) (share of profits).

- **Bequests and legacy giving**
  - Mechanism: wills, donor-advised funds, foundations.
  - Parameter: share of estates left to altruistic causes.

#### P.3 Hybrid and Market-Mediated Channels

- **Social impact bonds / outcome-based financing**
  - Investors fund interventions; governments or philanthropists pay out if outcomes are achieved.
  - Connects capital markets to altruistic projects.

- **Pay-for-results contracts**
  - Especially for global health/education; donors only pay for verified impacts.

- **Altruistic consumer products**
  - A share of purchase price goes to charity (e.g., “1% for the planet”).
  - Parameter: share of global consumption subject to such schemes.

In a full model, each channel can be assigned:

- A potential “headroom” (max plausible global volume).
- A set of policy/communication levers that shift actual realized volume as a fraction of that headroom.

### Q. Mechanisms to Draw In Labor: Volunteering, Discounted Work, and Talent Flows

Money is only one dimension; **human time and expertise** are equally critical.

#### Q.1 Volunteering

- Variables:
  - \( h_i \): number of volunteering hours per year by individual \( i \).
  - \( v_i \): economic value per hour of their skills (could be wage rate or shadow price).
  - Total volunteer-equivalent value: \( V^\text{vol} = \sum_i h_i v_i \).

- Channels to increase \( V^\text{vol} \):
  - **Norms and campaigns** (e.g., “100 hours for others per year”).
  - **Employer-supported volunteering** (paid volunteering days).
  - **Flexible remote volunteering platforms** (matching skills to needs).

#### Q.2 Discounted or Pro Bono Work

- Professional services (law, accounting, software, design, medical, etc.) offered:
  - At **zero price** (pro bono).
  - At **discounted price** relative to market rate.

Let:

- \( w_j \): market wage rate for service type \( j \).
- \( \delta_j \in [0,1] \): discount fraction (e.g., 0.5 for 50% discount).
- \( H_j \): total hours contributed under such schemes.

Then the **implicit altruistic transfer** via discounted work is roughly:

\[
V^\text{discount}_j \approx \delta_j w_j H_j,
\quad
V^\text{discount}_\text{total} = \sum_j V^\text{discount}_j.
\]

These can be combined with monetary flows for a fuller picture of resource deployment.

#### Q.3 Career Choice and Talent Allocation

Another dimension is **where people choose to work**:

- Direct work in high-impact organizations (NGOs, social enterprises, public health, etc.).
- Earning-to-give (high-paying careers with large donation fractions).

Modeling knobs:

- Fraction of graduates choosing each path.
- Differential impact multipliers (e.g., 1 unit of labor in a critical neglected field vs. 1 unit in an already well-staffed field).

### R. Deployment Strategy: “Multiple Birds with Few Stones”

We can conceptualize altruistic deployment strategies as solving a **multi-objective optimization problem**:

- Objectives (examples):
  - Minimize global DALYs.
  - Minimize extreme poverty.
  - Reduce catastrophic risk.
  - Improve long-run institutional quality.

- Constraints:
  - Total resource envelope (money + time).
  - Political constraints on where resources can go.
  - Diminishing returns by cause/region.

#### R.1 Portfolio View

Let:

- \( k \) index cause areas (global health, education, climate, institutional reform, etc.).
- \( x_k \): resources allocated to cause \( k \).
- \( f_k(x_k) \): impact function (e.g., DALYs averted, poverty reduction).

We then choose \( x_k \) subject to:

\[
\sum_k x_k \leq D^\text{total}, \quad x_k \ge 0,
\]

to maximize some weighted objective:

\[
\max_{x_k} \sum_k w_k f_k(x_k),
\]

where \( w_k \) reflect moral and strategic weights.

#### R.2 “Multiple Birds” Interventions

Some interventions simultaneously score highly on multiple objectives:

- Example categories:
  - **Cash transfers + health** (e.g., conditional cash transfers tied to vaccinations).
  - **Education + health** (school-based deworming, nutrition).
  - **Climate + poverty** (clean cookstoves, green jobs).
  - **Institutional reform + everything else** (anti-corruption, civil service reform).

These can be modeled with **multi-dimensional impact vectors** \( \mathbf{f}_k(x_k) \) rather than scalars, and evaluated either:

- Against a weighted sum of objectives; or
- As part of a Pareto frontier analysis of tradeoffs.

In the context of the earlier volume scenarios, we can ask:

- If Angels-of-Man-level resources were available and 30–50% were channeled into high-synergy interventions, how much could we move multiple indicators at once?

### S. Summary of the Expanding Design Space

Putting Sections P–R together with earlier material, the full “altruism design space” includes:

- **Resource sources**:
  - Taxes, surcharges, private giving, corporate philanthropy, wealth/billionaire pledges.
  - Volunteering, discounted/pro bono work, and career choices.

- **Allocation rules**:
  - State budget processes (domestic vs foreign).
  - Individual and institutional giving norms.
  - EA-style cost-effectiveness targeting and “multiple birds” portfolios.

- **Outcome evaluation**:
  - Volumes (USD, volunteer-hours).
  - Impact units (DALYs, poverty gap, educational outcomes, risk reduction).
  - Uncertainty and sensitivity.

The iterative project going forward is to:

1. Populate these channels quantitatively for as many countries and years as possible.
2. Layer increasingly realistic behavioral and political constraints.
3. Explore not just how much altruism we can muster, but **how to deploy it so that every dollar or hour is doing as much good as possible.**

### T. Actionable Package Roadmap – From Design to Evaluation

To keep this project organized and expandable, we define a roadmap for **actionable altruism packages** and their analysis.

#### T.1 Phases

- **Phase 1 – Package Design**
  - Define concrete, implementable packages (bundles of policies, norms, and mechanisms).
  - For each package, specify:
    - Target population(s) and countries.
    - Funding channels used (tax, pledges, corporate, etc.).
    - Labor channels used (volunteering, pro bono, careers).
    - Allocation rules across cause areas and geographies.

- **Phase 2 – Evaluation Methodology**
  - Choose a minimal, consistent impact model (volume → outcomes).
  - Identify cost-effectiveness priors for each major cause area.
  - Define equity/ethical lenses (e.g. global utilitarian, domestic-priority, risk-focused).

- **Phase 3 – Package Evaluation**
  - Implement code to:
    - Take package parameters as input.
    - Simulate flows of money and time.
    - Allocate across causes.
    - Compute volume, impact, and equity metrics.
  - Apply to all packages and generate comparable tables and figures.

- **Phase 4 – Synthesis and Narrative**
  - Compare packages on:
    - Raw impact (DALYs, poverty, etc.).
    - Robustness (sensitivity to assumptions).
    - Political and behavioral feasibility.
  - Write qualitative profiles and “character sketches” of each package.

### U. Initial Impact Evaluation Framework (Volume, Impact, Equity)

To apply a consistent lens across packages, we define a minimal evaluation framework with three layers: **volume**, **impact**, and **equity**.

#### U.1 Volume Layer

For a given package \( P \) in a context (e.g. region, income group) we compute:

- **Monetary volume**
  - \( D^\text{money}(P) \): total annual monetary resources generated (USD).
  - Broken down by channel:
    - Individual income pledges.
    - Tax increases earmarked for altruism.
    - Corporate profit shares.
    - Wealth pledges (annualized over a horizon \( T \)).

- **Labor volume**
  - \( H^\text{vol}(P) \): total volunteer hours per year.
  - \( V^\text{labor}(P) \): implicit economic value of those hours (e.g., using wage rates).

At this layer we do not yet care *where* resources go—only how much is mobilized.

#### U.2 Impact Layer

We map volumes into **outcomes** via:

- A set of cause areas \( k \) (e.g., global health, poverty, education, climate).
- Allocation shares \( a_k(P) \) (fraction of total resources going to cause \( k \)).
- Cost-effectiveness priors \( \kappa_k^\* \) (e.g., DALYs averted per dollar, poverty gap reduction per dollar).

For each cause \( k \):

\[
\text{Impact}_k(P) \approx D^\text{money}(P) \cdot a_k(P) \cdot \frac{\kappa_k^\*}{1000},
\]

where \(\kappa_k^\*\) is expressed per 1,000 USD for scaling. For labor, we can:

- Either convert \( V^\text{labor}(P) \) into equivalent dollars and add to \( D^\text{money}(P) \); or
- Use cause-specific labor productivities (e.g., DALYs per 100 specialist-hours).

The EA targeting share \( \text{ea\_target\_share}(P) \) influences \( a_k(P) \) by assigning a higher fraction to the most cost-effective causes.

#### U.3 Equity Layer

We track **who benefits** by introducing beneficiary groups \( b \) (e.g., domestic poor, global extreme poor, future generations). For each group:

\[
\text{Impact}_{k,b}(P) = \text{Impact}_k(P) \cdot \theta_{k,b},
\]

where \( \theta_{k,b} \) is the share of impact from cause \( k \) accruing to group \( b \).

We can then compute summary indices, for example:

- Share of total impact accruing to the global poorest decile.
- Ratio of domestic to foreign beneficiaries.
- Intergenerational split (present vs future).

#### U.4 Implementation Notes

This framework is deliberately simple and modular:

- Each package instance provides:
  - Pledge rates, tax changes, corporate shares, volunteer hours, EA targeting share.
- Context data (from the pipeline) provide:
  - Number of eligible people/firms, income levels, profit pools, wage rates.
- The evaluation code:
  - Multiplies and aggregates to get \(D^\text{money}(P)\) and \(H^\text{vol}(P)\).
  - Applies allocation and cost-effectiveness assumptions to get impact metrics.
  - Outputs a structured result (e.g., JSON, CSV) for comparison across packages.

More sophisticated models (e.g., diminishing returns in \( f_k(x_k) \), Section R) can be layered on later, but this initial framework provides a consistent backbone for comparative evaluation.
