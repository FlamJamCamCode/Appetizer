## Altruism Country Deep Dive – Saudi Arabia (SA)

> **Status**: First-pass scaffold with approximate ranges (**\***). To be refined with Saudi budget data, zakat flows, and philanthropy estimates.

### 1. Macro Context

- Population: ~36M\*
- GDP (nominal): ~USD 1.0–1.1T/year\*
- GDP per capita: ~USD 27,000–30,000/year\*
- High-income, resource-rich monarchy with strong Islamic institutions.

### 2. Government Social Spending and Fiscal Flows

Approximate:

- Total government expenditure ~30–35% of GDP\*.
- Social spending (broadly defined):
  - Public sector employment and wages.
  - Subsidies (historically energy, housing, etc.).
  - Health: ~4–5% of GDP\*.
  - Education: ~5–7% of GDP\*.
  - Direct cash/welfare transfers: context-specific and evolving.

Saudi Arabia is undergoing reforms (Vision 2030) that change the composition of spending and subsidies.

### 3. International Aid and Development Support

Saudi Arabia is a significant donor:

- Provides:
  - Bilateral aid to other Muslim-majority and developing countries.
  - Humanitarian assistance.
  - Development loans via funds like the Saudi Fund for Development.

ODA-like flows as % of GNI:

- Some years have exceeded 1% if including concessional loans and grants\*.
- However, these flows are often not fully captured in OECD DAC statistics.

### 4. Zakat and Private Religious Giving

In Islamic contexts:

- **Zakat**: obligatory almsgiving (often ~2.5% of qualifying wealth annually).
- **Sadaqah**: voluntary charity beyond zakat.

In Saudi Arabia:

- Precise aggregate data is scarce, but:
  - Zakat flows from individuals and corporations likely total **billions of USD per year**.
  - Some zakat is collected by official bodies; much is given directly or via religious institutions.

These flows often support:

- Poor households.
- Religious institutions.
- Humanitarian and social welfare programmes domestically and abroad.

### 5. Informal Altruism and Remittances

- Remittances from migrant workers in Saudi Arabia to their home countries are large in aggregate (benefiting origin countries).
- Saudi citizens’ informal giving:
  - Community and family support.
  - Contributions during Ramadan, Eid, and emergencies.

### 6. Cause-Area Context

Domestic:

- Leading causes of death:
  - Non-communicable diseases (CVD, diabetes, obesity-related conditions).
  - Road traffic injuries significant.
  - Some occupational and environmental health issues.

International:

- Saudi-origin aid often targets:
  - Humanitarian crises in Muslim-majority countries.
  - Infrastructure and development in partner states.

### 7. Cluster Scenarios

Clusters:

- High-wealth families, business elites, royal family members.

If, for example:

- A subset of wealthy Saudis (e.g. 100,000 individuals\*) committed to:
  - 1% of wealth/year explicitly for CESET-optimized interventions:
    - If average wealth ~USD 5M\*, flows ~USD 5B/year from this cluster alone.

Layered on top of:

- Official aid and zakat flows, such CESET-oriented commitments could strongly impact:
  - Child mortality in partner countries.
  - Refugee assistance.
  - Global Islamic philanthropy reoriented toward transparent, outcome-tracked projects.

### 8. NSS/Triad Considerations

Saudi Arabia is a plausible anchor for:

- **Zakat Optimization Triads**:
  - Collecting and deploying zakat in ways that maximize CESET reductions while remaining faithful to Islamic principles.
- **Ummah Solidarity Triads**:
  - Coordinating aid across the Muslim world with more data-driven targeting (using datasets like Our World in Data’s cause-of-death profiles).
