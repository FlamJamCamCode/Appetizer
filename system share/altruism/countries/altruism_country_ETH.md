### 4. Interpretive Notes for Ethiopia (Horn of Africa Context \*)

- **Poverty and health burden**  
  - Ethiopia is a low-income, predominantly rural country with high burdens of child and maternal mortality and infectious disease (including malaria in some regions).  
  - In such a context, the engine’s strong performance of **Volunteer & Talent Surge** (top package in the current run) is intuitive: there is a severe shortage of skilled health and education workers, so additional human capital is modeled as extremely valuable.

- **How high-commitment giving would play out**  
  - Packages like **Angels-lite** and **Tax & Match** (if feasible) would direct large monetary flows into highly cost-effective health and poverty interventions.  
  - Given Ethiopia’s low baseline incomes, the DALYs-per-dollar assumptions for global health and cash transfers likely understate their true impact here; targeting bottom deciles and rural areas would skew benefits heavily toward the **domestic poor** in the equity model.

- **State vs. private channels**  
  - Ethiopia historically receives significant **official development assistance**; state capacity exists but is uneven across regions.  
  - In practice, much of the additional altruistic flow modeled by the engine would likely be routed via:
    - International NGOs and multilateral agencies.  
    - Bilateral donors and global health funds.  
  - This suggests that for Ethiopia, packages emphasizing **external donor commitments** and **NGO-executed health and education projects** may be more realistic than large domestic tax-based increases in the short term.

- **Next refinement steps**  
  - Incorporate Ethiopia-specific disease burden (e.g., malaria, TB, undernutrition) and education indicators.  
  - Adjust cause weights and cost-effectiveness parameters so that “malaria/deworming/basic health” better reflect Ethiopia’s actual burden rather than global averages.
