## Altruism Country Deep Dive – United States (US)

> **Status**: First-pass scaffold. Ranges and several numbers are approximate and marked with **\***. This file is designed to be iteratively refined with more precise data and scripts.

### 1. Macro Context

- Population: ~333M\*
- GDP: ~USD 27T/year\*
- GDP per capita: ~USD 80,000/year\*
- Government structure: federal + state + local.

### 2. Government Social Spending and Fiscal Flows

Approximate social expenditure (all levels of government combined)\*:

- **Total social spending**: ~18–20% of GDP.
  - Pensions (Social Security): ~5% of GDP\*.
  - Health (Medicare, Medicaid, ACA subsidies, public health): ~8–9% of GDP\*.
  - Other social programmes (unemployment, SNAP, housing assistance, disability, family benefits): ~3–5% of GDP\*.

Non-social:

- Defense and national security: ~3–4% of GDP.
- Interest on the debt: ~2–3% of GDP (variable).

### 3. Official Development Assistance (ODA) / Foreign Aid

- ODA/GNI ratio: ~0.22%\*.
- Annual ODA volume: ~USD 50–65B/year\*.

Main channels:

- USAID and State Department programmes.
- PEPFAR and global health funding (HIV, TB, malaria).
- Humanitarian and disaster relief.
- Military/strategic aid.

### 4. Private Philanthropy

According to Giving USA and similar sources\*:

- Total private giving (individuals, foundations, bequests, corporations):
  - ~USD 450–600B/year in recent years.
- Rough breakdown:
  - Individuals: ~70% of total.
  - Foundations: ~18%.
  - Bequests: ~9%.
  - Corporations: ~3–5%.

Sectoral allocation (approximate share of total)\*:

- Religion: ~23%.
- Education: ~14%.
- Human services: ~14%.
- Grantmaking foundations: ~11%.
- Public-society benefit: ~11%.
- Health: ~10%.
- International affairs: ~6–7%.
- Arts, culture, environment, etc.: remaining share.

### 5. Giving and Volunteering by Demographics

Patterns (approximate)\*:

- Age:
  - Median donor age ~65.
  - Older cohorts give larger amounts (wealth accumulation & bequests).
- Income:
  - Higher-income households give more in absolute terms.
  - Middle-income households often give higher % of income.
- Volunteering:
  - ~25–30% of adults volunteer in a given year.
  - ~5B volunteer hours/year; imputed value ~USD 100–150B/year.

### 6. Microdistribution Sketch

Rough illustrative distribution of giving as % of income by income decile\*:

- Bottom decile: 0.5–1.0% of income.
- 2nd–5th decile: 1–2%.
- 6th–9th decile: 2–4%.
- Top decile: 3–6%.

Scenario overlays:

- Scenario A (“Angels as Mass Man”): could raise the **average** giving rate to ~2–3% of income across all deciles.
- Scenario C variants: focus on top decile (or top 5%) giving 10–20% of income.

### 7. Cause-Area Mapping for US-Origin Flows

US-origin altruistic resources (public + private) are currently distributed across:

- Domestic:
  - Health coverage, pensions, food assistance, housing.
  - Domestic NGOs (poverty, education, civil rights, etc.).
- International:
  - Global health (HIV, malaria, TB, vaccines).
  - Humanitarian aid, conflict response.
  - Development and infrastructure.

From a **global CESET** perspective:

- Significant share of official US aid and some private philanthropy already targets high-impact areas (e.g. child mortality, infectious disease).
- Large potential remains to:
  - Increase the **ODA/GNI ratio** toward 0.7–1.0%.
  - Reorient high-dollar sectors (religion, education, some domestic health philanthropy) towards more direct CESET reductions abroad.

### 8. Cluster Scenarios Inside the US

Examples (see master appendix A.18):

- High-income childless adults:
  - If 10M such individuals gave 10% of income (~USD 100k/year each on average), that alone could generate ~USD 100B/year for global CESET work.

  - A 1–2% wealth-commitment among US billionaires could mobilize tens to hundreds of billions annually.
