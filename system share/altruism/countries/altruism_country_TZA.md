### 4. Interpretive Notes for Tanzania (Nilotic / Lake Victoria Region \*)

- **Disease burden and geography**  
  - Tanzania spans coastal, inland, and Lake Victoria regions with substantial **malaria**, **neglected tropical disease**, and maternal/child health burdens, though progress has been made.  
  - Education access has expanded, but quality and completion remain challenges, especially in poorer regions.

- **Engine’s package logic**  
  - In Tanzania, as in Kenya and Uganda, packages that heavily fund **malaria-like**, **deworming**, **basic health**, and **targeted cash transfers** are modeled as very high-impact.  
  - Angels-lite and Tax & Match–style scenarios, where a relatively small group of high-commitment givers fund these interventions, therefore look especially powerful in the engine.

- **Equity and beneficiaries**  
  - Equity splits assign most gains from these causes to the **domestic poor** in Tanzania, consistent with the reality that rural communities, fishing communities around Lake Victoria, and poorer urban districts bear the greatest disease burden.  
  - Education and climate components also deliver benefits to **future generations**, aligning with the importance of long-term investments in schooling and climate resilience (e.g., agriculture, water).

- **Channels and actors**  
  - Tanzania relies on a mix of:
    - Government health and education spending,  
    - Global health partnerships and NGOs, and  
    - Faith-based and community organizations.  
  - High-impact packages in practice would likely be realized via:
    - Strengthened national systems,  
    - Expanded NGO programs, and  
    - Pooled international and domestic philanthropy.

- **Next refinement steps**  
  - Bring in Tanzania-specific malaria, NTD, and education effectiveness data to refine cause-level DALY estimates.  
  - Calibrate the model’s “cause mix” to the Tanzanian burden profile rather than global averages.
