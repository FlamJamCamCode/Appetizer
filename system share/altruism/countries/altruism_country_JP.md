## Altruism Country Deep Dive – Japan (JP)

> **Status**: First-pass scaffold with approximate ranges (**\***). To be refined with MOF, OECD, and Japanese philanthropy data.

### 1. Macro Context

- Population: ~124M\*
- GDP: ~USD 4.2–4.4T/year\*
- GDP per capita: ~USD 40,000–45,000/year\*
- Aging high-income country with large public debt and strong social cohesion norms.

### 2. Government Social Spending and Fiscal Flows

Approximate social expenditure\*:

- Total social spending: ~22–24% of GDP.
  - Pensions: ~10–11% of GDP\*.
  - Health and long-term care: ~9–10% of GDP\*.
  - Other social programmes: ~3–4% of GDP\*.

Other:

- Education: ~3–4% of GDP.
- Defense: ~1–2% of GDP (with recent increases).
- Very high public debt (over 200% of GDP), but mostly domestically held.

### 3. Official Development Assistance (ODA)

- ODA/GNI: ~0.3–0.35%\*.
- Annual ODA volume: ~USD 15–20B/year\*.

Focus areas:

- Infrastructure and economic development in Asia.
- Disaster prevention and resilience.
- Peacebuilding and governance support.

### 4. Private Philanthropy and Informal Altruism

Formal philanthropy:

- Private giving ~0.2–0.4% of GDP\* (lower than US/UK but non-trivial).
- Corporate giving and foundations present but less globally prominent.

Informal:

- Strong norms around:
  - Mutual help within families and communities.
  - Volunteering around disasters (earthquakes, tsunamis).

Religion:

- Buddhism and Shinto, but secularization is high; religious giving is less central than in some other countries.

### 5. Volunteering and Civil Society

Japan has:

- A broad network of:
  - Neighborhood associations.
  - NGOs and NPOs, particularly around aging, disability, and environment.

Volunteering rates:

- Moderately high, particularly among retirees.

### 6. Cause-Area Context

Within Japan:

- Leading causes of death:
  - Non-communicable diseases (cardiovascular, cancers).
  - Dementia/Alzheimer’s.
- Very low child mortality and infectious disease burden.
- Aging is central:
  - High proportion of population 65+.
  - Large burden of age-related diseases and care needs.

Implication:

- Domestic altruism heavily oriented toward:
  - Elderly care, dementia support, end-of-life care.
  - Mental health, social isolation.
- International altruism (ODA + philanthropy) more relevant to:
  - Disaster relief.
  - Development in less developed countries, especially in Asia.

### 7. Cluster Scenarios and Potential

Clusters:

- Wealthy elderly with no or few heirs.
- High-income, childless professionals.

Examples:

- If 1M wealthy Japanese retirees\* committed 1–2% of net wealth/year:
  - Could mobilize tens of billions of USD-equivalent annually, used for:
    - Aging research Triads.
    - Global CESET interventions (child mortality abroad).

### 8. NSS/Triad Considerations

Japan is an ideal base for:

- **Aging and Longevity Triads**:
  - Large domestic constituency that experiences aging issues first-hand.
  - High scientific and technological capacity.
- **Disaster Resilience Triads**:
  - Experience with earthquakes, tsunamis, and disaster response.

Japan-origin flows could:

- Help solve both:
  - Domestic age-related suffering.
  - Global child/infectious disease burdens.
