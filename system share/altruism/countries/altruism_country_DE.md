## Altruism Country Deep Dive – Germany (DE)

> **Status**: First-pass scaffold with approximate ranges (**\***). To be refined with OECD, German budget, and philanthropy data in later iterations.

### 1. Macro Context

- Population: ~84M\*
- GDP: ~USD 4.3–4.5T/year\*
- GDP per capita: ~USD 50,000–55,000/year\*
- High-income EU welfare state with strong social insurance and export-led economy.

### 2. Government Social Spending and Fiscal Flows

Approximate social expenditure (all government levels)\*:

- Total social spending: ~25–27% of GDP.
  - Public pensions: ~10% of GDP\*.
  - Health insurance and LTC: ~9–10% of GDP\*.
  - Unemployment, family benefits, housing, social assistance: ~5–7% of GDP\*.

Other:

- Defense: ~1.5–2.0% of GDP (increasing with NATO commitments).
- Education: ~4–5% of GDP.

Germany has one of the **largest social budgets** globally in per-capita terms.

### 3. Official Development Assistance (ODA)

- ODA/GNI: ~0.75–0.8%\* (one of the few large donors near or above the UN 0.7% target).
- Annual ODA volume: ~USD 30–35B/year\*.

Focus areas:

- Development cooperation (GIZ, KfW).
- Global health, education, infrastructure.
- Refugee-related costs (sometimes counted in ODA).

### 4. Private Philanthropy

Germany has:

- Formal giving ~0.4–0.6% of GDP\*.
- Total private donations on the order of **EUR 15–25B/year**\* (individuals, foundations, corporations).

Characteristics:

- Strong foundation sector (Stiftungen) often tied to family businesses.
- Corporate philanthropy and CSR significant but less “spectacular” than US tech philanthropy.
- Religion (church tax) plays a role:
  - Mandatory church tax (Kirchensteuer) for members of major churches (~8–9% of income tax) finances church activities, including some social services.

### 5. Informal Altruism and Volunteering

Volunteering:

- High participation in:
  - Sports clubs, volunteer fire brigades, cultural associations.
  - Social and charitable organizations.

Informal giving:

- Family transfers, neighbor help, and migrant remittances (within EU and beyond).

### 6. Cause-Area Context

Cause-of-death structure (similar to other rich countries):

- Dominated by non-communicable diseases:
  - Cardiovascular diseases.
  - Cancers.
  - Chronic respiratory diseases.
- Very low child mortality and infectious disease burdens.

Implication:

- Domestic altruism primarily addresses:
  - Social inclusion, elderly care, mental health, integration of migrants, environmental issues.
- International altruism (ODA + philanthropy) more relevant for:
  - High-burden countries in Africa, Asia, and Middle East.

### 7. Cluster and Scenario Potential

Clusters:

- High-income professionals and Mittelstand owners.
- Childless adults in urban centers (Berlin, Hamburg, Munich).

If, for example:

- 2M high-income childless adults committed to give 10% of income (say EUR 100,000/year each on average)\*:
  - Additional ~EUR 20B/year could be directed to global CESET work.

Another cluster:

- Elderly Germans with substantial pension income and assets:
  - Legacy or wealth-based commitments (1–2% of wealth annually) could easily reach **tens of billions** of EUR/year.

### 8. NSS/Triad Considerations

Germany as:

- A major pillar in:
  - “Top altruist state” scenarios (high ODA, strong social spending).
  - Potential funding base for:
    - Aging research Triads.
    - Climate/energy transition Triads.
    - Child mortality and global health Triads.
