## Altruism Country Deep Dive – Brazil (BR)

> **Status**: First-pass scaffold with approximate ranges (**\***). To be refined with Brazilian budget, IPEA, and philanthropy data.

### 1. Macro Context

- Population: ~215M\*
- GDP: ~USD 1.9–2.1T/year\*
- GDP per capita: ~USD 8,500–10,000/year\*
- Upper-middle-income country with high inequality and regional disparities.

### 2. Government Social Spending and Fiscal Flows

Approximate social expenditure\*:

- Total social spending (incl. pensions, health, social programmes): ~15–18% of GDP.
  - Public pensions (INSS + civil service): ~8–10% of GDP\*.
  - Health (SUS – Sistema Único de Saúde): ~4% of GDP\*.
  - Bolsa Família / Auxílio Brasil and other social programs: ~1–2% of GDP\*.

Other:

- Education: ~5–6% of GDP.
- Public debt service: substantial share of budget (varies by year).

### 3. ODA – Donor and Recipient

- Brazil is mostly a **recipient** of ODA but also engages in:
  - South–South cooperation in Latin America and Africa.
  - Technical assistance via Brazilian Cooperation Agency (ABC).
- ODA given as % of GNI: very small (<0.05%)\*.

### 4. Private Philanthropy and Informal Altruism

Formal giving:

- Estimates suggest formal charitable donations ~0.2–0.4% of GDP\*.
- Strong corporate philanthropy and CSR from large Brazilian firms.
- Growing foundation sector, often linked to business families.

Informal altruism:

- Very important:
  - Family support networks.
  - Religious giving (churches, especially evangelical, Catholic).
  - Community-level solidarity in favelas and rural areas.

### 5. Volunteering and Civil Society

Brazil has:

- A vibrant civil society:
  - NGOs focused on education, child protection, environmental issues (Amazon).
  - Faith-based charities.
  - Neighbourhood associations.

Volunteering data is incomplete; future iterations should:

- Use CAF World Giving Index and local surveys for rates of:
  - Helping strangers.
  - Donating money.
  - Volunteering time.

### 6. Cause-Area Context

Within Brazil:

- Child mortality has declined significantly, but:
  - Neonatal issues, diarrheal diseases, and infections remain in poorer regions.
- Major issues:
  - Violence and homicide (especially young men).
  - Road injuries.
  - Non-communicable diseases (CVD, diabetes).
  - Infectious diseases (e.g. dengue, TB) in some areas.

In global terms:

- Brazil’s main CESET contributions could come from:
  - Reducing domestic violence and accidents.
  - Protecting the Amazon (massive climate and biodiversity implications).

### 7. Cluster Scenarios

Examples:

- High-income Brazilians in São Paulo, Rio, Brasília:
  - If 1M such individuals (top few percent) gave an extra 5–10% of income, flows of **USD 5–10B/year** are plausible\*.
- Agribusiness and resource-sector elites:
  - Wealth-based commitments (1–2% of wealth/year) could unlock significant resources for:
    - Amazon conservation Triads.
    - Rural health and education Triads.

### 8. NSS/Triad Considerations

Potential Triads centered on Brazil:

- **Amazon Conservation and Indigenous Rights Triad**:
  - Combining climate, biodiversity, and human rights.
- **Urban Violence and Youth Triad**:
  - Focused on homicide reduction and opportunity creation for young men in favelas.
- **Rural Health and Sanitation Triad**:
  - Targeting diarrhea, parasites, and maternal health in poor rural areas.

These Triads could be funded both domestically (Brazilian philanthropists, CSR) and internationally.
