## Part IV: The Competitive Pressure Insight (The Key to Realistic Utopia)

### Chapter 9: Why MIRA Springs Can't Be Too Extractive

**The realization** (2037-2040):

**Bergen Spring has fusion monopole** (Schmidt controls, can extract massively from nations wanting fusion).

**Bergen's options**:

**Option A: Maximum extraction** (charge 80% tribute, pure profit, maximize extraction)
- Result: Nations suffer (80% extracted, subjects miserable, unhappy)
- Effect: Subjects flee (to bunkers if can afford, to non-fusion regions, to other Springs if accepted)
- Long-term: Bergen loses subjects (nobody works for 80% extraction willingly, enforcement costly, unsustainable)

**Option B: Moderate extraction** (charge 30-50%, build infrastructure with proceeds, benevolent use)
- Result: Nations benefit (fusion power, infrastructure, subjects happier)
- Effect: Subjects stay (prefer Bergen extraction to alternatives, willingly cooperate, enforcement unnecessary)
- Long-term: Bergen gains subjects (migration TO Bergen regions, willing cooperation, sustainable)

**The competitive pressure**: Bunkers exist (subjects can flee if extraction too harsh, €5M entry, freedom underground, alternative exists). This constrains Bergen (must stay gentle enough that subjects prefer Bergen extraction to bunker escape, can't be too harsh or lose subjects).

**Bergen choice**: Option B (moderate extraction, benevolent use, competitive pressure from bunkers forces this).

---

**The mechanism**: **Bunkers create competitive pressure on Springs to stay gentle** (subjects have exit option, Springs must compete with bunkers for subjects, can't be too extractive or subjects flee).

**This is the realistic path to utopia**:
- NOT "Springs are benevolent because members are good people" (naive, unrealistic, power corrupts)
- BUT "Springs are forced to be benevolent by competitive pressure from bunkers" (realistic, subjects can exit, Springs must stay gentle to retain subjects, market discipline)
