## Part II: Why Institutional Capital Avoids Long-Term R&D

### The Structural Barriers

**1. Fiduciary Duty Interpretation**

Pension funds operate under fiduciary duty to beneficiaries:
- **Interpreted as**: "Maximize returns while minimizing risk"
- **Leads to**: Investing in known, liquid, predictable assets
- **Avoids**: Unproven R&D, long-term bets, basic research

**The problem**: Conservative interpretation
- Trustees fear being sued for "risky" investments
- Even if long-term EV is positive, near-term uncertainty is liability
- **Result**: Herd into "safe" assets (government bonds, blue-chip stocks)

**Example**:
- Bell Labs ROI: 200:1 over 50 years
- But any single project might fail (90% of R&D fails)
- Trustee who funded failed project = sued
- Trustee who bought safe bonds = not sued (even if returns lower)

**Perverse incentive**: Punishes long-term thinking, rewards short-term safety

---

**2. Quarterly Reporting and Short-Termism**

**Asset managers judged quarterly**:
- Performance measured every 3 months
- Underperformance = clients withdraw funds
- **Investment horizon**: Effectively 1-4 quarters despite claiming "long-term"

**The pressure**:
- Must show returns THIS quarter
- R&D takes 5-20 years to pay off
- Can't justify to clients: "Trust me, in 15 years this will work"
- **Result**: Can only invest in assets with near-term liquidity/returns

**Example**:
- Bell Labs: Took 3 years (transistor), 11 years (laser), 22 years (Unix commercialization)
- Modern asset manager: Can't wait 3 years, let alone 22
- Would be fired before payoff

---

**3. Liquidity Requirements**

**Pension funds must pay beneficiaries**:
- Retirees need monthly checks
- Can't wait for R&D to pay off
- **Need**: Assets they can sell quickly if necessary

**Illiquidity premium exists but limited**:
- Pensions will accept SOME illiquidity (real estate, infrastructure)
- But only if RETURNS are guaranteed/predictable
- **Won't accept**: Illiquid AND uncertain (basic R&D)

**Example**:
- Real estate: Illiquid but predictable (rents, property value)
- Basic research: Illiquid AND uncertain (might discover nothing)
- **Pension chooses**: Real estate (can model returns)

---

**4. Information Asymmetry and Inability to Evaluate**

**Asset managers don't have expertise to evaluate R&D**:
- Physics research: Requires PhD physicists to assess
- Biotech: Requires domain experts
- AI research: Rapidly changing, hard to evaluate

**The problem**:
- Can't tell good R&D from bad R&D
- Fraudsters can pitch garbage as breakthrough
- No way to verify claims without being expert
- **Result**: Avoid R&D entirely (too hard to evaluate)

**Example**:
- Theranos fooled $700M in VC funding (blood testing fraud)
- Investors couldn't evaluate technology claims
- Lost everything
- **Lesson**: Don't fund what you can't understand

**Contrast with stocks**:
- Can evaluate companies via earnings, revenue, market share
- Quarterly reports provide information
- Analysts can model value
- **Result**: Comfortable investing

---

**5. The Principal-Agent Problem**

**Fund managers are AGENTS, not principals**:
- Don't own the capital (beneficiaries/clients do)
- Paid for managing it (fees)
- **Incentive**: Don't get fired, collect fees

**Risk-return asymmetry**:
- If risky bet pays off: Manager gets small bonus
- If risky bet fails: Manager gets fired
- **Rational strategy**: Take no risks, collect fees safely

**Example**:
- Manager considers $10B investment in fusion R&D
- If succeeds (10% probability): Creates $1 trillion value, manager gets $10M bonus
- If fails (90% probability): Loses $10B, manager gets fired, loses $1M/year job
- **Expected value for manager**: Negative (career risk >> bonus)
- **Result**: Don't invest in fusion

**Contrast with owner-operators**:
- Elon Musk owns SpaceX (principal, not agent)
- Can take 20-year bet on Mars
- If it works: He captures value
- If it fails: His loss, his choice
- **Result**: Will fund long-term R&D

**The agency problem prevents patient capital**

---

**6. The Coordination Problem**

**To fund something like Bell Labs requires**:
- $2 billion/year sustained for 50 years = $100 billion total
- No single fund wants to fund alone (risk concentration)
- Need consortium of 10-20 funds

**But coordination is hard**:
- Who manages the research?
- How are returns distributed?
- What if some funds want to exit?
- Who makes decisions about research direction?

**Result**: Each fund says "someone else should do it" (tragedy of the commons)

**Example**:
- CERN works because governments coordinate (23 member states)
- But took decades of negotiation
- Private funds can't coordinate as easily (competitive, no political mechanisms)

---

**7. The Bell Labs Monopoly Lesson**

**Why Bell Labs worked** (1925-1984):
- AT&T had legal monopoly on US telecommunications
- Guaranteed revenue stream ($26B/year in 1974)
- No competition (could afford long-term R&D without quarterly pressure)
- Regulators REQUIRED R&D spending (as condition of monopoly)

**What happened after 1984 breakup**:
- AT&T monopoly broken up
- Bell Labs funding cut dramatically
- Competitive pressure forced short-term focus
- Innovation declined sharply

**The lesson**: **Monopoly structure enabled patient capital** that competitive markets can't replicate

**Why competitive markets fail at R&D**:
- Companies in competition can't afford 20-year R&D (competitors will beat them with faster returns)
- Shareholders demand quarterly returns
- Executives compensated on short-term stock price
- **Result**: Only incremental innovation (3-5 year payoffs), no basic research

**This is the "theft of merit" problem MIRA identifies**:
- True innovators can't capture value (competitors copy once proven)
- Capital captures value through speed/scale (not innovation)
- **Result**: Innovators discouraged, system inefficient

**Examples**:

  - **ROI**: 200:1 over 50 years

  - **ROI**: 7-14:1 (conservative estimates)
- **CERN**: $20B investment → World Wide Web alone = $trillions
  - **ROI**: Incalculable (WWW transformed civilization)

**Compared to "safe" investments**:
- **Government bonds**: 2-5% annual return
  - Over 50 years: Doubles money 2-7× ($1 → $2-7)
- **Stock market**: 7-10% annual return
  - Over 50 years: Grows 30-100× ($1 → $30-100)
- **Bell Labs-style R&D**: 200:1 return over 50 years
  - **$1 → $200** (vastly superior)

**Why don't pensions invest in R&D if returns are 200:1?**

---

### The Six Barriers (Detailed)

**Barrier 1: Time Horizon Mismatch**

**Pensions/asset managers**:
- Claim "long-term" (30-50 years to pay retirees)
- But judged quarterly/annually
- Can't stomach 15-year "no returns" period
- Need to show progress to beneficiaries

**R&D reality**:
- First 10-15 years: Pure spending (no revenue)
- Years 15-30: Early commercialization (break-even)
- Years 30-50: Massive returns (technology matures)

**Mismatch**: Pensions CLAIM they can wait 50 years, but ACT like they can only wait 1 year

---

**Barrier 2: Risk Intolerance (Despite High Expected Value)**

**R&D characteristics**:
- 90% of projects fail completely (total loss)
- 5% break even
- 5% create enormous value (100-1000× returns)

**Expected value calculation**:
- $1B invested in 10 projects ($100M each)
- 9 fail: -$900M
- 1 succeeds: Returns $20B
- **Net**: +$19.1B on $1B investment (19:1 return)

**Pension fund psychology**:
- Sees "90% failure rate" 
- Fears headlines: "Pension fund lost $900M on failed research"
- Ignores: "Pension fund made $19.1B net"
- **Result**: Won't invest despite positive expected value

**This is irrational** but structurally enforced:
- Trustees sued for losses (not praised for wins)
- Public outcry over any loss (even if overall profitable)
- Beneficiaries don't understand expected value

---

**Barrier 3: Evaluation Impossibility**

**How does pension fund evaluate**:
- "Should we fund quantum computing research at $500M?"
- vs "Should we fund synthetic biology at $500M?"
- vs "Should we fund high-energy physics at $500M?"

**Problem**: Requires domain expertise pensions don't have
- Can't evaluate scientific merit
- Can't assess researcher quality
- Can't predict breakthrough probability
- Can't verify claims (researchers might lie)

**Result**: Funds give up on R&D entirely (too hard to evaluate)

**Contrast with VC**:
- VCs specialize (biotech VCs fund biotech only)
- Build domain expertise
- But even VCs avoid basic research (too uncertain)
- VCs fund commercialization (10% basic research, 90% product development)

---

**Barrier 4: The Free Rider Problem**

**If pension fund invests in basic research**:
- Research succeeds
- Results published (academic tradition)
- Entire world benefits (free access to knowledge)
- **Fund gets**: Small fraction of value created

**Example**:
- Fund invests $1B in fusion research
- Fusion achieved
- Results published
- All countries build fusion plants
- **Fund's return**: Maybe 1% of global fusion value (everyone else free-rides)
- **Social return**: 100% (entire world benefits)
- **Private return**: 1% (fund doesn't capture value)

**Rational response**: Don't fund (let someone else pay, free-ride on their research)

**This is why government funds basic research**: Can internalize social returns through tax revenue from resulting industries

**Pensions can't do this** (don't get tax revenue, only investment returns)

---

**Barrier 5: No Mechanism for Merit Capture**

**Bell Labs worked because**:
- AT&T owned the research
- Innovations stayed within AT&T (monopoly)
- **Could capture value** from inventions
- Patents protected (but eventually expire)

**Modern R&D problem**:
- Basic research can't be patented (fundamental discoveries)
- Applied research gets copied (competitors reverse-engineer)
- Academic tradition requires publication (can't keep secret)
- **Result**: Can't capture value from innovation

**Example**:
- Fund invests $500M in AI safety research
- Discovers alignment technique
- Publishes (academic norms)
- OpenAI, Google, everyone uses technique for free
- **Fund's return**: $0 (gave away the innovation)

**This is "theft of merit" MIRA describes**:
> "Theft of ideas and merit is a MAJOR reason why we have massive inefficiencies in capitalism. It is the capital that gets to own those with the merit and their produce, due to having employed them or facilitated their ability to have a life."

**But it's worse**: Even capital can't capture merit if research must be published/shared

---

**Barrier 6: No Sovereign Protection**

**Why Bell Labs could sustain 50 years**:
- AT&T monopoly protected by government (until 1984)
- Regulation REQUIRED R&D spending (as monopoly condition)
- Competitors couldn't enter market (legal protection)
- **Could afford patient capital** because no competitive threat

**Why modern companies can't replicate**:
- No monopoly protection (antitrust laws)
- Competitors will undercut if you spend on R&D
- Shareholders demand buybacks instead of R&D
- **Can't sustain Bell Labs-style research** in competitive market

**The proof**: Bell Labs collapsed after 1984 AT&T breakup
- 1970s: 25,000 employees, $2B/year R&D
- 1990s: Funding slashed, staff reduced
- 2000s: Broken up and sold
- 2024: Basically defunct (Nokia owns remnants, minimal R&D)

**Market competition killed the greatest R&D institution in history**
