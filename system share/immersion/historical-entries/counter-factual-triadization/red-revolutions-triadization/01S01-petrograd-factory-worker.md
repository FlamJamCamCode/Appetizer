# 01S01: The Factory That Chose Us

*Journal entries of Ivan Petrov, metalworker, Putilov Factory, Petrograd, October-December 1917*

---

## October 15, 1917

The smoke still stings my eyes, but it's different now. Not the old smoke—the kind that meant the foreman's whip if you slowed, the owner's profit if you worked yourself to bone. This smoke smells of possibility.

We held the assembly yesterday. Not a vote—God, not another vote where no supermajority towards a cause translate to representation and suitable action. No. This was different.

Mikhail stood on the crate, his hands shaking not from fear but from something else. Hope, maybe. Or terror of hope.

"Comrades," he said, and the word didn't sound like propaganda anymore. "They're offering us the factory. Not seizing it. Offering. A place to try collective ownership. To see if we actually want it."

The silence was thick. We'd heard promises before. The Tsar promised. The Provisional Government promised. Now the Bolsheviks promised. But this felt different.

"Who wants to try it?" Mikhail asked. "Not who votes for it. Who wants to *try* it? To live it, work it, see what it becomes?"

I raised my hand. So did half the floor. Not because we were sure. Because we were curious. Because the old way was breaking us, and maybe—just maybe—there was another way.

---

## October 18, 1917

Three days of living it. Three days of working without the owner's shadow.

The first day was chaos. Nobody knew who decided what. The foreman tried to keep his old authority, but workers just... ignored him. Not violently. Just stopped listening.

By the second day, we'd formed work circles. Groups of five, each choosing their tasks. I'm with the lathe operators—Vasily, Anna, Pavel, and me. We decide our own pace. We decide our own breaks. We decide how to divide the work.

It feels strange. Like learning to walk again after years in chains.

Anna said something today that stuck: "I keep waiting for someone to tell me I'm doing it wrong. But nobody does. We're all figuring it out together."

The owner came by this morning. Not with guards. Alone. He looked... lost. Like a man who'd forgotten his own name.

"Who's in charge here?" he asked.

Mikhail smiled. "We are. All of us. You want to stay? You can. But you work like the rest of us. No special privileges."

The owner left. I don't know where he went. Maybe to another factory where people still rally under him. Maybe not. The point is: he had a choice. We all have choices now.

---

## October 25, 1917

Word came from the city center. The Bolsheviks took the Winter Palace. But here, in our factory, it feels different. They didn't "take" it. We chose it.

I walked through the other districts today. The Menshevik factory down the street—they're trying gradual socialism. Some workers left us to join them. Some of theirs came to us. No fighting. Just... sorting.

Like finding your people. Like discovering you're not alone in wanting something different.

The capitalist factory three blocks over—they kept their owner. The workers there rallied to him. Said he treated them well, shared profits, listened. So they chose him. We chose ourselves.

This is what they mean by rally dynamics, I think. Not voting where the majority crushes the minority. But places for everyone. Even if you're the only one who wants something, there's a place for you to try it.

---

## November 2, 1917

We're discovering truth through experience. That's what Mikhail calls it.

Some things work. The work circles? They work. We're more productive now, not less. Because we're working for ourselves, not for someone else's profit.

Some things don't work. We tried to vote on everything. Every decision, every task. It was exhausting. Took three hours to decide what metal to order. So we changed it. Now each circle decides its own materials. The whole factory only votes on big things—major purchases, new hires, that kind of thing.

We're learning. Not from theory. From living it.

Vasily left yesterday. Said he missed the old way. The certainty of knowing his place. He went to the Menshevik factory. That's fine. He can come back if he wants. Or try something else. The point is: he has choices.

---

## November 15, 1917

The owner came back. Not as owner. As a worker.

"I want to try it," he said. "I want to see if I can earn my place here. Not by birthright. By work."

Mikhail looked at us. We looked at each other. Then we shrugged. Why not? Let him try. Let him discover truth through experience, same as us.

He's been here a week now. Works the lathe next to mine. He's slow. Unskilled. But he's learning. And he's not trying to take over. Just... working. Like the rest of us.

Anna says it's beautiful. The old owner, working beside us, discovering that maybe he's not so different after all. Or maybe he is different, and that's fine too. The point is: he's choosing. We're all choosing.

---

## December 1, 1917

Two months of living it. Two months of discovering what we actually want.

Some of us discovered we want this. Collective ownership. Working together. Deciding together. Building together.

Some of us discovered we don't. They left. Went to other places. Tried other models. That's fine. That's the point.

The factory is ours now. Not because we seized it. It was given to us alongside the surrounding city, as a part of the great facilitation of political disputes to have their experimential beginning. We are here, not because we built the factory; we are here because we are explorers of human organization and order. We rallied to it. We are together with it what is allowed become. An experiment.

The old owner? He's still here. Still working. Still learning. He says he's discovering something too: that maybe ownership isn't about who owns the factory. It's about who the factory chooses. Who rallies to it. Who makes it work.

We're not perfect. We make mistakes. We argue. We disagree. But we're figuring it out. Together. Through experience. Through truth-discovery.

That's what rally dynamics means, I think. Not voting. Not seizing. Not forcing. Just... trying. Living. Discovering.

---

## December 20, 1917

I walked through Petrograd today. The whole city is sorting itself. Not through violence. Through choice.

Bolshevik factories. Menshevik factories. Capitalist factories. Democratic factories. All coexisting. All trying different models. All discovering truth through experience.

Some will succeed. Some will fail. Some will adapt. That's fine. That's the point.

I met a peasant from the countryside. He said they're doing the same thing with land. Distributing estates. Trying collective farming. Trying private farming. Trying everything. Discovering what works.

"It's beautiful," he said. "For the first time in my life, I'm not being told what to do. I'm choosing. We're all choosing. Though choosing means moving to a place where that choice is being tried."

I think that's what this is about. Not communism. Not capitalism. Not any single system. But choice. The right to try. The right to discover. The right to rally to what you want, and leave what you don't.

---

*Ivan Petrov continued working at the Putilov Factory under collective ownership for the next fifteen years. He never became a party member. He never became a manager. He remained a metalworker, discovering truth through experience, one day at a time. His journals were discovered in 1952, after his death. They provide one of the most detailed first-person accounts of rally dynamics in action during the early days of the Russian Triadization.*
