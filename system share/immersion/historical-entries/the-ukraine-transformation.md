### Chapter 3.5: The Framework Proposals

**Proposal 1: No Respawn (Permanent "Death" Until War End)**

**Mechanism**:
- Soldier "killed" → Sent to island
- Stays on island for **entire war duration** (years potentially)
- Only returns when war officially ends
- Cannot re-enter combat

**Advantages**:
- Forces nations to preserve soldiers (limited manpower)
- Creates strategic depth (protect your forces)
- "Death" has real long-term consequences (soldier out for years)
- Families separated only while 'alive in war' duration (choice to co-habit with them on Isle of The 'Dead')

**Disadvantages**:
- Soldiers might be on island for years
- Limited manpower leads to stalemates faster

**Strategic math**:
- Each soldier "killed" = permanently lost until war ends
- Forces careful strategy (can't afford to waste troops)
- But: Depleted forces mean longer stalemates

---

**Proposal 2: Campaign Respawn (90-180 Day Cycles)**

**Mechanism**:
- Soldier "killed" → Sent to island for campaign duration (90-180 days)
- Returns home after campaign ends
- Can re-enter after 90-day rest period
- Can choose not to return (exit rights)

**Advantages**:
- Soldiers can leave permanently after any campaign
- Manpower renewable (soldiers can return)
- Exit rights preserved (can choose not to re-enter)

**Disadvantages**:
- Wars can continue indefinitely (renewable manpower)
- "Death" has less consequence (back in 6-12 months)
- Harder to achieve decisive outcomes

**Strategic math**:
- Each soldier "killed" = temporarily lost (6-12 months)
- Can exit after experiencing war (optimal stopping)
- Renewable manpower = longer wars but more choice

**Proposal 3: Immediate Respawn (24-48 Hours)**

**Mechanism**:
- Soldier "killed" → Sent to island for medical check (24-48 hours)
- Returns to front immediately after clearance
- Can exit anytime ("I leave the war")
- Like video game respawn but with medical oversight

**Advantages**:
- Minimal disruption (back in combat in days)
- Soldiers can test war without long commitment
- Maximum exit flexibility (leave after any "death")
- Most like Steppe Games model

**Disadvantages**:
- "Death" almost meaningless (back immediately)
- Wars become endless (no manpower depletion)
- Less strategic depth (casualties don't matter)
- Possible desensitization to "death"

**Strategic math**:
- Each "death" = 2-day delay only
- Optimal stopping: Can leave after first "death" if war not appealing
- Infinite manpower = wars never end through attrition

### Chapter 3.8: The Optimal Stopping Dynamics

**The mathematics of exit timing**:

**Expected value calculation**:
- **E(continue)** = P(survive campaign) × E(bonus if survive) - P(die) × E(loss if die)
- **E(exit now)** = Current bonuses kept + civilian life value

**Optimal strategy**: Exit when E(exit) > E(continue)

**Variables affecting decision**:
- Current bonus accumulated (higher = more to lose)
- Skill level (higher = better survival probability)
- War intensity (higher = lower survival probability)
- Civilian alternatives (better = higher exit value)
- Personal risk tolerance (higher = more likely to continue)

**The hot hand problem**:
- After killing 10 enemies without dying: "I'm hot, should I continue?"
- Gambler's fallacy: "I'm due to get 'killed' soon"
- Hot hand belief: "I'm skilled, I'll keep winning"
- Optimal stopping: Depends on actual skill vs luck

**The result**: 

**Skilled soldiers**: Stayed longer (could maintain streaks)
**Lucky soldiers**: Exited fast (before luck ran out)
**Risk-tolerant**: Stayed despite danger (chased bonuses)
**Risk-averse**: Exited early (kept small bonuses)

**Natural selection**: Wars staffed by skilled and risk-tolerant soldiers who WANTED to be there.
