### B. Insect Protein Substitution - Dogs

**Dog nutrition requirements**:
- Protein: 18-25% of diet (by weight)
- Can digest: Meat, grains, vegetables, insects
- Palatability: Dogs eat almost anything (less picky than cats)

**Insect protein viability**:
- Black soldier fly larvae (BSFL): 40-45% protein (dry weight)
- Nutritionally complete for dogs (all essential amino acids)
- **Already commercialized**: Several brands sell insect-based dog food (Yora, Jiminy's, etc.)

**Conversion**:
- 60 million tons dog meat/year → 60 million tons insect protein/year (1:1 substitution for dry weight)
- BSFL production: 1 kg input (waste) → 0.2 kg larvae (dry)
- **Need**: 300 million tons organic waste/year to produce 60 million tons BSFL

**Is this feasible?**
- Global food waste: ~1.3 billion tons/year (FAO estimate)
- We only need: 300 million tons (23% of food waste)
- **Yes, easily feasible.**

**Land/infrastructure**:
- BSFL farming: ~10-20 kg larvae/m²/year (intensive indoor farming)
- Need: 3-6 billion m² = **3,000-6,000 km²** of BSFL farms
- Compare to: Current livestock land = 45 million km² (BSFL is 0.01% of that)

**Feasibility**: ✓✓✓ **Highly feasible.** Dogs can be fed entirely on insect protein with minimal land/resource use.

### B. Hierarchy Alternation Strategy - "Every Other" Separation

**Your proposal**: Separate predators and prey, alternate reintroduction with new food sources.

**Method** (for one ecosystem example - African savanna):

**Step 1 - Initial separation**:
- Create Zone A: Herbivores only (zebra, wildebeest, antelope, buffalo)
- Create Zone B: Carnivores only (lions, hyenas, leopards)
- Physical barrier initially (fences, natural boundaries like rivers)
- Size: Zone A = 10,000 km², Zone B = 1,000 km² (carnivores need less space if fed)

**Step 2 - Feed carnivores alternative sources**:
- Zone B: Deliver carcasses from natural deaths (Zone A herbivores dying of old age)
- Supplement with insect protein (BSFL processed into "prey-shaped" forms)
- Gradually shift ratio: 100% natural carcasses → 80/20 → 50/50 → 20/80 → 100% insect

**Step 3 - Accustom herbivores to abundance**:
- Zone A: Plant engineered super-grasses (higher nutrition per bite)
- Remove predation pressure: Population grows initially
- Monitor carrying capacity: Adjust grass planting to support population
- Population stabilizes: Births ≈ natural deaths (old age, disease)

**Step 4 - Reintroduce with new behaviors**:
- After X time (measured in generations of both species):
  - Lions conditioned: Food comes from delivered sources, not hunting
  - Zebras conditioned: No predation fear (haven't been hunted for generations)
- Attempt reintroduction: Lower fences, allow mixing
- **Hypothesis**: Lions don't hunt zebras (no longer associate them with food)
- **Monitor**: If hunting resumes, re-separate and continue conditioning

**Quantified requirements for one ecosystem (10,000 km² savanna)**:

**Herbivore population** (Zone A):
- Carrying capacity: ~50,000 large herbivores (5/km² average)
- Natural deaths: ~5,000/year (10% annual mortality from old age/disease/accidents)
- Meat available: ~2,500 tons/year (500 kg average carcass weight)

**Carnivore population** (Zone B):
- Lions: ~200 (manageable pride size for 1,000 km²)
- Food needs: ~5 kg meat/lion/day × 200 × 365 = **365 tons/year**
- Natural deaths provide: 2,500 tons/year (7x more than needed!)
- **Surplus**: Can feed carnivores in other zones, or reduce herbivore area

**Insect supplement** (for taste conditioning):
- Start: 0% insects, 100% natural carcasses
- Gradually shift: 10% insects/year increase
- By 10th cycle: 100% insects (if conditioning successful)
- Insect needed (if 100%): 365 tons/year
- BSFL production: ~1,825 tons organic waste needed (manageable)

**Feasibility check**:
✓ Enough space (10,000 km² is small—consider Zone A could be 100,000 km² for more herbivores)
✓ Enough food (natural deaths exceed carnivore needs)
✓ Infrastructure (fences, monitoring, delivery systems—engineering challenge but feasible)
✗ **Behavioral uncertainty**: Will conditioning work? Unknown. Requires experimentation.

### B. Individualized Food Supply via Massive Parallel Breeding

**Your vision**: Each person gets food optimized for their "truth and taste."

**Mechanism**:

**Step 1 - Catalog human taste/nutrition variation**:
- Genetic testing: Taste receptor genes (bitter, sweet, umami, fat sensitivity)
- Metabolic profiling: How efficiently person processes certain nutrients
- Preference surveys: Self-reported likes/dislikes
- **Result**: Database of 8 billion individual profiles

**Step 2 - Parallel plant/mushroom breeding**:
- Start with base varieties: 100 mushroom strains, 100 plant varieties (tomato, lettuce, wheat, etc.)
- For each variety: Measure nutritional profile, flavor compounds, growth characteristics
- **Goal**: Create library of 10,000-100,000 distinct varieties (covering all possible taste/nutrition combinations)

**Step 3 - Match individual to optimal variety**:
- Algorithm: Input person's profile → Output top 10 varieties matched to them
- Person orders food → System automatically selects variety matched to their profile
- Continuous feedback: Rate each meal → Algorithm improves recommendations

**Step 4 - Natural selection pressure**:
- Varieties that are ordered more often → Planted more often (more seeds saved, propagated)
- Varieties that are ordered less → Reduced planting (but kept in seed bank for future)
- **Result**: Cultivar diversity maintained, but production skewed toward popular varieties

**Quantified scale**:

**Breeding program**:
- 100 base varieties × 100 generations of selection = 10,000+ distinct cultivars
- Generation time: 1-3 years (plants), 0.5-1 year (mushrooms)
- **Duration to create library**: 5-10 years (if parallel breeding, many simultaneous lines)

**Seed/spore production**:
- Need: Seeds for 8-10M km² of desert farms
- Planting density: ~10-50 plants/m² (depending on crop)
- **Total plants**: 80-500 trillion plants/year (considering continuous replanting)

**Can we produce this many seeds?**
- One plant produces: 100-10,000 seeds (depending on species)
- Seed production farms: 0.1-1% of total area dedicated to seed/spore production
- **Feasible**: Yes, standard practice in agriculture (seed industry already exists at massive scale)

### C. Sun Energy Accumulation - Quantifying Solar Capture

**Your point**: Solar energy is "truly green" (unlike biomass burning or fossil fuels).

**Solar irradiance in deserts**:
- Average: 6-7 kWh/m²/day (top-tier deserts like Sahara, Atacama)
- Annual: 2,200-2,500 kWh/m²/year

**Photosynthesis efficiency**:
- Typical plants: 1-2% solar-to-biomass conversion (very inefficient!)
- Optimized crops (sugarcane, algae): 3-6% under ideal conditions

**If we use 8 million km² desert with 2% efficiency**:
- Solar input: 8M km² × 2,400 kWh/m²/year = 19.2 trillion kWh/year of solar energy hitting land
- Captured by plants: 2% × 19.2T = **384 billion kWh/year = 1.38 × 10^18 joules/year**
- **In food terms**: 1.38 × 10^18 J ÷ 4,184 J/kcal = **330 trillion kcal/year**

**Human needs**: 7.3 quadrillion kcal/year (from Section II.A)

**Compare**:
- Desert agriculture produces: 330T kcal/year
- Humans need: 7,300T kcal/year
- **Gap**: We're 22x short!

**What went wrong?** Photosynthesis efficiency is terrible (1-2%). Most solar energy becomes heat, not food.

**Solutions**:

**1. Increase efficiency via breeding**:
- Select for faster growth, better light capture
- Genetic engineering: Optimize photosystem II, reduce photorespiration
- **Potential**: Increase efficiency from 2% to 4-6% (achievable in labs, not yet in fields)
- **New capture**: 660-990T kcal/year (closer, but still 7-11x short)

**2. Use less land area more intensively**:
- Vertical farming (stack crops in layers)
- Each layer captures some light, 5-10 layers possible indoors with artificial light

**Key insight**: "Lush green" = rapid r-selected species growth (weeds, insects breed fast, overwhelm). We want **K-selected species growth** (large animals, slow breeders, stable populations).

### B. Natural Selection Systems - How to Manage Without Killing

**Your specification**: "Natural selection and population control for higher life monotonely increasing or only natural deaths. No deliberate culling."

**Mechanisms**:

**1. Food availability control**:
- Plant engineered super-forage in specific areas (high nutrition, prefer large herbivores over small)
- Engineered plants resistant to rodents/insects (but palatable to deer, elephants, etc.)
- **Result**: Large herbivores thrive, small pests don't benefit as much

**2. Breeding rate modulation**:
- For animals we want more of: Provide abundant food during breeding season (increases fertility, survival of young)
- For animals we want stable: Provide maintenance food only (no excess → fewer births)
- **No killing**, just fertility control via environment

**3. Contraception for excess populations**:
- If population grows beyond carrying capacity: Use contraceptive baiting (PZP vaccine for herbivores, GonaCon for various species)
- Delivered via food (like medicating livestock, but wild animals eat bait)
- **Temporary**: Once population decreases, stop contraception

**4. Spatial separation (already discussed)**:
- Higher-order carnivores in separate zones (fed by delivery, not hunting)
- Herbivores in protected zones (no predation, can focus energy on growth not fear)

**5. Storage of "excess" life**:
- If we over-breed some species (say, too many elephants for habitat X):
  - Don't kill, **relocate** to temporary holding areas
  - Feed with desert-agriculture produce (abundant, cheap)
  - Later: Reintroduce when habitat space available (other animals die naturally)

**Your specification**: "Worst case of having allowed too much breeding is to 'store them away' in non-habitats with desert-agriculture food supply until 'they are needed again'."

**Feasibility**: 
- Holding areas: Require space (but less than natural habitat—can be denser if well-fed)
- Food cost: Covered by desert agriculture surplus (we have 8x overcapacity in my estimate)
- Ethics: Animals live well (fed, cared for), just not in "natural" habitat (but better than killing)
