## FRANTZ FANON: COLONIAL VIOLENCE AND THE MANICHEAN WORLD

### The Colonized and the Colonizer as Absolute Enemies

**Key Works:**
- *Black Skin, White Masks* (1952)
- *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961)
- *A Dying Colonialism* (1959)

**Core Thesis:**

Frantz Fanon (1925-1961) analyzes colonialism as creating an **absolute friend-enemy distinction** structured by race. The colonial world is divided into two zones—colonizer and colonized—with no middle ground. Decolonization requires counter-violence because the colonial relationship is founded on violence and cannot be transformed peacefully.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **The Manichean World**:
   - Colonial society divided into two camps: Black/White, colonizer/colonized
   - No shades of gray, no shared humanity
   - **Spatially separated**: European city vs. native quarter ("casbah")
   - Absolute friend-enemy: Clean/dirty, civilized/savage, human/subhuman

2. **Violence as Foundation**:
   - Colonialism established and maintained through violence
   - Police and military enforce racial boundaries
   - Violence is the "natural state" of colonial relations
   - **Enemy relationship is structural and permanent**

3. **Psychological Alienation**:
   - Colonized internalize inferiority (wear "white masks")
   - Desire to be white, to be colonizer
   - Self-hatred, horizontal violence (colonized fight each other)
   - **Internal enemy**: The colonized consciousness itself

4. **Decolonization as Absolute Violence**:
   - Cannot negotiate with colonialism (it doesn't recognize you as human)
   - Liberation requires "cleansing violence"
   - Violence psychologically liberates the colonized
   - "The last shall be first" - complete reversal of colonial order

5. **National Consciousness**:
   - Anti-colonial struggle creates new collective identity
   - Unity of colonized against colonizer
   - **Friend-enemy unifies disparate groups** (tribes, religions) against common enemy
   - But risk: New nation reproduces colonial structures

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Racialized Absolute Enmity**:
   - Not political disagreement but **ontological opposition**
   - Colonizer sees colonized as non-human (thing, animal)
   - Colonized must kill the colonizer (literally or symbolically) to become human
   - **No reconciliation possible within colonial structure**

2. **Spatial Segregation**:
   - Colonial city vs. native quarter
   - Borders policed violently
   - Friend-enemy inscribed in geography
   - **Apartheid as spatial friend-enemy**

3. **Violence as Humanizing**:
   - Fanon's controversial claim: Violence liberates the colonized psychologically
   - Killing colonizer = asserting humanity
   - Violence creates revolutionary consciousness
   - **Counter-violence transforms enemy relationship**

4. **The National Bourgeoisie as False Friends**:
   - Post-colonial elite replicate colonial structures
   - Become enemies of their own people
   - "Independence" doesn't end enmity, just changes who is enemy
   - **Friend-enemy persists after formal decolonization**

5. **Pan-African Solidarity**:
   - All colonized peoples are friends (united against colonialism)
   - Enemy is not just French/British but **colonialism as system**
   - International friend-enemy: Global South vs. Imperial North

**Examples:**

**Algerian War of Independence:**
- Manichean division: French settlers vs. Algerian natives
- FLN (National Liberation Front) used violence strategically
- French responded with torture, collective punishment
- Fanon: This violence was necessary and liberating
- Friend-enemy: Complete, no middle ground

**Apartheid South Africa:**
- Spatial Manichean division (townships vs. white suburbs)
- Violence maintained by State
- ANC struggled between non-violence (Mandela early) and violence (MK)
- Friend-enemy: Absolute racial division

**Palestine:**
- Colonial settler situation (Fanon's framework applies)
- Spatial division (settlements, walls, checkpoints)
- Violence maintains separation
- Friend-enemy: Absolute, no recognized middle ground

**Black Lives Matter:**
- Fanon's analysis applies to anti-Black racism
- Police violence as colonial violence
- "Defund the police" as challenging colonial State apparatus
- Friend-enemy: Black communities vs. police State

**Fanon vs. Gandhi:**

- **Gandhi**: Non-violence can shame oppressor into change
- **Fanon**: Oppressor sees colonized as non-human, cannot be shamed
- **Gandhi**: Moral appeals work
- **Fanon**: Only violence the oppressor understands
- **Gandhi**: Reconciliation possible
- **Fanon**: Liberation requires destroying colonial relationship entirely

**Fanon vs. Said:**

- **Said**: Orientalism is discourse (knowledge production)
- **Fanon**: Colonialism is violence (physical domination)
- **Said**: Enemy constructed through representation
- **Fanon**: Enemy relationship is material, immediate, violent
- Both: Colonial friend-enemy is absolute

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Fanon reveals colonialism as creating the **most absolute form of friend-enemy**—a Manichean world with no middle ground, no recognition of shared humanity, no possibility of reconciliation within the colonial structure. The enemy (colonizer) establishes and maintains the relationship through violence, and only counter-violence can break it. This is friend-enemy pushed to its existential extreme: to be human, one must kill (literally or symbolically) the colonizer.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **anti-colonial framework** showing friend-enemy as **racialized violence**. Explains why negotiation fails (enemy doesn't recognize you as human), why violence emerges (only language oppressor understands), why post-colonial nations struggle (new elites become enemies). Essential for understanding Israel/Palestine, anti-Black racism, indigenous struggles, and all colonial/settler-colonial situations.

## SLAVOJ ŽIŽEK: IDEOLOGY, SYMBOLIC ORDER, AND THE VIOLENCE OF UNIVERSALITY

### The Enemy as Symptom of the System

**Key Works:**
- *Violence: Six Sideways Reflections* (2008)
- *The Sublime Object of Ideology* (1989)
- *Welcome to the Desert of the Real* (2002)

**Core Thesis:**

Slavoj Žižek (1949-) argues that we must distinguish between **subjective violence** (visible: crime, terror, war) and **objective violence** (invisible: systemic, symbolic). Friend-enemy distinctions typically focus on subjective violence (terrorists, criminals) while obscuring the objective violence (capitalism, ideology) that produces these enemies. The real enemy is the system itself, but ideology prevents us from recognizing this.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Three Types of Violence**:
   - **Subjective violence**: Visible, spectacular (terrorism, riots, murder)
   - **Symbolic violence**: Language, culture imposing meanings
   - **Systemic violence**: Structural violence of capitalism, "normal" functioning of system
   - **Most harm comes from systemic violence**, but we only see subjective

2. **Ideology and Fantasy**:
   - Ideology structures reality itself (not just "false consciousness")
   - Fantasy makes reality bearable
   - **Enemy is fantasy figure** allowing us to avoid confronting systemic violence
   - "They stole our enjoyment" (enemy as explanation for our dissatisfaction)

3. **The Neighbor as Enemy**:
   - Following Lacan: The neighbor is the most foreign, threatening "thing"
   - Multiculturalism tries to neutralize this (enemy as exotic "Other")
   - But real enemy is **the too-close neighbor** who threatens our way of life
   - **Friend-enemy is about proximity, not distance**

4. **Divine Violence (following Benjamin)**:
   - Revolutionary violence that destroys existing legal order
   - Not instrumental (for this or that goal) but **pure means**
   - Žižek controversial: Sees potential in revolutionary terror
   - **Only divine violence breaks ideological friend-enemy**

5. **Tolerance as Ideology**:
   - Liberal tolerance masks systemic violence
   - "Respect for difference" depoliticizes real antagonisms
   - Makes enemies into diverse "partners"
   - **Friendship (tolerance) as ideology hiding real enmity (class)**

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Displaced Enmity**:
   - Real enemy (capitalist system) displaced onto scapegoats (immigrants, terrorists)
   - Ideology creates false enemies to hide true enemy
   - **Subjective violence distracts from objective violence**
   - Example: Blame immigrants for unemployment caused by capitalism

2. **The Symptom as Enemy**:
   - Society designates certain groups as "symptoms" (what's wrong with society)
   - Jews, immigrants, terrorists = symptoms
   - Attacking symptom doesn't solve problem (created by system)
   - **Enemy is symptom of systemic contradiction**

3. **Liberal Multiculturalism as False Friendship**:
   - Pretends conflict is cultural misunderstanding
   - "If we just learn about each other..."
   - Hides real conflicts (class, exploitation)
   - **Tolerance ideology obscures real friend-enemy**

4. **The Neighbor-Thing**:
   - Real enemy is not exotic foreigner but **too-close neighbor**
   - The one who shares your space but has different "way of enjoying"
   - Example: Not Muslims in Middle East but Muslim neighbor in Europe
   - **Proximity creates enmity**

5. **Revolutionary Violence vs. Humanitarian Intervention**:
   - Humanitarian interventions preserve systemic violence (capitalism)
   - Revolutionary violence challenges system itself
   - **Choosing right enemy**: System, not its symptoms

**Examples:**

**War on Terror:**
- Žižek's analysis: Terrorists as symptom
- Real violence: Capitalist globalization destroying traditional societies
- Terrorism is response (symptom) to systemic violence
- But ideology makes us see only terrorists (subjective violence)
- Friend-enemy: False (us vs. terrorists) hides real (capitalism vs. humanity)

**Immigration Panic:**
- Immigrants blamed for economic insecurity
- Real cause: Neoliberal capitalism destroying welfare state
- Immigrant as symptom-enemy
- Friend-enemy: False (natives vs. immigrants) hides real (capital vs. labor)

**Political Correctness:**
- "SJWs" as enemy of "free speech"
- Displaced from real power (corporate control of speech)
- Symptom-enemy (annoying activist) hides system-enemy (capitalist censorship)
- Friend-enemy: Cultural (PC vs. free speech) obscures material (class)

**COVID-19:**
- Anti-vaxxers as enemy vs. Pro-vaxxers
- Displaces from systemic issues (privatized healthcare, inequality)
- Subjective violence (anti-vax protests) distracts from objective (deaths of despair)
- Friend-enemy: False (masked vs. unmasked) hides real (capital vs. public health)

**Žižek vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy is the political essence (accept it)
- **Žižek**: Ideology creates false friend-enemy (critique it)
- **Schmitt**: Sovereign decides who is enemy
- **Žižek**: Capital structures who appears as enemy (symptom)

**Žižek vs. Foucault:**

- **Foucault**: Power is productive, everywhere, no center
- **Žižek**: Power has center (capitalism), ideology obscures this
- **Foucault**: Multiple resistances
- **Žižek**: Need revolutionary rupture (divine violence)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Žižek reveals that **most friend-enemy distinctions are ideological displacements**—false enemies (scapegoats, symptoms) that allow us to avoid confronting the real enemy (systemic violence of capitalism). We focus on subjective violence (terrorism, crime) while ignoring objective violence (poverty, exploitation, structural death). The immigrant, terrorist, or cultural enemy is a fantasy figure that makes the system's violence bearable by giving us someone to blame.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **ideological critique** of friend-enemy distinctions. Explains why wrong enemies are selected (immigrants not capital, terrorists not imperialism, "PC culture" not corporate power). Shows how liberalism's "tolerance" obscures real conflicts. Argues we must identify correct enemy (system) not symptoms (scapegoats). Essential for cutting through ideological fog to see actual power relations.

## EMMANUEL LEVINAS: ETHICS AND THE FACE OF THE OTHER

### The Other as Infinite Responsibility, Not Enemy

**Key Works:**
- *Totality and Infinity* (1961)
- *Otherwise Than Being* (1974)
- "Ethics as First Philosophy" (1984)

**Core Thesis:**

Emmanuel Levinas (1906-1995) provides a radical ethical alternative to friend-enemy thinking. The encounter with **the Face of the Other** creates infinite ethical responsibility that precedes and exceeds political friend-enemy distinctions. To recognize someone's face is to recognize the commandment "Thou shalt not kill"—making enmity a profound ethical violation.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **The Face (Le Visage)**:
   - Not physical face but ethical encounter with Other's vulnerability
   - Face "speaks": "Thou shalt not kill"
   - Face creates infinite, asymmetrical responsibility
   - **Precedes friend-enemy: Ethics before politics**

2. **Alterity (Otherness)**:
   - The Other is absolutely other (cannot be reduced to Same)
   - Enemy-making = reducing Other to category (terrorist, criminal)
   - Ethical relation preserves alterity
   - **Friend-enemy violates alterity** (makes Other into type)

3. **Asymmetrical Responsibility**:
   - I am responsible for Other, but Other not responsible for me (not reciprocal)
   - Responsibility is infinite (never discharged)
   - Cannot say "I've done enough" or "they're enemy so no responsibility"
   - **Responsibility precedes friend-enemy** (even for enemy's suffering)

4. **Totality vs. Infinity**:
   - **Totality**: System that encompasses all (State, ideology, politics)
   - **Infinity**: Ethical relation that exceeds all systems
   - Totality creates friend-enemy (everyone assigned category)
   - Infinity challenges this (each person exceeds category)

5. **Violence as Failure**:
   - Violence = treating Other as object (not face)
   - War = total negation of ethical relation
   - **Friend-enemy is ethical failure** (denying face of enemy)

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Ethics Precedes Politics**:
   - Before friend-enemy (political), there is face-to-face (ethical)
   - Political designation of enemy is **secondary violence**
   - Must maintain ethical responsibility even toward enemy
   - **Politics must be limited by ethics**

2. **Face Challenges Enemy Designation**:
   - To truly see enemy's face = recognize their humanity
   - This makes killing them problematic
   - Propaganda must de-face enemy (make them faceless)
   - **Enmity requires de-facing** (helmets, masks, distance, statistics)

3. **Hospitality to the Stranger**:
   - Stranger (potential enemy) makes ethical demand
   - Must be welcomed before being categorized
   - Friend-enemy comes after hospitality
   - **Radical hospitality precedes friend-enemy**

4. **Asymmetry Challenges Reciprocity**:
   - Friend-enemy assumes reciprocity (if they're my enemy, I'm their enemy)
   - Levinas: My responsibility doesn't depend on reciprocity
   - Even if they're my enemy, I'm responsible for them
   - **Breaks symmetry of friend-enemy**

5. **The Third Party**:
   - Face-to-face is absolute, but there are others (third party)
   - Politics becomes necessary (justice among multiple others)
   - Friend-enemy enters here (limited politics)
   - **But always constrained by ethics** (never total war)

**Examples:**

**Soldiers and "Rules of Engagement":**
- Levinas explains why soldiers troubled by killing
- Face of enemy soldier makes moral demand
- Dehumanization necessary to overcome this (propaganda, training)
- Friend-enemy: Ideology vs. ethical encounter

**Palestinian-Israeli Conflict:**
- Both sides must see each other's faces
- Each makes ethical demand (responsibility)
- Politics (friend-enemy) necessary but limited by ethics
- Levinas (Jewish but critical): Cannot ignore Palestinian faces

**Criminal Justice:**
- "Criminal" = faceless category
- Restorative justice: Face-to-face between victim and offender
- Recognizing face changes relationship
- Friend-enemy: Citizen vs. criminal challenged by face

**Refugee Crisis:**
- Refugees present their faces at border
- Ethical: Must welcome (infinite responsibility)
- Political: Friend-enemy (citizen vs. foreigner)
- Tension between ethics and politics

**Levinas vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Political (friend-enemy) is autonomous, primary
- **Levinas**: Ethical (face-to-face) precedes and limits political
- **Schmitt**: Enemy is existential threat (intensity)
- **Levinas**: Enemy still makes ethical demand (face)
- **Schmitt**: Sovereign decides exception
- **Levinas**: Ethics has no exception (always responsible)

**Levinas vs. Hobbes:**

- **Hobbes**: State of nature = war of all against all (everyone enemy)
- **Levinas**: Original state = ethical responsibility (everyone Other)
- **Hobbes**: Politics creates friendship (through Sovereign)
- **Levinas**: Ethics precedes politics (face before friend-enemy)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Levinas provides **ethical critique** of friend-enemy distinction. The face of the Other creates infinite responsibility that precedes political categories. To designate someone as enemy requires **de-facing** them—making them faceless, reducing them to category, treating them as object. But the ethical relation persists beneath politics, constantly challenging friend-enemy designations. This explains moral discomfort with killing, need for dehumanization propaganda, and limits that should constrain enmity.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **ethical foundation** for questioning friend-enemy. Explains why killing feels wrong even in "just" wars (face makes demand), why propaganda must dehumanize enemies (eliminate face), why some refuse military service (ethical exceeds political). Offers normative vision: Politics necessary but must be constrained by ethics. Enemy remains Other for whom I'm responsible, even as enemy.

## MICHAEL HARDT & ANTONIO NEGRI: EMPIRE, MULTITUDE, AND THE NEW ENEMY

### Post-Modern Sovereignty and the Deterritorialized Enemy

**Key Works:**
- *Empire* (2000)
- *Multitude: War and Democracy in the Age of Empire* (2004)
- *Commonwealth* (2009)

**Core Thesis:**

Michael Hardt (1960-) and Antonio Negri (1933-2023) argue that sovereignty has transformed from nation-states to a deterritorialized "Empire"—a diffuse network of power without center. This creates new forms of friend-enemy: Empire vs. Multitude. The enemy is no longer another nation but **resistance itself** (terrorism, migration, insurgency), while friends are integrated into global capital flows.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Empire (Not Imperialism)**:
   - Not US imperialism but **global network of power**
   - No territorial center (everywhere and nowhere)
   - Includes: Capital, international institutions, NGOs, media, tech
   - **Friend-enemy becomes global and diffuse**

2. **Biopower and Control**:
   - Empire governs through biopower (managing life itself)
   - Not disciplinary (Foucault) but **control societies** (Deleuze)
   - Modulation (constant adjustment) not confinement
   - **Friends modulated, enemies excluded**

3. **The Multitude**:
   - Not "the people" (unified) but **multiplicity that remains multiple**
   - Source of resistance to Empire
   - Productive power (generates wealth, culture, life)
   - **Potential friend-to-itself against Empire-enemy**

4. **The New Enemy**:
   - Empire's enemy: Anyone refusing integration
   - **Terrorist, migrant, insurgent** = figures of non-integration
   - Not nation-states fighting nation-states
   - **Global police action against disorder**

5. **Hybrid Warfare**:
   - No more wars between states (Empire is singular)
   - Only **police actions** and **insurgencies**
   - Enemy is everywhere and nowhere (diffuse threat)
   - **Permanent war against non-state enemies**

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Deterritorialized Enmity**:
   - No geographic enemy (no "other nation")
   - Enemy is **the excluded** (those outside capital flows)
   - Friends are **the integrated** (part of global networks)
   - **Network vs. exclusion** replaces nation vs. nation

2. **Empire Creates Its Enemies**:
   - Empire's expansion produces resistance (terrorism, migration)
   - These resistances then justify Empire's police power
   - **Circular**: Empire creates enemies to legitimize itself

3. **Absolute Enemy**:
   - Empire claims universal values (democracy, human rights)
   - Those who resist are **absolute evil** (terrorists, tyrants)
   - No legitimate opposition (only good vs. evil)
   - **Moral friend-enemy** (not political as Schmitt)

4. **Just War Doctrine**:
   - Empire always fights "just wars" (humanitarian intervention)
   - Enemy is always unjust (rogue state, terrorist)
   - **Friend-enemy moralized** (not Schmittian political equality)

5. **Multitude as Counter-Power**:
   - Multitude must recognize Empire as common enemy
   - But remain multiple (not unified "people")
   - **Democratic friend-enemy**: Common enemy, but diverse friends

**Examples:**

**War on Terror:**
- Perfect example of Empire's enemy
- Not war against nation but diffuse network (Al-Qaeda, ISIS)
- Everywhere and nowhere (Afghanistan, Iraq, Yemen, Somalia, etc.)
- Justifies permanent state of war
- Friend-enemy: Empire (integrated world) vs. terrorists (excluded)

**Migration:**
- Migrants refuse to stay in place (challenge territorial order)
- Empire's answer: Walls, detention, deportation
- But migrants already integrated economically (labor)
- Friend-enemy: Citizens (legal) vs. migrants (illegal) but both necessary

**Humanitarian Intervention:**
- Empire intervenes globally for "human rights"
- Enemy: "Rogue states," "failed states," "dictators"
- No political legitimacy recognized (only moral judgment)
- Friend-enemy: Democratic Empire vs. authoritarian outlaws

**COVID-19:**
- Virus as enemy requiring global management
- Vaccine apartheid: Integrated countries (friends) get vaccines
- Excluded countries (enemies/irrelevant) don't
- Friend-enemy through biopolitical integration

**Hardt & Negri vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy between sovereign states
- **H&N**: Empire is singular, no equal enemies (only police targets)
- **Schmitt**: Political friend-enemy
- **H&N**: Moral friend-enemy (good vs. evil)
- **Schmitt**: Respect for enemy
- **H&N**: Empire criminalizes enemy

**Hardt & Negri vs. Huntington:**

- **Huntington**: Clash of civilizations (multiple centers)
- **H&N**: Empire singular (one center, diffuse)
- **Huntington**: Cultural enemies
- **H&N**: Integration vs. exclusion

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Hardt & Negri reveal **post-national friend-enemy**: Empire (global network of capital and biopower) vs. those who refuse integration (terrorists, migrants, insurgents). The enemy is no longer another nation-state but **exclusion from networks** or **resistance to integration**. Wars become police actions, enemies become criminals, and friend-enemy is moralized (Empire = good, resistance = evil). This explains permanent war, humanitarian interventions, and migration crises.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **post-national framework** for 21st-century friend-enemy. Explains War on Terror (not war but global police action), migration crises (movement challenges territorial friend-enemy), humanitarian interventions (Empire moralizes enmity). Shows how friend-enemy shifted from state vs. state to integration vs. exclusion, network vs. outside.

## GAYATRI CHAKRAVORTY SPIVAK: THE SUBALTERN AND EPISTEMIC VIOLENCE

### Can the Enemy Speak? The Silencing of the Other

**Key Works:**
- "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988)
- *A Critique of Postcolonial Reason* (1999)
- *An Aesthetic Education in the Era of Globalization* (2012)

**Core Thesis:**

Gayatri Spivak (1942-) argues that **epistemic violence**—the violence of knowledge production—silences the subaltern (the most marginalized). The subaltern cannot speak because the dominant knowledge systems (Western, male, elite) do not provide categories for their voice. Friend-enemy operates through who gets to speak (friends) and who is spoken for or silenced (enemies/subaltern).

**Key Concepts:**

1. **The Subaltern**:
   - From Gramsci: Subordinated groups
   - Spivak: **Most marginalized** (poor, colonized, women, especially poor colonized women)
   - Not just oppressed but **lacking voice** in dominant discourse
   - Cannot represent themselves, must be represented

2. **Epistemic Violence**:
   - Violence of knowledge systems silencing certain voices
   - Western knowledge as **universal** (epistemicide of other ways of knowing)
   - Colonial education destroyed indigenous knowledge
   - **Friend-enemy through epistemology**: Whose knowledge counts?

3. **Strategic Essentialism**:
   - Oppressed groups sometimes must claim identity (essentialize) for political purposes
   - But recognize this is strategic, not essence
   - Friend-enemy: Must create "us" (temporarily) to fight "them"
   - **Tactical friend-enemy**

4. **Double Bind of Representation**:
   - Subaltern needs representation (cannot speak)
   - But representation by elites **silences** subaltern voice
   - Postcolonial intellectuals speak "for" subaltern but betray them
   - **Friend (representative) becomes enemy** (silencer)

5. **Sanctioned Ignorance**:
   - Dominant groups actively ignore/erase subaltern knowledge
   - Not just absence but **production of ignorance**
   - Friend-enemy: Whose ignorance is preserved (dominant) vs. whose knowledge erased (subaltern)

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Epistemic Friend-Enemy**:
   - Friends: Those whose knowledge is legitimated (Western, male, elite)
   - Enemies: Those whose knowledge is erased (colonized, female, subaltern)
   - **Epistemicide as enmity** (destroying ways of knowing)

2. **Who Can Speak?**:
   - Friend-enemy through speech itself
   - Some voices heard as legitimate
   - Others don't register as "speech" (just noise, emotion, irrationality)
   - **Subaltern as enemy because cannot speak**

3. **Representation as Violence**:
   - Elite speaking "for" subaltern = epistemic violence
   - Postcolonial nationalism speaks for peasants but silences them
   - Feminism speaks for "third world women" but exoticizes them
   - **Friends (representatives) are enemies (silencers)**

4. **Strategic Essentialism's Risk**:
   - Creating "we" (friends) vs. "they" (enemies) strategically
   - But risk: Essentialized identity becomes fixed
   - **Tactical friend-enemy becomes permanent**

5. **Listening as Ethics**:
   - Must learn to listen to subaltern (not just speak for them)
   - Requires **unlearning privilege**
   - Friend-enemy: Those who listen vs. those who only speak

**Examples:**

**Sati (Widow Burning):**
- British colonizers: "White men saving brown women from brown men"
- Indian nationalists: "Women voluntarily choosing tradition"
- Both speak **for** widow, neither listen to her
- Subaltern woman: Cannot speak in either discourse
- Friend-enemy: Both "friends" silence the subaltern woman

**Development Discourse:**
- Global North "develops" Global South
- Indigenous knowledge dismissed as "backward"
- Local voices silenced by "experts"
- Friend-enemy: Developers (friends who help) vs. developed (enemies of progress if resist)

**Climate Justice:**
- Indigenous peoples have climate knowledge
- But excluded from COP negotiations
- Western science is only "legitimate" knowledge
- Friend-enemy: Scientific knowledge (friends) vs. indigenous knowledge (irrelevant)

**Academic Feminism:**
- Western feminists speak about "third world women"
- Represent them as victims needing saving
- Don't listen to their actual voices
- Friend-enemy: Feminist saviors (friends) silence those they claim to help

**Spivak vs. Said:**

- **Said**: Orientalism produces knowledge about the East
- **Spivak**: This knowledge production **silences** the Eastern subject entirely
- **Said**: Representation as misrepresentation
- **Spivak**: Representation as silencing

**Spivak vs. Fanon:**

- **Fanon**: Colonized can speak through violence
- **Spivak**: Subaltern (especially women) cannot even speak through violence
- **Fanon**: Revolutionary subject can emerge
- **Spivak**: Subaltern remains silent even in revolution

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Spivak reveals **epistemic friend-enemy**—friend-enemy operating through whose voice counts as legitimate knowledge. The enemy is not just politically opposed but **epistemically silenced**—their knowledge systems destroyed, their voices unheard even when they speak. This is most extreme for the subaltern (poor, colonized, female) who cannot speak in dominant discourses and are spoken for by elite "friends" who silence them.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **epistemological dimension** to friend-enemy. Explains how knowledge systems themselves create enemies (those whose knowledge doesn't count), how representation silences (speaking "for" subaltern), how Western knowledge is epistemic violence (destroying other ways of knowing). Essential for understanding how marginalized voices are excluded not just politically but **epistemically**—they literally cannot be heard in dominant systems.

## WENDY BROWN: WALLED STATES, WOUNDED SOVEREIGNS

### Borders as Theater of Friend-Enemy in Neoliberal Era

**Key Works:**
- *Walled States, Waning Sovereignty* (2010)
- *Undoing the Demos* (2015)
- *In the Ruins of Neoliberalism* (2019)

**Core Thesis:**

Wendy Brown (1955-) argues that the proliferation of walls and borders in the neoliberal era represents a **theatrical staging of sovereignty and friend-enemy distinction** at the very moment when sovereignty is waning. Walls don't actually secure but perform the fantasy of clear friend-enemy boundaries in a globalized world where such clarity is impossible.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Waning Sovereignty**:
   - Nation-state sovereignty eroded by globalization, capital flows, supranational institutions
   - State can't actually control what matters (capital, data, pollution)
   - But state **performs** control through borders and walls
   - **Friend-enemy theater** (not reality)

2. **Walls as Fantasy**:
   - Walls don't work practically (capital, data, drugs flow anyway)
   - But walls provide psychic comfort (fantasy of boundary, purity, control)
   - **Friend-enemy as fantasy**: Clear inside/outside that doesn't actually exist

3. **Neoliberal Subject**:
   - Neoliberalism creates insecure subjects (precarity, competition)
   - These subjects crave security, boundaries, order
   - **Walls provide fantasy of security** while neoliberalism produces insecurity

4. **Sovereignty as Performance**:
   - State performs sovereignty it doesn't have
   - Border enforcement, walls, deportations = sovereignty theater
   - **Friend-enemy as performance** (not function)

5. **Masculinity and Walls**:
   - Walls associated with masculine protection
   - Waning sovereignty = wounded masculinity
   - Walls restore fantasy of male protector
   - **Gender dimension of friend-enemy** (masculine self vs. threatening other)

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Theatrical Friend-Enemy**:
   - Borders create clear friend (citizen, inside) vs. enemy (migrant, outside)
   - But this clarity is **fantasy** (economy requires migrants, culture is hybrid)
   - **Performance without substance**

2. **Neoliberal Contradiction**:
   - Neoliberalism requires free flow (capital, goods, labor)
   - But produces anxiety requiring walls
   - **Friend-enemy contradicts neoliberal logic** but compensates for its effects

3. **Scapegoat Politics**:
   - Migrants blamed for neoliberalism's harms (inequality, insecurity)
   - Walls appear to address this
   - **False enemy** (migrants) distracts from real enemy (neoliberal capital)

4. **Wounded Sovereignty**:
   - State cannot control economy, environment, technology
   - So emphasizes control over bodies (migrants, criminals)
   - **Friend-enemy: Those who can be controlled** become primary enemies

5. **Nihilistic Politics**:
   - Walls represent nihilism (giving up on political solutions)
   - Just exclude, don't transform
   - **Friend-enemy as resignation** (can't solve problems, just wall them out)

**Examples:**

**US-Mexico Border Wall:**
- Doesn't stop migration or drugs effectively
- But performs sovereignty for anxious citizens
- Neoliberalism (NAFTA) destroyed Mexican agriculture → migration
- Wall blames migrants, not NAFTA
- Friend-enemy: Americans vs. Mexicans (distracts from capital vs. labor)

**Israeli Separation Wall:**
- Doesn't provide security (rockets go over, tunnels go under)
- But creates fantasy of separation

- Fortress Europe despite needing migrant labor

- Friend-enemy: Europeans vs. migrants (despite dependence on migrant labor)

**Gated Communities:**
- Private walls (same logic as state walls)
- Fantasy of security while inequality increases
- Perform class separation
- Friend-enemy: Wealthy (inside gates) vs. poor (outside)

**Brown vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy defines real politics
- **Brown**: Friend-enemy is often **fantasy/theater** (not functional)
- **Schmitt**: Sovereignty decides friend-enemy
- **Brown**: Waning sovereignty performs friend-enemy to appear sovereign

**Brown vs. Hardt & Negri:**

- **H&N**: Empire is deterritorialized, no more borders
- **Brown**: Borders proliferate precisely because deterritorialized (compensation)
- **H&N**: Post-national
- **Brown**: Hyper-national (desperate reassertion)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Brown reveals **friend-enemy as theater**—walls and borders proliferate as sovereignty wanes, providing fantasy of control and clear boundaries in globalized world where neither exists. Walls don't actually secure but perform sovereignty for insecure, precarious subjects produced by neoliberalism. Friend-enemy distinction (citizen vs. migrant) becomes **staged spectacle** distracting from actual powerlessness of state to control capital, environment, or technology.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **neoliberal analysis** of contemporary friend-enemy. Explains border militarization (theater of sovereignty), immigration panic (scapegoating for neoliberal harms), wall building (fantasy of control). Shows friend-enemy as **compensatory mechanism**—staged to make people feel secure while actual security (economic, environmental) erodes. Essential for understanding contemporary border politics and immigration discourse.

## HANNAH ARENDT: TOTALITARIANISM AND THE ELIMINATION OF PLURALISM

### When Friend-Enemy Becomes Total War Against Humanity

**Key Works:**
- *The Origins of Totalitarianism* (1951)
- *The Human Condition* (1958)
- *Eichmann in Jerusalem: A Report on the Banality of Evil* (1963)
- *On Violence* (1970)

**Core Thesis:**

Hannah Arendt (1906-1975) analyzes totalitarianism as a novel form of government that seeks to eliminate **all plurality**—transforming not just political enemies but all of humanity into an undifferentiated mass. Totalitarianism pushes friend-enemy logic to its ultimate extreme: eventual total enmity toward human spontaneity and individuality itself.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Totalitarianism vs. Tyranny**:
   - Tyranny has enemies (political opponents)
   - Totalitarianism eliminates category of "enemy" by eliminating all categories
   - Creates **total terror** where everyone is potential victim
   - **Friend-enemy transcended into universal precarity**

2. **The Camps**:
   - Laboratories of total domination
   - Goal: Destroy human spontaneity, plurality, uniqueness
   - Inmates neither friends nor enemies but **specimens**
   - **Beyond friend-enemy: All are superfluous**

3. **Superfluousness**:
   - Totalitarianism treats humans as superfluous (unnecessary, disposable)
   - Not just enemies but all people potentially killable
   - Camps demonstrate this logic
   - **Friend-enemy collapses: All are enemies to totalitarian logic**

4. **Loneliness and Atomization**:
   - Totalitarianism destroys all bonds (family, friendship, community)
   - Creates isolated, lonely atoms
   - No friends, no solidarities, no trust
   - **Friend-enemy impossible: No "we" can form**

5. **Banality of Evil**:
   - Eichmann not monster but bureaucrat
   - Evil as thoughtlessness, rule-following
   - **Enemies mass-produced through administrative procedures**
   - Genocide as paperwork

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Beyond Friend-Enemy**:
   - Totalitarianism doesn't operate through friend-enemy (Schmitt)
   - Creates **universal enmity**: State against all spontaneity
   - Even party members potential enemies
   - **Permanent purges**: Today's friends are tomorrow's enemies

2. **Ideology as Total Enmity**:
   - Totalitarian ideology (race, class) designates enemies
   - But enemies expand infinitely (Jews → Slavs → eventually all)
   - Ideology creates **limitless enmity** (reality must conform to ideology)
   - **Historical process as enemy** (anything contradicting ideology)

3. **Terror Eliminates Opposition**:
   - Not just political enemies but **possibility of opposition**
   - Terror creates universal fear, universal suspicion
   - No one can trust anyone (all potential informers)
   - **Friend-enemy internalized: Everyone enemies to themselves and others**

4. **The Masses**:
   - Totalitarianism creates "the masses" (atomized individuals)
   - Masses have no friends (no solidarity), only **mass movements**
   - Movement directed at ever-shifting enemies
   - **Friend-enemy as mob psychology**: Whoever ideology designates today

5. **Anti-Politics**:
   - Totalitarianism destroys politics (Arendtian sense: plural action)
   - Politics requires friend-enemy (Schmitt correct)
   - But totalitarianism eliminates even this
   - **Anti-political: No friends, only administered masses and infinite enemies**

**Examples:**

**Stalinist Purges:**
- Old Bolsheviks (friends) became "wreckers," "Trotskyites" (enemies)
- Friend-enemy unstable: Today's hero, tomorrow's traitor
- Terror: Everyone potential enemy
- Ideology: Anyone can be enemy if historical process demands it

**Nazi Camps:**
- Jews initially defined as enemies
- But eventually Slavs, disabled, homosexuals, Romani, political opponents
- Camp: All reduced to numbers, superfluous life
- Friend-enemy: Initially racial, eventually anyone contradicting ideology

**North Korea:**
- Three generations of punishment
- Family members of "enemies" also enemies
- Total surveillance, total isolation
- Friend-enemy: Impossible to know who is friend (all suspect)

**Cultural Revolution (China):**
- Shifting categories of enemy ("capitalist roaders," intellectuals)
- Friend-enemy reversed daily
- Students denounce teachers, children denounce parents
- Totalitarian logic: No stable friendships possible

**Arendt vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy defines politics
- **Arendt**: Totalitarianism destroys politics (no stable friend-enemy)
- **Schmitt**: Enemy unifies friends
- **Arendt**: Totalitarianism atomizes (no friends)
- **Schmitt**: Sovereign decides exception
- **Arendt**: Exception becomes rule, all potentially excluded

**Arendt vs. Foucault:**

- **Foucault**: Disciplinary power produces normalized subjects
- **Arendt**: Totalitarian power eliminates subjects (makes superfluous)
- **Foucault**: Power productive
- **Arendt**: Totalitarian power purely destructive (of human spontaneity)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Arendt reveals totalitarianism as **beyond friend-enemy**—it doesn't just have enemies but treats all humans as potentially superfluous. Friend-enemy distinction becomes impossible because no stable categories exist (today's friends are tomorrow's enemies), and terror atomizes population so no solidarities can form. This is friend-enemy logic pushed beyond itself—universal enmity where even the concept of "friend" is destroyed.

**Critical Addition**: Shows **extreme form of enmity**: Totalitarianism doesn't just oppose enemies but seeks to eliminate **human plurality itself**. Explains Stalin's purges (friend-enemy reversible), Nazi camps (all humans potentially superfluous), and any system where terror makes all relationships suspicious. Also provides normative vision: Politics requires plurality; destroying friend-enemy entirely (totalitarian goal) destroys politics and humanity.

## AIMÉ CÉSAIRE: COLONIAL DISCOURSE AND NEGRITUDE

### The Colonizer's Friend-Enemy Boomerang

**Key Works:**
- *Discourse on Colonialism* (1950)
- *Notebook of a Return to the Native Land* (1939)
- *Toussaint Louverture* (1960 play)

**Core Thesis:**

Aimé Césaire (1913-2008) argues that colonialism's treatment of colonized peoples as enemies/non-humans **boomerangs back** to Europe—the violence and dehumanization practiced in colonies becomes the model for fascism in Europe. The friend-enemy logic used against Black and colonized peoples eventually applied to Europeans themselves (Jews, Slavs).

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Colonialism as Training Ground**:
   - Europe learned to treat humans as things in colonies
   - Genocide practiced on colonized (Congolese, Herero, etc.)
   - **Techniques perfected on colonial enemies** then applied in Europe
   - Hitler did to Europeans what Europe did to Africans

2. **The Boomerang Effect**:
   - Violence toward colonial enemy returns home
   - Friend-enemy: Initially European (friend) vs. African (enemy)
   - Eventually: Aryan (friend) vs. Jew/Slav (enemy within Europe)
   - **Colonial enmity becomes European self-enmity**

3. **Negritude**:
   - Assertion of Black identity and culture
   - Reverses colonial friend-enemy
   - Black is beautiful, Black culture is civilization
   - **Friend-enemy inverted**: Black pride against white supremacy

4. **Civilizational Critique**:
   - European "civilization" is built on colonial barbarism
   - Friend (civilized European) requires enemy (savage African)
   - But colonizer becomes barbaric through colonizing
   - **Civilization = barbarism** (dialectic)

5. **Decolonial Consciousness**:
   - Must recognize colonialism as system
   - All colonized are friends (Pan-African solidarity)
   - Enemy is not individual colonizers but **colonialism as structure**
   - **Structural friend-enemy** (not racial hatred but system opposition)

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Boomerang of Enmity**:
   - Treat others as enemies/non-humans long enough, become capable of treating anyone this way
   - Colonial friend-enemy normalizes dehumanization
   - Eventually turned inward (European fascism)
   - **Enmity is habit-forming**

2. **Civilization vs. Barbarism**:
   - Colonial discourse: Europeans civilized, Africans barbaric
   - Césaire: Colonialism itself is barbarism
   - Friend-enemy: Who is actually civilized?
   - **Reversal of colonial friend-enemy**

3. **Race as Friend-Enemy**:
   - Colonialism created modern racism
   - Race becomes permanent friend-enemy marker
   - Cannot be shed (unlike political allegiance)
   - **Biological friend-enemy** (most intractable)

4. **Hitler as Colonial Project at Home**:
   - Lebensraum = colonization of Eastern Europe
   - Slavs as colonized (subhuman, disposable)
   - Jews as ultimate enemy (as Tutsis, Hereros were)
   - **Colonialism coming home**

5. **Negritude as Counter-Identity**:
   - Must create strong friend identity (Black consciousness)
   - Against enemy (white supremacy)
   - But risk: Essentialism (fixing racial categories)
   - **Strategic friend-enemy** (necessary but dangerous)

**Examples:**

**Holocaust:**
- Césaire's argument: Hitler applied to Jews what Europe did to Africans
- Concentration camps = colonial forced labor camps
- Genocide = colonial genocide brought to Europe
- Friend-enemy: Colonial logic turned on Europeans

**Belgian Congo:**
- 10+ million killed under Leopold II
- Hands cut off for failing rubber quotas
- Complete dehumanization
- Europe ignored (only Africans, not "real" victims)
- Friend-enemy: European lives matter, African lives don't

**Herero Genocide:**
- German colonial genocide in Namibia (1904-1908)
- Explicitly racial extermination
- General Lothar von Trotha: "The Herero nation must vanish"
- Training for Nazi genocides
- Friend-enemy: Colonial (German vs. Herero) → Fascist (Aryan vs. non-Aryan)

**Fascism in Europe:**
- Mussolini's Ethiopia invasion
- Used gas, terror, mass killing
- Colonial methods
- Friend-enemy: Colonial violence becomes fascist violence

**Césaire vs. Arendt:**

- **Arendt**: Totalitarianism is novel, unprecedented
- **Césaire**: Totalitarianism practiced in colonies first
- **Arendt**: Holocaust unique
- **Césaire**: Holocaust is colonialism applied to Europeans
- **Arendt**: Saw totalitarianism as radical break
- **Césaire**: Saw continuity with colonialism

**Césaire vs. Fanon:**

- **Fanon**: Violence liberates colonized
- **Césaire**: Violence practiced by colonizers eventually destroys them too
- **Fanon**: Focus on colonized subject
- **Césaire**: Focus on colonizer's self-destruction
- Both: Colonialism is absolute friend-enemy

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Césaire reveals **colonial origins of European totalitarianism**—the friend-enemy logic that made Africans killable eventually made Jews killable. Dehumanization is habit-forming: Practice treating some humans as non-human long enough, and you can treat anyone this way. The boomerang of colonial violence: What Europe did to Africa, Hitler did to Europe. Friend-enemy distinctions honed in colonies (racial, absolute, genocidal) returned home.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **colonial genealogy** of totalitarianism. Explains Holocaust as application of colonial methods (camps, genocide, racial dehumanization) to Europeans. Shows how friend-enemy logic practiced abroad eventually returns home (boomerang effect). Essential for understanding relationship between colonialism and fascism, and for seeing racial friend-enemy as modern European creation tested in colonies.

## ALBERT MEMMI: THE COLONIZER AND THE COLONIZED - MUTUALLY CONSTRUCTED ENMITY

### The Dialectic of Domination

**Key Work:**
- *The Colonizer and the Colonized* (1957)

**Core Thesis:**

Albert Memmi (1920-2020) provides detailed psychological analysis of how **colonizer and colonized mutually construct each other** as enemies through colonial relationship. Both are deformed by colonialism—colonizer becomes racist oppressor, colonized becomes resentful, self-hating subject. Friend-enemy is not pre-existing but **produced by structural relationship**.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **The Colonial Relationship**:
   - Colonizer and colonized define each other mutually
   - Neither exists independent of relationship
   - **Structural enmity**: Not personal hatred but system position

2. **Portrait of the Colonizer**:
   - **Colonizer who accepts**: Becomes racist, privileges blind him
   - **Colonizer who refuses**: Must leave or becomes traitor
   - No good colonizer (relationship itself is enemy relationship)
   - **Enmity is role, not choice**

3. **Portrait of the Colonized**:
   - Stripped of culture, language, history
   - Internalized inferiority ("colonized complex")
   - Two responses: Assimilation (become colonizer) or revolt
   - **Self-enmity**: Colonized hates himself, his people, his culture

4. **Impossibility of Reform**:
   - Colonial relationship cannot be reformed
   - "Good" colonizers still benefit from oppression
   - Only end: Complete end of colonial relationship
   - **No friendship possible in structural enmity**

5. **Two Refusals**:
   - Colonized must refuse: Colonial identity AND desire to become colonizer
   - Must create new identity (decolonized self)
   - **Double negation**: Refuse enemy's definition AND desire to be enemy

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Structural Production of Enmity**:
   - Colonizer and colonized don't choose enmity
   - **Relationship produces the subjects** (and their enmity)
   - Cannot have "friendly" colonialism
   - **Enmity is inherent to structure**

2. **Good Intentions Irrelevant**:
   - Even "progressive" colonizers are oppressors
   - System makes them enemies regardless of personal views
   - **Structural position overrides individual intent**
   - Friend-enemy: Systemic, not personal

3. **Mutual Deformation**:
   - Both colonizer and colonized damaged by relationship
   - Colonizer becomes arrogant, racist, blind
   - Colonized becomes self-hating, resentful, dependent
   - **Enmity deforms both sides**

4. **Assimilation as Betrayal**:
   - Colonized who assimilate betray their people
   - Become "friends" of colonizer, enemies of colonized
   - But never fully accepted (remain marked as Other)
   - **False friendship**: Assimilated still enemy underneath

5. **Revolt as Only Solution**:
   - Cannot reform colonial relationship (inherently enemy)
   - Must destroy relationship entirely
   - Decolonization requires recognizing: We are enemies, and must end this enmity by ending colonialism
   - **Recognize enmity to transcend it**

**Examples:**

**French Algeria:**
- Memmi was Tunisian Jew under French colonialism
- Experienced colonial enmity personally
- Analyzed pieds-noirs (European settlers) as trapped in colonial logic
- Even "good" settlers benefited from oppression
- Friend-enemy: Structural (settlers vs. natives), not personal

**Apartheid:**
- White liberals opposed apartheid but benefited from it
- Position (white) made them oppressors regardless
- Black consciousness rejected white allies as structurally complicit
- Friend-enemy: Structural (white vs. Black), not intentional

**Neocolonialism:**
- Independence doesn't end colonial relationship
- New elites replicate colonial positions
- Structural enmity persists (Global North vs. Global South)
- Friend-enemy: Recreated in new forms (IMF, World Bank, debt)

**Zionism and Palestinians:**
- Memmi (Zionist) struggled with parallel
- Israeli settlers vs. Palestinians = colonial relationship?
- Structural enmity regardless of individual intentions
- Friend-enemy: Produced by settler-colonial structure

**Memmi vs. Fanon:**

- **Fanon**: Emphasizes revolutionary violence
- **Memmi**: Emphasizes psychological analysis
- **Fanon**: Manichean world (clear enemies)
- **Memmi**: Both sides deformed (mutual enemy-creation)
- Both: Colonial relationship must end entirely

**Memmi vs. Sartre:**

- **Sartre**: Colonizer's freedom depends on colonized's oppression
- **Memmi**: Both colonizer and colonized trapped (different ways)
- **Sartre**: Bad faith of colonizer
- **Memmi**: System makes roles (individuals cannot escape)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Memmi reveals friend-enemy as **structurally produced**—colonizer and colonized are made into enemies by their positions in colonial system, regardless of personal intentions. No "good" colonizers because the role itself is oppressive. Both sides are deformed: Colonizer becomes racist oppressor, colonized becomes self-hating subject. Only solution is **ending the relationship entirely**—recognizing structural enmity to transcend it.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **structural-psychological** analysis of friend-enemy. Explains how roles (colonizer/colonized, oppressor/oppressed) produce enmity independent of individuals' choices. Shows impossibility of "friendly" oppression. Essential for understanding settler-colonialism (Israel/Palestine), neocolonialism, and any situation where structural positions create enemies. Also shows: Both sides damaged by enmity, both need liberation.

## ÉTIENNE BALIBAR: BORDERS, VIOLENCE, AND DEMOCRATIC CITIZENSHIP

### The Border as Site of Friend-Enemy Production

**Key Works:**
- *Politics and the Other Scene* (2002)
- *We, the People of Europe?* (2004)
- *Violence and Civility* (2015)

**Core Thesis:**

Étienne Balibar (1942-) analyzes how **borders** function as the primary site where friend-enemy distinctions are produced and maintained in the modern world. Borders create citizens (friends) and aliens (enemies), but also produce extreme violence against those trying to cross. Friend-enemy operates through the violent policing of boundaries.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **The Border**:
   - Not just geographic line but **social institution**
   - Produces citizen (friend) and alien (enemy)
   - Borders are where sovereignty is performed
   - **Friend-enemy materialized in space**

2. **Border Violence**:
   - Borders are sites of extreme violence (deaths in Mediterranean, Rio Grande, Sahara)
   - Violence against border-crossers is acceptable (enemy status)
   - **Differential violence**: Citizens protected, migrants killable

3. **Democratization of Borders**:
   - Historically: Borders controlled elites (peasants tied to land)
   - Modern: All citizens participate in border maintenance (immigration politics)
   - **Democratic friend-enemy**: "The people" define themselves against migrants

4. **The Right to Have Rights**:
   - Following Arendt: Rights depend on citizenship
   - Non-citizens have no rights (stateless, refugees)
   - Border creates those **without right to rights**
   - **Friend-enemy through rights**: Citizens (rights-bearers) vs. aliens (rightless)

5. **Conflicting Universalisms**:
   - Universal human rights vs. national sovereignty
   - Borders violate universalism (not all humans equal)
   - But borders necessary for democracy (the demos must be bounded)
   - **Contradiction**: Universal rights require particularistic boundaries

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Border as Friend-Enemy Machine**:
   - Border continuously produces friend (citizen crossing freely) and enemy (migrant detained/deported)
   - Same action (crossing), different status (friend vs. enemy)
   - **Automated friend-enemy designation**

2. **Extreme Violence at Margins**:
   - Border violence is extreme and accepted
   - Thousands die crossing (Mediterranean, desert)
   - Deaths don't produce outrage (enemies dying)
   - **Friend-enemy allows violence at boundaries**

3. **Citizenship as Friendship**:
   - To be citizen = to be friend of the State
   - To be non-citizen = to be potential enemy
   - Citizenship is exclusive privilege
   - **Legal friend-enemy**: Who has papers determines who lives/dies

4. **Border Externalization**:
   - Rich countries push borders outward (EU pays Libya to stop migrants)
   - Creates buffer zones where extreme violence occurs
   - Out of sight (Mediterranean, Sahara, Mexican desert)
   - **Friend-enemy at distance** (don't see enemy dying)

5. **Democratic Exclusion**:
   - Democracies define "the people" through borders
   - The people decide who can join (immigration politics)
   - **Democratic friend-enemy**: People vote to exclude aliens

**Examples:**

**Mediterranean Deaths:**
- 25,000+ migrants died since 2014
- Deaths from drowning, exposure, violence
- Accepted as normal (enemy status)
- Balibar: These deaths constitute borders
- Friend-enemy: European citizens vs. African/Middle Eastern migrants

**US Border:**
- Thousands die crossing Sonoran desert
- Deaths from dehydration, heat, exposure
- Border Patrol destroys water left by humanitarians
- Friend-enemy: Americans vs. "illegals" (killable through abandonment)

**Australia's Pacific Solution:**
- Offshore detention in Nauru, Manus Island
- Indefinite detention, suicide, abuse
- Out of sight, out of mind
- Friend-enemy: Australians (protected) vs. boat people (detained indefinitely)

**Calais Jungle:**
- Refugee camp at French border
- Repeatedly demolished, migrants dispersed
- Police violence, tear gas, beatings
- Friend-enemy: French citizens vs. refugees (subjected to violence)

**Balibar vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy is political decision
- **Balibar**: Friend-enemy is **institutionalized in borders** (automatic)
- **Schmitt**: Sovereign decides exception
- **Balibar**: Border is permanent exception (violence normalized)

**Balibar vs. Agamben:**

- **Agamben**: Camp as paradigm of modern power
- **Balibar**: Border as paradigm (camps are just extreme border enforcement)
- **Agamben**: Homo sacer (excluded from law)
- **Balibar**: Migrant (excluded from territory and rights)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Balibar reveals **borders as friend-enemy machines**—continuously producing citizens (friends) and aliens (enemies) through violent policing of boundaries. The border is where friend-enemy becomes materially enforced: Cross with right papers (friend passes freely), cross without (enemy detained, deported, dies). Modern sovereignty is increasingly **border sovereignty**—cannot control economy but can control bodies at borders, making migrants the perfect enemy for waning states.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **border-focused analysis** of friend-enemy. Explains Mediterranean deaths (enemy status makes thousands of deaths acceptable), immigration detention (camps at borders), border militarization (sovereignty theater), and citizenship as primary friend-enemy distinction. Shows how democracy itself is exclusionary (the people define themselves by excluding aliens). Essential for understanding migration politics and border violence.

## ERNST JÜNGER: TOTAL MOBILIZATION AND THE WORKER AS NEW FRIEND-ENEMY TYPE

### Technology and the Transformation of War

**Key Works:**
- *Storm of Steel* (1920)
- *Total Mobilization* (1930)
- *The Worker* (1932)
- *On Pain* (1934)

**Core Thesis:**

Ernst Jünger (1895-1998) analyzes how modern technology transforms friend-enemy relations through **total mobilization**—the integration of entire societies into war machines. The "Worker" (der Arbeiter) becomes the new human type—defined by technological mobilization rather than class or nation. Friend-enemy becomes total: entire populations, economies, and technologies arrayed against each other.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Total Mobilization**:
   - Modern war mobilizes everything (economy, science, population, culture)
   - No distinction between soldier and civilian, front and home
   - **Total friend-enemy**: Entire societies become warring units
   - World War I as paradigm

2. **The Worker**:
   - New human type shaped by technology
   - Not proletarian (Marx) but **technological-organizational type**
   - Defined by integration into technical apparatus
   - **Friend-enemy through technical integration**

3. **The Mobilized Landscape**:
   - Nature transformed into war resource
   - Geography becomes strategic (everything weaponizable)
   - Total environment enlisted in conflict
   - **Friend-enemy extends to material world**

4. **Pain and Distance**:
   - Modern warfare creates distance (artillery, bombers, drones)
   - Killing without seeing enemy
   - Pain becomes abstract, statistical
   - **Friend-enemy without proximity** (kill unseen enemies)

5. **Organic Construction**:
   - Technology and organization fuse with organic life
   - Humans become parts of larger technical systems
   - Friend-enemy: Systems arrayed against systems
   - **Post-human friend-enemy**

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Total War = Total Friend-Enemy**:
   - No non-combatants (all mobilized)
   - No safe spaces (strategic bombing)
   - No neutral ground (either with us or against us)
   - **Friend-enemy extends to entire population**

2. **Technological Friend-Enemy**:
   - Not ideological or political enemies but **technical adversaries**
   - Industrial production becomes weapon
   - Scientific research becomes warfare
   - **Friend-enemy through technological capacity**

3. **The Administered Enemy**:
   - War becomes industrial process
   - Enemies processed efficiently (logistics, systems)
   - **Rationalized enmity**: Scientific management of killing
   - Holocaust as extreme: Industrial murder

4. **Distance and Abstraction**:
   - Modern weapons create distance from enemy
   - Never see who you kill (drones, missiles, bombers)
   - **Friend-enemy without face**: Abstract categories, not persons

5. **Permanent Mobilization**:
   - Total mobilization doesn't end with war
   - Peace is just preparation for next war
   - **Cold War as permanent mobilization**
   - Friend-enemy as permanent condition of technological society

**Examples:**

**World War I:**
- Jünger fought throughout (wounded 14 times)
- Entire economies mobilized
- Millions killed through industrial means (artillery, gas, machine guns)
- Friend-enemy: National-industrial blocs (Entente vs. Central Powers)

**World War II:**
- Total mobilization perfected
- Strategic bombing (Dresden, Tokyo, Hiroshima)
- All civilians legitimate targets
- Friend-enemy: Total (Allies vs. Axis, no neutrality)

**Cold War:**
- Permanent mobilization without war
- Entire societies organized around enemy (USSR vs. USA)
- Nuclear weapons: Total threat, total deterrence
- Friend-enemy: Ideological-technical (capitalism vs. communism, but fought through tech)

**Drone Warfare:**
- Jünger's vision realized: Kill without proximity
- Operator in Nevada, target in Yemen
- Total distance, zero risk
- Friend-enemy: Abstract, algorithmic

**Jünger vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy as political decision
- **Jünger**: Friend-enemy as technological mobilization
- **Schmitt**: Intensity creates enmity
- **Jünger**: Technology creates new enmity (systematic, industrial)
- **Schmitt**: Political friend-enemy
- **Jünger**: Technical friend-enemy

**Jünger vs. Heidegger:**

- **Heidegger**: Technology "enframes" (Gestell), destroys Being
- **Jünger**: Technology creates new human type (Worker)
- **Heidegger**: Technology nihilistic
- **Jünger**: Technology reveals new order
- Both: Technology transforms friend-enemy

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Jünger reveals **total mobilization** as transforming friend-enemy into systematic, industrial, technological relationship. Modern war doesn't just involve armies but entire societies, economies, sciences, natures. Friend-enemy becomes **total**: All resources enlisted, no civilians, no neutral ground. Technology creates distance (drone strikes) and intensity (nuclear weapons). The Worker (technologically integrated human) replaces the citizen as primary type. Friend-enemy becomes abstract, efficient, systematic.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **technological analysis** of friend-enemy. Explains World Wars (total mobilization), Cold War (permanent mobilization), drone warfare (distance and abstraction), and how technology transforms enmity from personal/political to systematic/industrial. Shows friend-enemy as increasingly **automated and algorithmic**—systems fighting systems, with humans as components. Essential for understanding modern warfare and technological society.

## GEORGES SOREL: REVOLUTIONARY VIOLENCE AND THE POLITICAL MYTH

### The General Strike and Friend-Enemy Intensification

**Key Work:**
- *Reflections on Violence* (1908)

**Core Thesis:**

Georges Sorel (1847-1922) argues that revolutionary violence, organized through the **myth of the General Strike**, creates authentic friend-enemy distinctions by intensifying class conflict rather than mediating it. Parliamentary democracy weakens this distinction (makes enemies into negotiating partners), while revolutionary syndicalism sharpens it (makes class war explicit).

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Political Myths**:
   - Not rational programs but **mobilizing images**
   - The General Strike as myth (not prediction but inspiration)
   - Myths create solidarity (friends) and identify enemies
   - **Friend-enemy through myth** (not analysis)

2. **Revolutionary Violence**:
   - Violence of workers against capitalists (direct)
   - Not state violence (repressive) but **proletarian violence** (liberating)
   - Violence clarifies friend-enemy (no mediation)
   - **Violence as clarification** (not terror)

3. **Against Parliamentary Socialism**:
   - Parliament obscures class conflict
   - Makes enemies into "partners" (social dialogue, compromise)
   - **Democracy weakens friend-enemy** (Schmitt agrees)
   - Revolutionary syndicalism maintains class enmity

4. **Intensity and Dignity**:
   - Heroic violence creates dignity
   - Workers through strike/sabotage assert themselves as enemies (not beggars)
   - **Friend-enemy creates identity**: Know yourself through knowing your enemy

5. **Bourgeois Decadence**:
   - Bourgeoisie weakened by humanitarianism, compromise
   - No longer acts as enemy (vacillates)
   - Proletariat must be **unambiguous enemy** to force confrontation
   - **Clear enmity prevents false friends**

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Class as Friend-Enemy**:
   - Working class (friends) vs. capitalist class (enemies)
   - Not individuals but **classes as collective subjects**
   - Enmity is structural (relation to means of production)
   - **Economic friend-enemy**

2. **Violence Intensifies Distinction**:
   - Parliamentary politics blurs friend-enemy (negotiation, compromise)
   - Violence clarifies: We are enemies, not partners
   - **Enmity through confrontation** (not declaration)

3. **Myth Unifies Friends**:
   - General Strike myth creates working-class solidarity
   - All workers united against all capitalists
   - No mediation (unions bureaucratize, parliaments compromise)
   - **Friend-enemy through mythic imagination**

4. **Against Social Peace**:
   - Social democrats seek peace (friends with capitalists)
   - Sorel: This betrays workers (false friends, real enemies)
   - Must maintain class enmity
   - **Permanent conflict** (not negotiation)

5. **Dignity Through Enmity**:
   - Workers achieve dignity by being enemies (not supplicants)
   - Strike/sabotage = assertion of power
   - **Enmity as self-affirmation** (not victimhood)

**Examples:**

**General Strikes:**
- 1909 Barcelona, 1919 Seattle, 1926 Britain
- Entire cities shut down
- Workers assert power as enemies of capital
- Friend-enemy: Clear class lines (workers vs. owners)

**Sabotage:**
- Direct action against capitalist property
- Workers as active enemies (not passive victims)
- French word *sabotage* (throwing sabot/wooden shoe into machinery)
- Friend-enemy: Workers destroy enemy's means

**Syndicalism:**
- Workers organized by industry (unions)
- Direct action, no parliamentary politics
- Prepare for revolutionary confrontation
- Friend-enemy: Class-based, unmediated

**Fascist Appropriation:**
- Mussolini was Sorelian initially
- Fascism took Sorel's violence and myth
- But changed friend-enemy: Nation vs. nation (not class vs. class)
- Friend-enemy: Sorel's method, fascist content

**Sorel vs. Marx:**

- **Marx**: Scientific analysis of capitalism's contradictions
- **Sorel**: Political myth (General Strike) mobilizes workers
- **Marx**: Objective class enemy
- **Sorel**: Subjective assertion of enmity (through violence)

**Sorel vs. Social Democrats:**

- **Social Democrats**: Negotiate with capitalists (parliamentary)
- **Sorel**: Maintain class enmity (revolutionary)
- **Social Democrats**: Capitalists as partners
- **Sorel**: Capitalists as enemies (no compromise)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Sorel reveals **violence as intensifier of friend-enemy**—revolutionary violence doesn't just attack enemies but **clarifies** who enemies are by refusing mediation. Parliamentary democracy obscures class conflict (makes enemies into negotiating partners). Revolutionary syndicalism asserts class enmity through direct action (strikes, sabotage). The General Strike myth unifies workers as friends against capital as enemy. Friend-enemy through confrontation, not analysis.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **revolutionary syndicalist** perspective on friend-enemy. Explains why some movements reject electoral politics (weakens friend-enemy), why direct action (violence intensifies distinction), why myths mobilize (General Strike creates solidarity). Shows how parliamentary democracy **obscures** friend-enemy while revolutionary politics **clarifies** it. Influential on both left (anarcho-syndicalism) and right (fascism's appropriation).

## WILLIAM E. CONNOLLY: PLURALISM, IDENTITY, AND THE POLITICS OF ENMITY

### Identity Requires Difference, Difference Breeds Enmity

**Key Works:**
- *Identity/Difference: Democratic Negotiations of Political Paradox* (1991)
- *The Ethos of Pluralization* (1995)
- *Pluralism* (2005)

**Core Thesis:**

William E. Connolly (1938-) argues that **identity always requires difference**—to define "us" (friends) requires defining "them" (enemies or at least others). But difference can be managed either through violence (converting difference into enmity) or through agonistic respect (maintaining difference without domination). Democratic pluralism requires transforming identity/difference from friend-enemy into agonistic engagement.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Identity/Difference**:
   - Identity depends on difference (cannot be "us" without "them")
   - Difference triggers anxiety (challenges our identity)
   - Two responses: Respect or enmity
   - **Friend-enemy emerges from identity anxiety**

2. **The Augustinian Imperative**:
   - Christianity's drive to convert (universalize one truth)
   - Those who resist conversion become enemies
   - **Univocal ontology creates enmity** (one truth, all else error)
   - Pluralism: Multiple ontologies (many truths)

3. **Agonistic Respect**:
   - Not tolerance (condescension) but respect (recognition)
   - Engage with difference without destroying it
   - **Agon not enemy**: Contest not war
   - Schmittian enmity transformed into democratic agonism (similar to Mouffe)

4. **The Paradox of Democracy**:
   - Democracy requires bounded demos (the people)
   - But also requires pluralism (inclusion of difference)
   - **Tension**: Include difference but maintain identity
   - Friend-enemy: Always threatening to emerge from identity/difference

5. **Resonance Machines**:
   - Different groups can resonate (support each other) despite differences
   - Coalitions across difference
   - **Friends without identity sameness**
   - Possibility of solidarity without homogeneity

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Identity Anxiety Produces Enemies**:
   - Strong identity requires clear boundaries
   - Those outside boundaries threaten identity
   - **Anxiety converts difference into enmity**
   - Friend-enemy: Those who affirm our identity vs. those who challenge it

2. **Conversion or Enmity**:
   - Univocal cultures (Christianity, Islam, Liberalism) want universal conversion
   - Those who won't convert become enemies
   - **Universalism creates enmity** (paradoxically)
   - Pluralism: Accept difference without conversion

3. **Democratic Inclusion's Limit**:
   - Cannot include those who reject democratic norms
   - "Militant democracy" excludes anti-democrats
   - **Friend-enemy at boundary of tolerance** ("no tolerance for intolerance")

4. **Multiple Agonisms**:
   - Friend-enemy not singular but multiple
   - Race, class, gender, religion, nation all create different agonisms
   - **Intersecting friend-enemy relations**

5. **Ethos of Engagement**:
   - Can engage difference without enmity
   - Requires: Generosity, critical responsiveness, presumption of worth
   - **Agonism not antagonism**: Opponent not enemy

**Examples:**

**Religious Pluralism:**
- Medieval Christianity: Heretics as enemies (must be converted or killed)
- Modern pluralism: Different faiths as agonistic (contest but respect)
- Friend-enemy: Shift from enemies (burn heretics) to adversaries (debate beliefs)

**Culture Wars:**
- Conservatives vs. progressives
- Can be enmity (cancel culture, culture war rhetoric)
- Or agonism (debate, political contest)
- Friend-enemy: Same difference, two modes of engagement

**Immigration:**
- Can be enemy (threat to identity)
- Or agonistic (negotiate incorporation)
- Depends on whether identity is anxious (creates enemies) or secure (tolerates difference)
- Friend-enemy: Identity anxiety determines response

**Democratic Boundaries:**
- Who is "the people"?
- Cannot include all humanity (infinite demos = no demos)
- But must include sufficient pluralism (or not democratic)
- Friend-enemy: At boundary of democratic inclusion

**Connolly vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy is essence of political (accept it)
- **Connolly**: Identity/difference can be agon or enmity (choose agon)
- **Schmitt**: Homogeneity strengthens friendship
- **Connolly**: Pluralism enriches democracy (heterogeneity possible)

**Connolly vs. Mouffe:**

- Both: Agonistic democracy (adversaries not enemies)
- **Mouffe**: Focus on political institutions
- **Connolly**: Focus on ethos (generosity, respect)
- Both: Transform friend-enemy into agonism

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Connolly reveals that **identity always creates difference**, and difference can become enmity when identity is anxious. Democratic pluralism requires managing identity/difference agonistically rather than antagonistically—maintaining difference without converting it into friend-enemy. This requires an **ethos of generosity** and critical responsiveness. Friend-enemy isn't inevitable; it's one way (violent, anxious) of managing identity/difference. Agonism is the alternative.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **identity-based analysis** of friend-enemy. Explains how identity anxiety converts difference into enmity, how univocal cultures (universalisms) create enemies through conversion imperatives, and how democratic pluralism can transform enemies into agonistic opponents. Shows friend-enemy as emerging from **identity anxiety** rather than objective threats. Essential for culture wars, religious conflicts, and democratic theory.

## GLEN COULTHARD: RED SKIN, WHITE MASKS - INDIGENOUS RESURGENCE AND COLONIAL RECOGNITION

### The Politics of Recognition as Continued Enmity

**Key Work:**
- *Red Skin, White Masks: Rejecting the Colonial Politics of Recognition* (2014)

**Core Thesis:**

Glen Coulthard (Yellowknives Dene) argues that liberal **politics of recognition**—where settler states "recognize" indigenous rights and identities—is a new form of colonialism that maintains the colonizer-colonized friend-enemy relationship while appearing to end it. True decolonization requires **refusal of recognition** and assertion of indigenous sovereignty, land, and resurgence.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Politics of Recognition as Colonial**:
   - Settler state "grants" recognition to indigenous peoples
   - But this maintains **master-slave dynamic** (colonizer grants, indigenous receive)
   - Recognition without land return or sovereignty
   - **False friendship**: Recognition maintains enmity under new form

2. **Self-Recognition vs. State Recognition**:
   - Indigenous don't need settler recognition to exist
   - Already have their own identities, governance, relationships to land
   - State recognition = **demand for indigenous submission** to settler authority
   - **Friend-enemy**: State as master, indigenous as dependent

3. **Land as Central**:
   - Colonialism is about land theft (dispossession)
   - Recognition without land return is meaningless
   - **Friend-enemy through land**: Settlers occupy, indigenous dispossessed
   - Material basis of enmity (not just cultural or political)

4. **Grounded Normativity**:
   - Indigenous ethical/political systems based on relationships to land
   - Alternative to Western individualism
   - Settler colonialism destroys these relationships
   - **Friend-enemy**: Grounded normativity vs. settler capitalism

5. **Resurgence not Recognition**:
   - Don't seek recognition from settler state
   - Assert indigenous sovereignty directly
   - Practice indigenous governance, ceremony, land relationships
   - **Refuse settler friend-enemy frame entirely**

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Recognition as Colonial Tool**:
   - Settler state offers "friendship" (recognition)
   - But maintains structural enmity (dispossession continues)
   - **False friends**: Recognition without material transformation
   - Friend-enemy: Recognition masks continued colonialism

2. **Dispossession as Ongoing**:
   - Colonialism not historical but **present and continuous**
   - Indigenous as permanent enemies (in way of "development")
   - Land continuously expropriated (pipelines, mines, dams)
   - **Structural enmity persists** regardless of recognition

3. **Sovereignty vs. Rights**:
   - Liberal framework: Indigenous have "rights" (granted by state)
   - Indigenous framework: Inherent sovereignty (not granted)
   - **Friend-enemy**: Who has authority to grant/recognize?

4. **Refusal as Politics**:
   - Refuse settler recognition (don't want their friendship)
   - Assert indigenous difference autonomously
   - **Strategic enmity**: Maintain oppositional stance
   - Don't be incorporated as "multicultural" diversity

5. **Land Back**:
   - Slogan: "Land Back" (not reconciliation)
   - Requires **ending settler colonialism** (not reforming it)
   - Friend-enemy: Cannot be friends while settlers occupy land
   - Recognition without land return = continued enmity

**Examples:**

**Canadian Reconciliation:**
- Truth and Reconciliation Commission
- Apologies, recognition, but **land remains stolen**
- Pipelines still forced through indigenous territories
- Friend-enemy: State offers recognition (friendship) while continuing dispossession (enmity)

**Idle No More:**
- Indigenous resistance movement (Canada)
- Reject recognition politics
- Assert sovereignty through blockades, occupation
- Friend-enemy: Clear (settlers vs. indigenous, land rights)

**Standing Rock:**
- Dakota Access Pipeline resistance
- State violence despite "recognition" of indigenous rights
- Land and water at stake
- Friend-enemy: Material (land, water) not symbolic (recognition)

**Wet'suwet'en:**
- Hereditary chiefs vs. pipeline
- State recognizes elected councils (who approved) not hereditary governance
- **Selective recognition** (only those who comply)
- Friend-enemy: State chooses compliant "friends," makes resisters enemies

**Coulthard vs. Taylor (Charles):**

- **Taylor**: Politics of recognition necessary for justice
- **Coulthard**: Recognition maintains colonialism (refuse it)
- **Taylor**: Multiculturalism through recognition
- **Coulthard**: Recognition without decolonization is meaningless

**Coulthard vs. Fanon:**

- **Fanon**: Colonized must assert themselves (violence)
- **Coulthard**: Indigenous must refuse recognition (sovereignty)
- **Fanon**: Black Skin, White Masks (colonized internalize oppressor)
- **Coulthard**: Red Skin, White Masks (parallel for indigenous)
- Both: Psychological liberation requires rejecting colonizer's categories

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Coulthard reveals **recognition as continued colonialism**—settler states offer "friendship" (recognizing indigenous rights) while maintaining structural enmity (dispossessing land). Politics of recognition is false friendship that masks ongoing enemy relationship. True decolonization requires **refusing recognition** and asserting sovereignty, land rights, and indigenous governance. Friend-enemy: Cannot end through symbolic recognition while material dispossession continues.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **indigenous/decolonial critique** of recognition politics. Explains why "reconciliation" fails (maintains colonial power), why "rights" aren't enough (sovereignty is the issue), why land is central (material basis of enmity). Shows liberal "friendship" (recognition, apology, multiculturalism) can mask continued enmity (dispossession, resource extraction). Essential for understanding settler-colonialism, indigenous struggles, and limits of liberal recognition politics.

## SILVIA FEDERICI: PRIMITIVE ACCUMULATION AND THE WITCH AS ENEMY

### Capitalism's Origin in Violence Against Women

**Key Works:**
- *Caliban and the Witch: Women, the Body and Primitive Accumulation* (2004)
- *Revolution at Point Zero: Housework, Reproduction, and Feminist Struggle* (2012)

**Core Thesis:**

Silvia Federici (1942-) argues that capitalism's origins lie in **primitive accumulation**—violent separation of people from land and means of subsistence. A crucial part was the **witch hunts** (16th-17th centuries), which destroyed women's autonomy, knowledge, and collective power. Women were made into enemies to establish capitalist social relations. Friend-enemy operates through gender.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Primitive Accumulation as War**:
   - Marx: Capitalism began through violent dispossession (enclosures)
   - Federici adds: War against women was central
   - Witch hunts destroyed female autonomy
   - **Women as enemies of emerging capitalism**

2. **The Witch as Enemy**:
   - Not delusion but **targeted persecution**
   - Midwives, healers, independent women made enemies
   - Controlled reproduction, contraception, abortion
   - **Enemy of demographic accumulation** (capital needs population growth)

3. **Reproductive Labor**:
   - Capitalism requires women's unpaid reproductive labor
   - Witch hunts subordinated women to reproductive role
   - Women's bodies became **territory of conflict**
   - **Friend-enemy through reproductive control**

4. **Wage vs. Unwaged**:
   - Men: Waged (official workers, friends of capital)
   - Women: Unwaged (reproductive laborers, excluded from wages)
   - **Gender friend-enemy** (waged vs. unwaged)

5. **Commons Destroyed**:
   - Enclosures destroyed collective land use
   - Witch hunts destroyed collective women's knowledge/power
   - **Friend-enemy**: Commons (collective) vs. capital (private)

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Women as First Enemies of Capital**:
   - Witch hunts targeted women who resisted capitalist reproduction
   - **100,000+ women killed** in European witch hunts
   - Enemy designation: Independent women who controlled reproduction
   - Friend: Subordinated women (housewives, dependent)

2. **Reproductive Enmity**:
   - Women's bodies became battlefield
   - Control of contraception, abortion, midwifery
   - State/Church/Capital vs. women's autonomy
   - **Friend-enemy through bodies**

3. **Gendered Division of Labor**:
   - Men: Productive labor (visible, waged)
   - Women: Reproductive labor (invisible, unwaged)
   - **Friend-enemy through work**: Recognized vs. unrecognized labor

4. **Wages as Friend-Enemy Marker**:
   - Waged workers (friends of capital, included)
   - Unwaged (reproductive workers, excluded)
   - Housewives: Economically dependent on husband (friend or enemy?)

5. **Accumulation Requires Enemies**:
   - Capitalism accumulates through creating enemies:
     - Witches (independent women)
     - Commons users (collective land rights)
     - Indigenous (on resource-rich land)
   - **Friend-enemy through accumulation** (dispossess enemies)

**Examples:**

**European Witch Hunts (1450-1750):**
- 100,000+ women killed
- Targeted: Midwives, healers, herbalists, independent women
- Destroyed women's control over reproduction
- Friend-enemy: Emerging capitalist state vs. autonomous women

**Enclosures:**
- Common lands privatized
- Peasants dispossessed, became wage laborers
- Those who resisted = enemies (vagrants, criminals, witches)
- Friend-enemy: Private property vs. commons

**Abortion Bans:**
- Modern continuation of reproductive control
- Women's bodies as territory of conflict
- State control over reproduction
- Friend-enemy: Reproductive autonomy vs. state/religious power

**Unpaid Domestic Labor:**
- $10+ trillion of unpaid work annually
- Not counted as "real" work
- Women's labor invisible
- Friend-enemy: Waged (friends) vs. unwaged (enemies/invisible)

**Federici vs. Marx:**

- **Marx**: Primitive accumulation = violence against peasants (men)
- **Federici**: Also violence against women specifically
- **Marx**: Focused on production
- **Federici**: Reproduction equally important (or more)
- **Marx**: Class friend-enemy
- **Federici**: Gender friend-enemy (intersecting with class)

**Federici vs. Foucault:**

- **Foucault**: Biopower manages populations
- **Federici**: Biopower specifically targets women's reproduction
- **Foucault**: General analysis
- **Federici**: Gender-specific analysis

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Federici reveals **gendered friend-enemy** at capitalism's origins—women (especially autonomous women) were made into enemies through witch hunts to establish capitalist reproductive relations. Women's bodies became territory of conflict, women's unpaid labor became foundation of accumulation, women's knowledge was destroyed. Friend-enemy operates through reproductive control: Women who control their bodies/reproduction are enemies; subordinated women are conscripted "friends" (housewives producing workers).

**Critical Addition**: Provides **feminist-materialist** analysis of friend-enemy. Explains witch hunts (women as enemies of emerging capitalism), reproductive politics (abortion, contraception as battlegrounds), unpaid domestic labor (gendered friend-enemy through wages). Shows capitalism originated through **war against women**, not just enclosures. Essential for understanding gender as friend-enemy dimension, reproductive politics, and feminist struggles.

## BYUNG-CHUL HAN: THE BURNOUT SOCIETY AND THE DISAPPEARANCE OF THE ENEMY

### Auto-Exploitation and the Self as Enemy

**Key Works:**
- *The Burnout Society* (2010)
- *The Transparency Society* (2012)
- *Psychopolitics: Neoliberalism and New Technologies of Power* (2014)
- *The Expulsion of the Other* (2016)

**Core Thesis:**

Byung-Chul Han (1959-) argues that neoliberal society has **eliminated the external enemy** only to turn the subject against itself. We move from a **disciplinary society** (Foucault—external enemies, norms) to an **achievement society** where individuals exploit themselves. The enemy is no longer "them" but **the self**—we are our own enemies in the burnout society.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Achievement Subject**:
   - No longer disciplined by external power (boss, teacher, State)
   - Self-exploits in pursuit of achievement, optimization, performance
   - **Auto-exploitation**: I am my own master AND my own slave
   - **Self as friend and enemy**

2. **Disappearance of the Other**:
   - Neoliberalism eliminates alterity (otherness)
   - Everything becomes Same (market logic, optimization, data)
   - **No enemy** because no Other (only replications of Self)
   - Narcissism not dialectic (Hegel: Self and Other)

3. **Exhaustion not Repression**:
   - Disciplinary society: Repressed by external power (creates resistance)
   - Achievement society: Exhausted by self-demand (creates depression, burnout)
   - **No resistance** because no external enemy (only self-blame)

4. **Positive Power**:
   - Not "You must not" (negative, prohibition, creates enemies)
   - But "You can" (positive, possibility, creates self-exploitation)
   - **Friendly power**: Enabling, not forbidding (but still dominating)
   - Friend-enemy: Self splits (achiever vs. failure)

5. **Transparency and Porn**:
   - Everything made transparent (no secrets, no mystery)
   - Other becomes pornographic (totally exposed, no alterity)
   - **No enemy** because nothing hidden to fear
   - But also: No genuine friendship (requires opacity, trust)

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Self as Own Enemy**:
   - No longer fight external enemies (boss, State, father)
   - Fight self (must achieve more, optimize, perform)
   - **Internalized enmity**: Depression, burnout, self-harm
   - Friend-enemy collapsed into self-division

2. **Disappearance of Enmity**:
   - Neoliberalism claims no enemies (only partners, competitors, collaborators)
   - But violence intensifies (against self)
   - **Hidden enmity**: Market competition, precarity, burn

out
   - Enmity denied but pervasive

3. **Friendly Power as Worse**:
   - Disciplinary power had clear enemies (could resist)
   - Achievement society has no enemies (cannot resist, only optimize harder)
   - **Friendly power more total** than enemy power (colonizes consciousness completely)

4. **Social Media as False Sociality**:
   - Appearance of friends (followers, likes)
   - Actually: Performance for metrics (self-exploitation)
   - **No genuine friendship** (only narcissistic self-projection)
   - Friend-enemy: Irrelevant (only Self and its reflections)

5. **Burnout Epidemic**:
   - Not individual pathology but systemic
   - Result of self-exploitation
   - **Self-enmity as political condition** (not psychological)

**Examples:**

**Gig Economy:**
- "Be your own boss" (achievement ideology)
- Self-exploitation (work 80 hours, no benefits)
- No external enemy to fight (you chose this)
- Friend-enemy: Self as boss AND slave

**Social Media Influencers:**
- Constant content production (self-exploitation)
- Burnout, depression epidemic
- But no external enemy (you're "living the dream")
- Friend-enemy: Self vs. self (must perform perfection)

**Academia:**
- "Publish or perish" (achievement imperative)
- Self-exploitation (endless work)
- Depression, burnout prevalent
- Friend-enemy: Self as achiever (friend) vs. failure (enemy)

**Startup Culture:**
- "Hustle culture," "grind mentality"
- Self-exploitation as virtue
- No external oppressor (you're "entrepreneur")
- Friend-enemy: Successful self vs. lazy self

**Han vs. Foucault:**

- **Foucault**: Disciplinary power creates docile bodies (external control)
- **Han**: Achievement power creates exhausted subjects (internal control)
- **Foucault**: Panopticon (external surveillance)
- **Han**: Self-surveillance (internalized metrics)
- **Foucault**: Resistance possible (against external power)
- **Han**: No resistance (only depression, because fighting self)

**Han vs. Deleuze:**

- **Deleuze**: Control societies (modulation, flexibility)
- **Han**: Achievement society (optimization, self-exploitation)
- Both: Post-disciplinary power
- **Han**: More pessimistic (no escape from self)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Han reveals **neoliberalism's elimination of external enemies** only to make the self its own enemy. Achievement society demands constant self-optimization, producing burnout and depression when the impossible is not achieved. Friend-enemy collapses into self-division (achiever vs. failure). Power becomes "friendly" (enabling) rather than repressive, which makes it more total—colonizes consciousness entirely. No external enemy means no resistance, only self-blame.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **neoliberal subjectivity analysis**. Explains burnout epidemic (self-exploitation without external enemy), social media narcissism (disappearance of Other), gig economy precarity (self-enmity), and why resistance is difficult (fighting yourself). Shows how **friendly power** (achievement, optimization) is more oppressive than enemy power (prohibition, discipline). Essential for understanding contemporary depression, burnout, and why neoliberal subjects don't resist (no enemy to fight).

## BONNIE HONIG: THE FOREIGNER AS NECESSARY ENEMY

### Democracy Requires the Outsider

**Key Works:**
- *Political Theory and the Displacement of Politics* (1993)
- *Democracy and the Foreigner* (2001)
- *Public Things: Democracy in Disrepair* (2017)

**Core Thesis:**

Bonnie Honig (1959-) argues that democracies are founded on and continuously require **the figure of the foreigner**—someone who is simultaneously inside and outside, friend and enemy. The foreigner is the constitutive outside that defines the democratic "we" but also threatens it, making the foreigner necessary for democratic identity but always potentially an enemy.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **The Foreigner**:
   - Neither fully inside nor fully outside (liminal)
   - Necessary for founding democracy (brings new ideas, energy)
   - But also threat (to "our" way of life)
   - **Ambiguous friend-enemy**: Necessary stranger

2. **Founding Violence**:
   - All democracies founded on exclusions
   - Some declared non-members (enemies)
   - "We the People" always leaves some out
   - **Friend-enemy at foundation**

3. **Xenophilia and Xenophobia**:
   - Democracy needs foreigners (renewal, vitality)
   - But fears them (contamination, transformation)
   - **Oscillation**: Foreigner as heroic founder AND dangerous enemy
   - Both needed for democratic imagination

4. **The Good vs. Bad Immigrant**:
   - "Good" immigrant: Assimilates, contributes, grateful
   - "Bad" immigrant: Maintains difference, demands rights, ungrateful
   - **Friend-enemy splitting**: Some foreigners friends, some enemies

5. **Perpetual Founding**:
   - Democracy must continuously refound itself
   - Foreigners force this (bring new claims, challenges)
   - **Enemy-as-renewal**: Foreigner-enemy revitalizes democracy

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Constitutive Outsider**:
   - Democratic "we" requires "them" (outsiders)
   - Cannot have democracy without **boundary** (who's in, who's out)
   - Foreigner at boundary: Potentially friend (can join) or enemy (threatens us)
   - **Friend-enemy defines democratic identity**

2. **Ambivalence**:
   - Foreigner is neither pure friend nor pure enemy
   - **Oscillates**: Sometimes welcomed, sometimes expelled
   - Immigration politics shows this oscillation
   - Friend-enemy: Unstable, context-dependent

3. **Founding Myths**:
   - Many democracies have "foreigner founder" myths (Moses, Aeneas)
   - Foreigner brings something necessary (law, civilization)
   - But also dangerous (not one of us)
   - **Friend-enemy in mythology**: Necessary enemy

4. **Naturalized vs. Born Citizens**:
   - Naturalized always slightly suspect (can be denaturalized)
   - "Real" citizens vs. "naturalized" citizens
   - Friend-enemy: Levels of belonging

5. **The Ungrateful Foreigner**:
   - Good foreigner is grateful (accepts subordination)
   - Ungrateful foreigner demands equality (becomes enemy)
   - **Friend-enemy through gratitude**: Accept lower status or become threat

**Examples:**

**US Founding:**
- "Nation of immigrants" mythology
- But founded on Native genocide and slavery
- Friend-enemy: Who counts as "immigrants" (Europeans yes, forced Africans no)

**Refugee Resettlement:**
- "Good" refugees: Grateful, assimilate, work hard
- "Bad" refugees: Demand rights, maintain culture, "ungrateful"
- Friend-enemy: Compliant vs. demanding foreigners

**Denaturalization:**
- Naturalized citizenship can be revoked
- Never fully secure friend status
- Perpetual potential enemy
- Friend-enemy: Born citizens (secure) vs. naturalized (precarious)

**"Go Back Where You Came From":**
- Said to citizens of color, immigrants
- Marks as perpetual foreigners (never fully friends)
- Friend-enemy: White natives (belong) vs. others (don't belong, enemy)

**Honig vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy is political decision (sovereign)
- **Honig**: Friend-enemy is democratic necessity (foreigner as constitutive)
- **Schmitt**: Homogeneity needed
- **Honig**: Heterogeneity necessary (foreigners revitalize)

**Honig vs. Liberal Multiculturalism:**

- **Liberals**: Include foreigners through tolerance
- **Honig**: Foreigners **needed** (not just tolerated)
- **Liberals**: Assimilation ideal
- **Honig**: Difference productive

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Honig reveals **the foreigner as necessary enemy-friend**—democracies require outsiders to define themselves but also fear these outsiders as threats. The foreigner is constitutively ambiguous: Can be heroic founder or dangerous enemy, assimilated friend or threatening other. Immigration politics shows this oscillation (welcome some, expel others). Friend-enemy not fixed but dynamic through the figure of the foreigner.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **foreigner-centered analysis** of democratic friend-enemy. Explains immigrant ambivalence (needed but feared), founding myths (foreigner founders), naturalization politics (never fully friend), and oscillation between xenophilia/xenophobia. Shows democracy **requires** the foreigner-enemy to maintain identity and vitality. Essential for understanding immigration politics and democratic boundaries.

## KIMBERLÉ CRENSHAW: INTERSECTIONALITY AND MULTIPLE ENEMY POSITIONS

### How Black Women Face Compounded Enmity

**Key Works:**
- "Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex" (1989)
- "Mapping the Margins" (1991)
- *On Intersectionality: Essential Writings* (2022)

**Core Thesis:**

Kimberlé Crenshaw (1959-) develops **intersectionality** theory—showing how multiple systems of oppression (race, gender, class) intersect to create unique positions of enmity. Black women face **compounded enmity**: Enemies to white supremacy (as Black), enemies to patriarchy (as women), enemies to capitalism (as workers). Friend-enemy is not singular but **multiply determined**.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Intersectionality**:
   - Oppression not additive (racism + sexism) but **multiplicative** (unique position)
   - Black women's experience ≠ Black men's + white women's
   - **Unique enemy position**: Targeted in ways neither Black men nor white women are

2. **Structural Intersectionality**:
   - Institutions organize enmity through multiple vectors
   - Example: Police violence toward Black women (gendered AND racialized)
   - **Multiple enemy designations** operating simultaneously

3. **Political Intersectionality**:
   - Political movements organized around single axis (race OR gender)
   - Black women marginalized in both (too female for Black movements, too Black for feminist)
   - **Enemies to single-axis movements** (complicate the narrative)

4. **Representational Intersectionality**:
   - Cultural images of Black women (controlling images)
   - Mammy, Jezebel, Sapphire, Welfare Queen
   - **Enemy stereotypes multiply** at intersections

5. **Matrix of Domination** (Patricia Hill Collins):
   - Multiple systems of power intersecting
   - Race, gender, class, sexuality, ability, nationality
   - **Friend-enemy multidimensional** (not single axis)

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Compounded Enmity**:
   - Black women are enemies in multiple systems:
     - White supremacy (Black)
     - Patriarchy (women)
     - Capitalism (workers, unemployed)
   - **Intersectional enemy position**: More vulnerable than those with single marker

2. **Invisible at Intersections**:
   - Antiracist movements focus on Black men (ignoring gender)
   - Feminist movements focus on white women (ignoring race)
   - **Neither friend nor enemy** (just invisible)
   - Intersectional subjects fall through cracks

3. **Multiply Targeted**:
   - Police violence, sexual violence, economic exploitation all at once
   - Cannot separate oppressions (experienced simultaneously)
   - **Friend-enemy from multiple directions**

4. **Coalition Complications**:
   - Who are friends? (Black men? White women? Both marginalize Black women)
   - Who are enemies? (White men? But Black men can be patriarchal too)
   - **Friend-enemy multiply determined** (not simple)

5. **Structural Position Creates Enmity**:
   - Not individual traits but **position in multiple hierarchies**
   - Poor Black women most vulnerable (most intersectional oppression)
   - Rich white men least vulnerable (most intersectional privilege)
   - **Friend-enemy through intersectional position**

**Examples:**

**Police Violence:**
- Black women killed by police (Sandra Bland, Breonna Taylor)
- Invisible in "police brutality" discourse (focuses on Black men)
- Gendered violence (sexual assault by police)
- Racialized violence (Black women targets)
- Friend-enemy: Police vs. Black women (intersectionally targeted)

**Reproductive Justice:**
- Black women forcibly sterilized (eugenics)
- Poor women denied abortion access
- Intersection of race, gender, class
- Friend-enemy: State control over Black women's reproduction

**Mass Incarceration:**
- Black women fastest-growing prison population
- Criminalized for poverty (shoplifting), drugs, sex work
- Mothers separated from children
- Friend-enemy: Intersectional (Black + woman + poor = most vulnerable)

**Welfare Politics:**
- "Welfare Queen" stereotype targets Black women specifically
- Intersection: Black + woman + poor = ultimate enemy of "deserving citizens"
- Friend-enemy: "Taxpayers" vs. Black welfare mothers

**Crenshaw vs. Single-Axis Approaches:**

- **Antiracism (focusing on Black men)**: Ignores gender-specific oppression of Black women
- **Feminism (focusing on white women)**: Ignores racial oppression of Black women
- **Crenshaw**: Must analyze intersections (new friend-enemy positions emerge)

**Crenshaw vs. Class Reductionism:**

- **Class reductionism**: Only class matters (unity of workers)
- **Crenshaw**: Race, gender also structure oppression
- **Class reductionism**: Black and white workers are friends (class unity)
- **Crenshaw**: White workers benefit from racism (complicit in Black women's oppression)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Crenshaw reveals **intersectional friend-enemy**—oppressions multiply at intersections, creating unique enemy positions. Black women face compounded enmity (racism + sexism + classism) in ways neither Black men nor white women face. Friend-enemy is **multidimensional**: Cannot reduce to single axis (race OR gender OR class). Those at multiple intersections of oppression are enemies in multiple systems simultaneously—most vulnerable to violence, exploitation, marginalization.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **intersectional framework** for friend-enemy. Explains why single-axis analyses fail (miss compounded enmity), why Black women face unique violence (intersectional enemy position), why coalitions difficult (friend in one dimension may be enemy in another). Shows friend-enemy as **matrix** (multiple dimensions intersecting). Essential for understanding any situation involving multiple forms of oppression.

## ANGELA Y. DAVIS: PRISON ABOLITION AND THE CARCERAL ENEMY

### The Prison-Industrial Complex as Friend-Enemy Machine

**Key Works:**
- *Are Prisons Obsolete?* (2003)
- *Women, Race & Class* (1981)
- *Abolition Democracy* (2005)
- *Freedom Is a Constant Struggle* (2016)

**Core Thesis:**

Angela Davis (1944-) analyzes the **prison-industrial complex** as a system for creating and managing enemies—primarily Black, brown, and poor people designated as "criminals." Prisons don't rehabilitate but warehouse permanent enemies of the social order. Abolition requires recognizing prisons as enemy-production machines and transforming social relations entirely.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Prison-Industrial Complex**:
   - Prisons as industry (profit from incarceration)
   - Creates permanent enemy class ("criminals")
   - Intersectional: Race, class, gender determine who is imprisoned
   - **Friend-enemy through criminalization**

2. **The Criminal as Permanent Enemy**:
   - "Felon" status permanent (even after release)
   - Civil death (lose voting, housing, employment rights)
   - Can never fully rejoin society (friends)
   - **Once enemy, always enemy**

3. **Abolition not Reform**:
   - Prisons cannot be reformed (designed to create enemies)
   - Must abolish and create alternative responses to harm
   - Abolition democracy: Transform conditions producing "crime"
   - **Refuse friend-enemy framework** (reject "criminal" category entirely)

4. **Racial Capitalism**:
   - Prison continues slavery/racial subjugation
   - 13th Amendment: Slavery permitted for "criminals"
   - Black people disproportionately criminalized
   - **Racial friend-enemy through criminal law**

5. **Alternatives to Carceral Logic**:
   - Restorative justice, transformative justice
   - Address harms without creating enemies
   - **Beyond friend-enemy**: Neither friends nor enemies, just people in conflict

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Criminal as Permanent Enemy**:
   - Designated "criminal" = permanent enemy status
   - Cannot escape (background checks, registries, parole)
   - **Friend-enemy for life** (no redemption)

2. **Selective Criminalization**:
   - Same acts: Some criminalized (Black drug user), some not (white drug user)
   - **Friend-enemy through selective enforcement**
   - Who is enemy determined by race/class, not behavior

3. **Profit from Enemies**:
   - Private prisons profit from mass incarceration
   - Prison labor (modern slavery)
   - **Economic interest in creating enemies**
   - More "criminals" = more profit

4. **Fear as Justification**:
   - "Tough on crime" politics requires dangerous enemies
   - Media amplifies fear (Black men as predators)
   - **Enemy needed to justify carceral expansion**
   - Scarier the enemy, more prisons built

5. **Abolition as Refusing Enemy Designation**:
   - Don't accept "criminal" as permanent status
   - Address harm without creating enemies
   - **Transform friend-enemy** into processes of accountability and healing

**Examples:**

**Mass Incarceration:**
- 2.3 million incarcerated (US)
- Disproportionately Black/Latino (enemy = racialized)
- Profit: Private prisons, prison labor, phone companies
- Friend-enemy: "Law-abiding citizens" vs. "criminals"

**War on Drugs:**
- Black people imprisoned at 6x rate of whites for drugs
- Despite similar usage rates
- Enemy: Black drug user (imprisoned), Friend: White drug user (treatment)
- Friend-enemy: Racialized through "crime"

**Felon Disenfranchisement:**
- 5.2 million cannot vote (felony convictions)
- Disproportionately Black
- Civil death: Not full citizens
- Friend-enemy: Citizens (vote) vs. felons (excluded)

**Three Strikes Laws:**
- Life imprisonment for third felony (even non-violent)
- Creates permanent enemies (never released)
- Disproportionately affects Black men
- Friend-enemy: "Dangerous criminals" locked away forever

**Davis vs. Foucault:**

- **Foucault**: Prisons discipline (create docile bodies)
- **Davis**: Prisons warehouse (create permanent enemies)
- **Foucault**: Panopticon (reform inmates)
- **Davis**: Prisons don't reform, just punish

**Davis vs. Reformists:**

- **Reformists**: Better prisons, less harsh
- **Davis**: Abolish prisons entirely
- **Reformists**: Fix friend-enemy distinction
- **Davis**: Reject friend-enemy framework (no "criminals")

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Davis reveals **prisons as enemy-production machines**—creating permanent "criminal" class through racialized criminalization. Once designated enemy (criminal), status is permanent: Cannot vote, get housing, employment. Prison-industrial complex profits from enemies (more inmates = more money). Abolition means refusing to designate people as permanent enemies—transforming how we respond to harm without creating disposable enemy class.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **prison abolitionist** framework for friend-enemy. Explains mass incarceration (racial enemy-creation), felon disenfranchisement (permanent enemy status), prison-industrial complex (profiting from enemies), and why reform fails (system designed to create enemies). Shows criminal justice as **racialized friend-enemy designation**. Essential for understanding carceral state, mass incarceration, and abolition movements.

- *Black Marxism: The Making of the Black Radical Tradition* (1983)

Cedric Robinson (1940-2016) argues that **capitalism has always been racial**—it didn't "become" racist, but emerged through racialization. Friend-enemy under capitalism is always already racialized. **Key Concepts:**

1. **Racial Capitalism**:
   - Capitalism didn't adopt racism; it **emerged through racialization**

   - Colonialism, slavery foundational (not aberrations)
   - **Friend-enemy is racial from capitalism's origin**

2. **Black Radical Tradition**:
   - Black resistance to racial capitalism (not just "class" struggle)
   - Different epistemology (not Marxist determinism)
   - Maroonage, Quilombos (escape not revolution)
   - **Friend-enemy: Refuse capitalist terms entirely**

3. **Maroonage**:
   - Enslaved Africans escaped to create autonomous communities
   - Not seizing state power but **exiting the system**
   - Alternative to friend-enemy politics (neither join nor fight, just leave)
   - **Maroon friend-enemy**: Build alternatives elsewhere

4. **European Racialization**:
   - Before colonialism: Europeans racialized "inferior" Europeans (Irish, Slavs, Jews)
   - Capitalism required creating racialized enemies (to dispossess)
   - **Friend-enemy through race**: Created to enable accumulation

5. **Rejection of Historical Materialism**:
   - Marx assumes European universality
   - Black Radical Tradition has different logics
   - Not "false consciousness" but **different consciousness** (not waiting for revolution)

   - Friend-enemy under capitalism always racialized

2. **Class Unity Impossible**:
   - White workers have material interest in racism
   - "Workers of the world unite" ignores racial divisions
   - **Friend-enemy within working class** (white vs. Black workers)

3. **Maroon Alternative**:
   - Don't fight for state power or capitalism
   - **Exodus**: Create autonomous zones
   - Quilombos, maroon communities, autonomous spaces
   - Friend-enemy: Exit the framework (neither friend nor enemy to capital)

4. **Black Internationalism**:
   - All Black people face racial capitalism globally
   - Pan-African solidarity (all friends against racial capitalism)
   - Enemy: Global racial capitalism (not just "the bourgeoisie")
   - **Racial friend-enemy transcends nations**

5. **Different Freedom**:
   - Not "workers seize means of production" (Marxist)
   - But create autonomous communities, preserve culture
   - **Friend-enemy redefined**: Freedom as autonomy, not revolution

- Neither joined capitalist society nor fought it directly
- Friend-enemy: Exited the framework

- AFL excluded Black workers
- White unions struck against Black workers being hired
- **Friend-enemy within working class**: White workers vs. Black workers
- Racial capitalism divides potential allies

**Black Lives Matter:**
- Black Radical Tradition continuation
- Not just "reform police" but challenge racial capitalism
- Intersectional (race, gender, class, abolition)
- Friend-enemy: Racial capitalism vs. Black freedom

- **Robinson**: Capitalism creates racial enemies (white vs. Black from origin)

- **Robinson**: Race divides working class (white workers are enemies to Black workers)

Black Radical Tradition offers **maroonage** (exit) rather than revolution (seize power). Friend-enemy: Racial capitalism vs. Black freedom, but freedom defined differently (autonomy, culture, community, not just economic redistribution).

**Critical Addition**: Provides **racial capitalism framework**. Explains why racism persists under capitalism (structural, not ideological), why working-class unity fails (white workers benefit), why Black struggles different (not just class). Shows friend-enemy as **racial from capitalism's origin**—not aberration but foundation. Essential for understanding racial dynamics, labor movement failures, and Black liberation movements.

## RUTH WILSON GILMORE: CARCERAL GEOGRAPHY AND ORGANIZED ABANDONMENT

### The State Produces Enemies Through Spatial Isolation

**Key Works:**
- *Golden Gulag: Prisons, Surplus, Crisis, and Opposition in Globalizing California* (2007)
- *Abolition Geography: Essays Towards Liberation* (2022)

**Core Thesis:**

Ruth Wilson Gilmore (1950-) analyzes the **carceral state** as organizing enmity through geography—building prisons in rural areas to warehouse urban (Black, brown) "enemies," creating what she calls **organized abandonment**. The state produces surplus populations (enemies), isolates them spatially, and profits from their imprisonment. Friend-enemy operates through geographic isolation.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Organized Abandonment**:
   - State abandons certain populations (defunds schools, health care, jobs)
   - Creates "surplus" populations (unemployable, desperate)
   - Then criminalizes these populations
   - **Abandonment creates enemies**, then manages them through prisons

2. **Carceral Geography**:
   - Prisons built in rural areas (white, economically depressed)
   - Inmates from urban areas (Black, brown, poor)
   - **Spatial friend-enemy**: Rural profits from urban punishment
   - Distance isolates enemies from communities

3. **Racism as State-Sanctioned Vulnerability**:
   - Racism = exposing some to premature death
   - Through: Pollution, police, poverty, prison, disease
   - **Friend-enemy through differential life expectancy**
   - Black people die younger (enemy status = death sentence)

4. **Prison Expansion as Economic Development**:
   - Rural towns compete for prisons (jobs, revenue)
   - Profit from housing urban "enemies"
   - **Economic friend-enemy**: Rural employment from urban incarceration

5. **Abolition Requires Geographic Justice**:
   - Can't just abolish prisons without transforming geographies
   - Must provide resources where abandonment occurred
   - **Transform spaces creating enemies**

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Geographic Production of Enemies**:
   - State creates spaces of abandonment (deindustrialization, defunding)
   - People in these spaces become surplus (enemies)
   - Then spatially isolated (prisons far from cities)
   - **Friend-enemy through spatial management**

2. **Rural/Urban Friend-Enemy**:
   - Rural (white) areas profit from incarcerating urban (Black) people
   - Economic interest in maintaining enemy class
   - **Geographic friend-enemy**: Rural guards vs. urban inmates

3. **Premature Death**:
   - Friend-enemy through life expectancy:
     - Friends: Protected, healthy, long-lived
     - Enemies: Exposed, sick, die young
   - **Biological friend-enemy outcome**

4. **Vulnerability as Enmity**:
   - State makes some populations vulnerable (to pollution, police, poverty)
   - Vulnerability = enemy status (disposable)
   - **Friend-enemy through differential protection**

5. **Abolition Geography**:
   - Transform abandoned spaces
   - Provide resources (health, education, employment)
   - **End abandonment = end enemy production**

**Examples:**

**California Prison Expansion:**
- 33 prisons built (1980-2000) in rural areas
- Inmates from Los Angeles, Oakland (urban)
- Rural towns economically depend on prisons
- Friend-enemy: Rural employment from urban punishment

**Flint Water Crisis:**
- Organized abandonment: Black city defunded, poisoned
- Created health crisis, premature deaths
- State response: Neglect (enemies left to die)
- Friend-enemy: White suburbs (clean water) vs. Black Flint (poisoned)

**Cancer Alley (Louisiana):**
- Petrochemical plants concentrated in Black communities
- Extreme pollution, cancer rates
- State allows this (organized abandonment)
- Friend-enemy: White areas protected vs. Black areas sacrificed

**COVID-19 Disparity:**
- Black, Latino, indigenous died at 2-3x rates
- Organized abandonment: Frontline workers, crowded housing, poor healthcare
- Friend-enemy: Protected (white, wealthy) vs. exposed (Black, poor)

**Gilmore vs. Davis:**

- **Davis**: Prison abolition (end carceral logic)
- **Gilmore**: Abolition geography (transform spaces)
- **Davis**: Focus on ideology/criminalization
- **Gilmore**: Focus on geography/abandonment
- Both: Prisons produce enemies

**Gilmore vs. Environmental Justice:**

- **EJ**: Environmental racism = pollution in Black areas
- **Gilmore**: Part of organized abandonment (produce surplus/enemies)
- Both: Geographic friend-enemy (protection vs. exposure)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Gilmore reveals **geographic production of enemies**—state creates abandoned spaces through disinvestment, populations in these spaces become surplus/enemies, then spatially isolated in prisons. Friend-enemy operates through geography: Protected spaces (friends) vs. abandoned spaces (enemies), rural profit from urban punishment, differential exposure to pollution/police/premature death. Abolition requires **transforming the geographies** that produce enemies, not just closing prisons.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **geographic/spatial analysis** of friend-enemy. Explains mass incarceration (geographic isolation of enemies), environmental racism (spatial abandonment), premature death gaps (differential vulnerability), and prison-town economics (profit from enemies). Shows friend-enemy as **spatially organized**—through abandonment of certain areas and populations. Essential for understanding carceral geography and abolitionist movements.

**Core Thesis:**

Sara Ahmed (1969-) analyzes how **emotions circulate** to create friend-enemy distinctions. Hate, fear, and disgust don't originate in individuals but circulate through society, attaching to certain bodies and making them enemies. Friend-enemy is maintained through **affective economies**—systems of emotional investment and circulation.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Affective Economies**:
   - Emotions circulate like capital (flow, accumulate, generate value)
   - Hate circulates: Attached to certain bodies (immigrants, Muslims, queers)
   - **Emotional friend-enemy**: Who is hated vs. who is loved

2. **Stranger Danger**:
   - Strangers (foreigners, Others) represented as dangerous
   - Fear circulates, attaches to racialized/gendered bodies
   - Creates enemies through affect (not rational assessment)
   - **Friend-enemy through fear**

3. **The Complaint**:
   - Complaining about injustice makes you "killjoy" (enemy)
   - Institutions protect themselves by marking complainants as problems
   - **Friend-enemy through complaint**: Silent friends vs. troublemaking enemies

4. **Stickiness**:
   - Certain signs "stick" to certain bodies
   - "Terrorist" sticks to Muslim/brown bodies
   - "Criminal" sticks to Black bodies
   - **Friend-enemy through semiotic stickiness**

5. **Happy Objects**:
   - Some objects/identities designated "happy" (friends)
   - Others designated sources of unhappiness (enemies)
   - **Affective friend-enemy**: Who/what makes "us" happy vs. unhappy

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Emotional Circulation Creates Enemies**:
   - Fear doesn't originate in individual encounter
   - Circulates through media, discourse, culture
   - Attaches to certain bodies (makes them enemies)
   - **Friend-enemy through affect**, not reason

2. **The Killjoy**:
   - Those who point out injustice designated killjoys
   - Enemies of institutional happiness
   - **Friend-enemy through complaint**: Those who complain become enemies

3. **Sticky Signs**:
   - "Terrorist," "immigrant," "criminal" stick to racialized bodies
   - Once attached, hard to remove
   - **Friend-enemy through semiotics**: Who signs stick to

4. **Love and Hate**:
   - National love (patriotism) requires national enemies
   - "We" love our nation because we hate its enemies
   - **Friend-enemy through affective attachment**

5. **Bodies That Matter**:
   - Some bodies register as human (friends)
   - Others register as threat (enemies)
   - **Friend-enemy through recognition** (who is seen as human)

**Examples:**

**Post-9/11 Islamophobia:**
- Fear circulated, attached to Muslim/brown bodies
- Any Muslim/brown person potentially terrorist
- Hate crimes, discrimination, surveillance
- Friend-enemy: White Americans (safe) vs. Muslims/brown people (dangerous)

**Anti-Immigrant Rhetoric:**
- "Invasion," "infestation" (affective language)
- Fear circulates about migrants
- Attaches to brown bodies specifically
- Friend-enemy: Through fear and disgust (affective economy)

**The Feminist Killjoy:**
- Women who point out sexism = killjoys
- Enemies of institutional happiness
- Marked as problems (not the sexism they identify)
- Friend-enemy: Compliant women (friends) vs. feminist killjoys (enemies)

**Institutional Complaints:**
- Those who complain about racism/sexism marked as troublemakers
- Institutions protect themselves by making complainant the problem
- Friend-enemy: Institution (friend) vs. complainer (enemy)

**Ahmed vs. Schmitt:**

- **Schmitt**: Friend-enemy is political decision (rational)
- **Ahmed**: Friend-enemy is affective circulation (emotional)
- **Schmitt**: Sovereign decides
- **Ahmed**: Emotions circulate, attach to bodies (no single decider)

**Ahmed vs. Butler:**

- **Butler**: Grievability determines whose lives matter
- **Ahmed**: Emotions (fear, hate) determine who is threat
- Both: Affective dimensions of friend-enemy
- **Ahmed**: Focus on circulation (how emotions move)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Ahmed reveals **affective economies** creating friend-enemy—emotions (fear, hate, disgust) circulate through society and attach to certain bodies, designating them as enemies. Not rational threat assessment but **emotional circulation**. Strangers become dangerous, Muslims become terrorists, Black people become criminals through circulation of fear. Also: Complainants become killjoys (enemies) for pointing out injustice. Friend-enemy is affectively maintained through emotional investments.

**Critical Addition**: Provides **affective/emotional analysis** of friend-enemy. Explains Islamophobia (fear circulated post-9/11), anti-immigrant rhetoric ("invasion" language), feminist killjoy (complaining = enemy), and how institutions protect themselves (mark complainants as enemies). Shows friend-enemy as **emotionally circulated**—through fear, hate, disgust attaching to bodies. Essential for understanding affective dimensions of enmity.

## FINAL MAJOR THEORIST - JODI DEAN: COMMUNICATIVE CAPITALISM AND THE COMRADE

### The Friend Returns - Against Networked Enmity

**Key Works:**
- *Democracy and Other Neoliberal Fantasies* (2009)
- *The Communist Horizon* (2012)
- *Crowds and Party* (2016)
- *Comrade: An Essay on Political Belonging* (2019)

**Core Thesis:**

Jodi Dean (1962-) argues that **communicative capitalism** (networked media, platforms) creates appearance of democratic participation while maintaining elite power. Against networked enmity and isolated achievement subjects (Han), Dean proposes **the comrade**—a figure of political friendship that creates collective power against capitalist enemies.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Communicative Capitalism**:
   - Endless communication (social media, platforms) replaces political action
   - Creates illusion of participation (posting ≠ power)
   - **Networked enmity**: Everyone fighting everyone online
   - No collective friend-enemy (only individual grievances)

2. **The Comrade**:
   - Political friend based on **shared struggle** (not identity)
   - Horizontal equality among comrades
   - Against common enemy (capital, State)
   - **Friend through struggle** (not liberal tolerance)

3. **The Crowd**:
   - Collective subject that exceeds individuals
   - Physical assembly (not virtual)
   - Creates power through numbers
   - **Friends united in crowd** against enemy

4. **The Party**:
   - Organization that maintains revolutionary friend-enemy over time
   - Not electoral party but vanguard organization
   - **Institutionalized friendship** (comradeship) for long struggle
   - Comrades vs. enemies (class war)

5. **Against Horizontal**:
   - Horizontalism (no leadership, consensus) fails
   - Requires discipline, leadership, organization
   - **Clear friend-enemy** (party knows its enemies)

**Friend-Enemy Implications:**

1. **Communicative Capitalism Fragments**:
   - Social media creates appearance of connection
   - Actually: Isolated individuals (no collective friends)
   - **Networked enmity**: Everyone vs. everyone
   - No organized friend-enemy (just chaos)

2. **Comrade as True Friend**:
   - Not liberal friend (tolerance, respect)
   - Not identity friend (same race/gender/nation)
   - But **political friend** (shared struggle)
   - Comrades vs. capitalist enemies

3. **The Crowd Unifies**:
   - Physical assembly creates collective identity
   - Against enemy (police, State, capital)
   - **Friend-enemy through crowd** (not individual)
   - Tahrir Square, Occupy, protests

4. **The Party Maintains**:
   - Crowd dissipates; party persists
   - Organizes long-term struggle against enemies
   - **Friend-enemy institutionalized**
   - Bolsheviks, Black Panthers

5. **Discipline and Clarity**:
   - Know who your comrades are (party members)
   - Know who enemies are (capitalists, State, sellouts)
   - **Clear friend-enemy** (no ambiguity)

**Examples:**

**Social Media:**
- Appearance of community (friends, followers)
- Actually: Isolated users performing for algorithms
- No collective power (just individual "takes")
- Friend-enemy: Fragmented (everyone vs. everyone)

**Occupy Wall Street:**
- Crowd assembled (created temporary friendship)
- Against common enemy ("the 1%")
- But: No party, dissipated
- Friend-enemy: Clear (99% vs. 1%) but unsustainable without organization

**Black Lives Matter:**
- Crowds in streets (collective friends)
- Against common enemy (police, racial capitalism)
- But: Horizontalist (no central organization)
- Friend-enemy: Strong in moments, weak institutionally

**Bolsheviks:**
- Party organization (comrades)
- Clear enemies (Tsar, bourgeoisie, Mensheviks, kulaks)
- Maintained struggle over time
- Friend-enemy: Institutionalized (party discipline)

**Dean vs. Han:**

- **Han**: Neoliberalism eliminates external enemy (self-exploitation)
- **Dean**: Capitalism is enemy (just obscured by communicative capitalism)
- **Han**: No friends (only isolated achievement subjects)
- **Dean**: Comradeship possible (political friendship)

**Dean vs. Mouffe:**

- **Mouffe**: Agonistic democracy (adversaries within liberal framework)
- **Dean**: Communist party (enemies to overthrow, not debate with)
- **Mouffe**: Transform enemies to adversaries
- **Dean**: Maintain clear enemies (capitalists)

**Key Insight for Comprehensive Analysis:**

Dean reveals **communicative capitalism's fragmentation** of collective friend-enemy into individual networked enmity. Social media creates appearance of connection while isolating users. Against this, Dean proposes **the comrade**—political friendship based on shared struggle against common enemy (capital). The crowd creates temporary collective power, the party maintains it. Friend-enemy: Comrades (organized friends) vs. capitalist enemies, but requires discipline and organization (not horizontal diffusion).

**Critical Addition**: Provides **communist/party** perspective on friend-enemy. Explains why social media doesn't create real solidarity (communicative capitalism), why crowds dissipate (need party), why horizontalism fails (requires organization), and what true political friendship is (comradeship). Shows friend-enemy requires **clear organization** and **maintained struggle** (not just moments of unity). Essential for understanding contemporary protest movements and organizational debates.
