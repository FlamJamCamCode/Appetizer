### Chapter 20: Why "Good Enough" Approximations Fail

**The temptation**: "Sure, 1D loses information, but maybe it's *good enough* approximation?"

**Why this fails**:

**Good approximation** requires: Captured dimensions dominate lost dimensions.

**Example—Height approximation**:
- Person's volume ≈ height × width × depth (3D)
- Approximate by height alone (1D): volume ≈ k × height
- Works okay if: width and depth roughly proportional to height (for adult humans, often true)
- **Why it works**: Lost dimensions (width, depth) correlated with kept dimension (height)

**But for nebulae** (infinite-dimensional semantic concepts):
- No guarantee that 1 dimension dominates ∞-1 dimensions
- No guarantee that lost dimensions correlate with kept dimension
- Context determines which dimensions matter (changes constantly)

**Trust score says**: 0.8 (trustworthy).

**Reality**: 0.2 in the dimension that matters (not trustworthy in this context).

**Result**: Trust score catastrophically wrong because it averaged away the critical dimension.

1D reduction **averages** over all dimensions (or picks arbitrary subset).
