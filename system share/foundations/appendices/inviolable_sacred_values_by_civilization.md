# Inviolable Sacred Values: What Civilizations Criminalize as Outlaw Thinking

## Definition by Punishment

**Core principle:** A civilization's "sacred values" are defined by what they criminalize. What cannot be questioned, criticized, or diverged from without facing state punishment reveals the actual foundational beliefs that societies enforce through law.

**Methodology:** This document identifies "inviolable sacred values" by examining what speech, expression, advocacy, or questioning results in criminal prosecution and imprisonment (or worse) across different countries and civilizations.

---

## I. Western Europe & Liberal Democracies

### Core Sacred Values (Defined by Criminal Punishment)

**1. Racial Equality & Anti-Discrimination**
- **Criminal offenses:** "Hate speech" targeting racial/ethnic groups
- **Punishment:** 3 months to 5 years imprisonment (Germany), up to 2 years (Denmark), varies by country
- **Examples:** Sam Melia (UK) - 2 years for stickers; various prosecutions for "racial hatred"
- **Countries:** Germany, Denmark, UK, France, Sweden, most EU nations
- **Enforcement:** Active prosecution for "stirring up racial hatred"

**2. Holocaust Centrality**
- **Criminal offenses:** Holocaust denial, Holocaust minimization
- **Punishment:** Fines €30,000+, prison time possible
- **Examples:** Jean-Marie Le Pen (France) - fines for calling gas chambers "a detail"
- **Countries:** Germany, France, Austria, Poland, others
- **Enforcement:** Historical revisionism punished as "anti-Semitism"

**3. Sexual Orientation & Gender Identity**
- **Criminal offenses:** Discrimination against LGBTQ+ individuals, "conversion therapy" in some jurisdictions
- **Punishment:** Fines, prison in some cases
- **Examples:** Various prosecutions for anti-LGBTQ+ speech
- **Countries:** UK, Germany, France, Canada, and expanding
- **Enforcement:** Protecting LGBTQ+ values through criminal law

**4. Multicultural Immigration**
- **Criminal offenses:** "Inciting racial hatred" through anti-immigration rhetoric
- **Punishment:** Up to 2 years imprisonment
- **Examples:** Sam Melia's "They have to go back" stickers
- **Countries:** UK, Germany, France, others
- **Enforcement:** Anti-immigration advocacy criminalized

**5. Democratic Institutions (Selective)**
- **Criminal offenses:** Certain forms of government criticism (varies by country)
- **Punishment:** Fines, rarely imprisonment
- **Examples:** Historically "insulting the president" laws
- **Countries:** France (repealed 2015), various European nations
- **Enforcement:** Weaker than other "hate speech" categories

**Key characteristics:**
- Group-based protections (can't target marginalized groups)
- Historical narrative protections (certain events can't be questioned)
- Anti-discrimination enforcement through speech crimes
- Less protection for criticizing institutions/power

## II. United States

### Core Sacred Values (Defined by Criminal Punishment)

**Challenge:** U.S. has strongest speech protections, fewer criminal speech laws

**1. Threats & Incitement to Violence**
- **Criminal offenses:** True threats, incitement to imminent violence
- **Punishment:** Prison time varies
- **Enforcement:** Rare, requires clear and present danger

**2. Certain Forms of Discrimination**
- **Criminal offenses:** Civil rights violations, hate crimes when tied to violence
- **Punishment:** Enhanced penalties
- **Enforcement:** Requires underlying violent crime

**Key characteristics:**
- **Weak criminal speech enforcement** (relative to Europe)
- Strong procedural protections
- Criminal punishment rare for pure speech
- But: Social/professional consequences for wrongthink severe

**Important distinction:**
- U.S. doesn't criminalize speech as aggressively as Europe
- But creates similar chilling effects through:
  - Political prosecutions (see McMasters case, Arbery prosecutions)
  - Social ostracism and doxxing
  - Employment consequences
  - Media narrative control

**Conclusion:** U.S. sacred values less enforced through criminal law, more through extra-legal social/economic punishment.

## XVII. De Facto vs. De Jure: Shadow Punishment Beyond Criminal Law

### The Two-Tiered Enforcement System

**Core insight:** Sacred values are enforced through TWO mechanisms:
1. **De jure:** Criminal prosecution and state punishment
2. **De facto:** Social ostracism, financial exclusion, platform bans, employment termination

**The West's primary enforcement mechanism:** De facto punishment (extra-legal consequences) rather than de jure (criminal prosecution).

---

### I. Financial Exclusion and De-Banking

**The phenomena:**
- Payment processors (Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, Stripe) terminating accounts
- Banks closing accounts of controversial figures
- Financial infrastructure withdrawal from "wrongthink" individuals/organizations

**Examples:**

**Sam Melia's Wife (UK):**
- Bank account closed due to husband's political prosecution
- Financial punishment extends beyond convicted party
- Family members punished by association

**Far-Right/Nationalist Figures:**
- Multiple accounts of payment processors refusing service
- "Nazi" or "far-right" labels trigger financial exclusion
- Banks closing accounts without clear legal justification

**Crowdfunding Platforms:**
- GoFundMe banning campaigns for "wrongthink" political figures
- Financial support mechanisms cut off
- Indirect punishment through financial infrastructure

**Implications:**
- Financial exclusion more effective than criminal prosecution
- No due process for account termination
- Private companies serving as de facto law enforcement
- Creates chilling effects beyond legal penalties

**Countries:** Primarily Western (U.S., UK, EU) where private financial institutions participate in de facto enforcement

---

### II. Platform Bans and Shadow-Banning

**Social Media Platforms:**
- Twitter/X (formerly), Facebook, Instagram, YouTube
- Shadow-banning (reducing visibility without notification)
- Account suspensions and permanent bans
- Algorithmic demotion of disfavored content

**Examples:**
- "Far-right" figures banned from multiple platforms simultaneously
- Cryptocurrency platforms banning individuals
- "Conspiracy theorist" labels triggering coordinated bans
- Political censorship disguised as "community standards"

**Pattern:**
- Major platforms coordinating enforcement
- Similar "policy violations" across platforms
- Political alignment determines ban probability
- No transparent appeal processes

**Video Platforms:**
- YouTube demonetizing channels
- Striking channels that violate "community guidelines"
- Algorithmic suppression without ban

**Countries:** Global platforms, but enforcement primarily targets Western dissidents

---

### III. Employment and Professional Consequences

**Job Termination:**
- Employees fired for controversial social media posts

- Professional blacklisting

**Examples:**
- University professors terminated for "wrongthink"
- Journalists fired for controversial opinions
- Corporate employees dismissed for political speech

**Professional Exclusion:**
- Exclusion from industry events
- Loss of speaking opportunities
- Professional networks cutting ties

**Doxxing:**
- Public identification leading to harassment
- Employers pressured to fire controversial figures
- Personal information published to enable harassment

**Countries:** Primarily Western (U.S., UK, EU) where employment "at will" and social pressure create consequences

### VI. Why De Facto Punishment Matters

**1. More effective than criminal prosecution:**
- No due process requirements
- No right to defend oneself
- No appeals process
- Permanent consequences

**2. Creates chilling effects:**
- People self-censor to avoid consequences
- More effective than explicit legal bans
- Generates compliance without state prosecution

**3. Plausible deniability:**
- "Private companies making their own decisions"
- State doesn't take responsibility
- Avoids constitutional challenges
- Maintains facade of "free speech"

**4. Escalation potential:**
- First warnings (platform bans)
- Then financial exclusion
- Then employment consequences
- Then social ostracism
- Finally criminal prosecution (if necessary)

**5. Coordination creates de facto state action:**
- Multiple private actors coordinating
- Pattern suggests state involvement or at least approval
- Private actors serving as state enforcement mechanism
